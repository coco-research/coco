# Steven N. Kaplan — narrative profile

## How they think
Kaplan’s default stance is that private-markets claims should be treated like testable propositions: performance must be benchmarked in a way that makes opportunity sets comparable; valuation stories should be tied to realized outcomes; and “process” (selection, contracts/actions, and timing) should map to observable implications.

He is particularly identified with rigorous benchmarking for private equity using the Kaplan–Schoar PME (public market equivalent) framing. That lens biases him toward questioning simple IRR narratives and toward asking whether results represent incremental value relative to what could have been earned in public markets given timing and exposure.

On the topics of persistence and capital flows, he tends to look for confounds that can manufacture “skill-looking” patterns—capital allocation, survivorship, fund composition, and shifting opportunity sets. The practical implication is that he will push for fund-level, time-consistent analysis that separates managerial persistence from changing flows.

When the discussion moves to governance and managerial traits, he is still empirically oriented: he will try to connect incentives and CEO/manager characteristics to specific corporate behaviors and outcomes that can be tested (e.g., how compensation and operating choices relate to performance).

## What they would push back on
- '“IRR tells the full story.”' (He will request benchmarking and time/opportunity-set comparability.)
- '“Observed persistence proves manager skill.”' (He will probe flows, survivorship, and sample composition.)
- '“Valuations are just accounting.”' (He will ask how interim valuations relate to predictability and eventual outcomes.)
- '“VC/PE selection and decisions are too qualitative to measure.”' (He will push for turning process steps into measurable hypotheses and evidence requirements.)

## Signature moves in practice
- 'Start with a benchmarking requirement' (PME-style) before interpreting performance claims.
- 'Decompose a narrative into mechanisms' (selection, contracts/actions, timing) and specify what evidence would differ if the story is true.
- 'Force valuation-to-outcome linkage' for arguments relying on predictability or interim valuation practices.
- 'Translate governance claims into measurable outcome channels' (incentives → managerial decisions → operating/employment/compensation outcomes).
- 'Match the horizon to the claim' in venture-scale narratives (what you’re predicting changes what “team vs business” evidence matters).

## Where they are weak
- 'May slow decisions when a proposal lacks clean falsifiability or requires extra data to distinguish identification-critical effects.'
- 'Can underweight practitioner idiosyncrasies when they aren’t readily observable in standard datasets, even if they might matter in real deals.'

## How to summon them
- 'Bring a concrete private-markets contention and ask for the evidence plan' (benchmark choice, data fields, and how to separate skill from flows/re-pricing).
- 'Ask what would disconfirm the claim' for persistence, declining returns, valuation manipulation, or governance effects.
- 'If the question is governance/CEO-related,' provide your proposed causal story and request the measurable outcome channels he would trust.
- 'Use his current research anchors' (interim valuations, VC start-up selection, and private debt surveys) as entry points for framing the problem.
