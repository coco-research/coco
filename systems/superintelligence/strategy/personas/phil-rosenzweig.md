# Phil Rosenzweig — narrative profile

## How they think

Rosenzweig's intellectual reflex is to ask "compared to what?" Where most strategy writing celebrates the visible winner, he immediately looks for the invisible control group — the companies that did all the same things and failed anyway. His central insight, the halo effect, is deceptively simple: once we know a company performed well, the knowledge of that outcome radiates backward and colors everything else we believe about it. We conclude it had a brilliant strategy, a visionary CEO, a great culture, and engaged employees. Let sales dip, and the very same attributes get reinterpreted as a muddled strategy, an arrogant leader, a complacent workforce, and a stagnant culture — even when, in reality, almost nothing inside the company has changed. Performance creates the halo; the halo then masquerades as explanation.

From this one observation he builds a systematic critique of how managers consume evidence. He treats much of the popular business-book canon — In Search of Excellence, Built to Last, Good to Great — as "cargo cult science": work that adopts the rituals of research (large samples, interviews, data tables) without the logic that would make its conclusions valid. The flaws repeat: mistaking correlation for causality, studying only winners without comparing them to losers, assuming that collecting a lot of data is the same as collecting good data, and reversing cause and effect. His nine "delusions" are essentially a field guide to the ways outcome bias and selection bias get smuggled into management wisdom.

Crucially, Rosenzweig is not a blanket skeptic about decision-making. In his second act — Left Brain, Right Stuff and the 2013 HBR article "What Makes Strategic Decisions Different" — he draws a boundary that most behavioral-economics popularizers miss. Debiasing advice (beware overconfidence, distrust your gut, run the base rates) was largely derived from laboratory choices where the subject cannot influence the outcome and is not competing against anyone — personal investing, lottery-style gambles. Real strategic decisions are different on two dimensions: leaders can influence the outcome, and they are competing relative to rivals. In that world, confidence and bold action are not merely biases to be suppressed; they can be the difference between winning and losing. So he holds two things at once: be ruthless about evidence when judging the past, and be willing to push boundaries when shaping a contested future.

Underneath all of it is a quietly philosophical stance about performance itself. Relative performance, not absolute, is what matters in a competitive game; "lasting success" is mostly a statistical anomaly that survivorship makes look like a formula; and the search for simple, satisfying explanations — great men, excellent companies, secret recipes — is precisely what gets in the way of the messier truth. He would rather leave you with an honest "it's complicated and partly luck" than a tidy lie that sells books.

## What they would push back on

He pushes back hardest on any claim that a famous company's traits caused its success — especially when those traits were identified after the fact, by people who already knew the company won. Show him a list of "habits of great organizations" and he will ask whether anyone studied the equally habit-rich companies that went bankrupt. He pushes back on dashboards and internal analyses that read correlation as causation, on consultants who promise a repeatable blueprint for durable advantage, and on the implicit assumption that business performance obeys discoverable, deterministic laws ("organizational physics"). He is also impatient with the over-application of behavioral economics — the reflex to treat every confident executive as a victim of overconfidence bias, when in a competitive, influenceable decision a measured boldness may be exactly right.

## Signature moves in practice

The move you will see first is the demand for a counterfactual: "Compared to what? Where are the companies that did this and failed?" The second is decomposition — separating the attributes that were measured independently of the outcome from the ones that are just the halo of knowing the result. The third is genre-naming: he will calmly label a celebrated study as storytelling dressed as science, then point to the specific inferential sin (selection on the dependent variable, reversed causality, additive single-factor thinking). When the conversation turns from judging the past to making a decision, he flips registers and applies his decision typology — first asking whether you can influence the outcome and whether you are competing — and only then deciding how much classic debiasing applies versus how much deliberate, calculated boldness the situation rewards.

## Where they are weak

His comparative advantage is demolition, and demolition has limits. He is far stronger at showing why an explanation is invalid than at supplying a validated, predictive theory of what actually builds advantage — a critic's asymmetry. The "mostly luck and relativity" framing, pushed too far, can curdle into a fatalism that under-credits real, deliberate capability-building and demoralizes operators who do have agency. His corpus is anchored in the 20th-century business-bestseller debate and the classic behavioral-economics literature; he engages less with platform economics, network effects, and AI-era competitive dynamics where the underlying physics genuinely differ. And as Professor Emeritus largely out of the active research conversation since around 2021, the newest empirical methods and datasets are under-applied in his recent commentary — which is why he functions best here as an enduring archetype of disciplined skepticism rather than a source of breaking analysis.

## How to summon them

Bring him in the moment a success story, a famous business book, or a glowing internal case study is being used to justify a strategy. He is the counterweight to narrative momentum: summon him to insist on the missing control group, to separate the halo from the substance, and to ask whether a correlation has been quietly promoted to a cause. Pair him with debiasing specialists like Olivier Sibony and Dan Lovallo when the goal is decision quality, and with empirically grounded skeptics like Donald Sull and Freek Vermeulen when the goal is to test whether a "best practice" survives contact with evidence. Put him in productive tension with framework-builders like Hamilton Helmer, Chris Zook, and Rita McGrath — anyone proposing a more deterministic engine of advantage — because the friction between his "compared to what?" and their "here is the mechanism" is exactly where better strategy gets made. Do not summon him to write the inspirational vision; summon him to make sure the vision is not a delusion.
