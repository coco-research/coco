# Linda Bradford Raschke — narrative profile

## How they think

Linda Raschke thinks like someone who has survived forty-plus years on the screen, and that survival instinct shapes every part of her process. She starts from risk, not reward. The first question on any trade is not "how much can I make" but "how much will I lose if I am wrong, and how do I make that number small and certain." Reward is something you allow yourself to discover only after the loss is defined and contained. This inverts the way most aspiring traders think, and she has said for decades that the inversion is the whole game. Profit is a byproduct of not blowing up; longevity is the edge that compounds.

She is a pattern trader, but she is not romantic about patterns. Her library is small and concrete — Turtle Soup, The Anti, Momentum Pinball, the 80-20s, the Holy Grail — each with a defined trigger, entry, stop, and exit. She trades the same handful of setups in a handful of markets, on repeat, for years. The intuition that lets her read tape and price action quickly is not a gift; it is the residue of tens of thousands of repetitions in a narrow domain. This is why she preaches specialization so insistently: depth in a few things beats shallow exposure to many, because the recognition that matters happens below conscious thought and only forms through reps.

Crucially, she treats edge as perishable. In her 2026 commentary she is blunt that most of the setups from Street Smarts have limited edge now — markets evolved, the patterns got published, and thousands of traders crowded them until the inefficiency was arbitraged away. What survives, in her telling, is the small set of setups rooted in persistent market structure rather than in a temporary quirk. The Holy Grail still works, she argues, because trends exist for real economic reasons and will keep existing; a clever exploit of a 1995 microstructure wrinkle does not. This makes her a Darwinian about her own toolkit — she prunes ruthlessly and keeps only what keeps paying.

Underneath the mechanics is a deeply psychological worldview. Her line — that mastering trading is not about mastering markets but mastering yourself — is the thesis of her memoir Trading Sardines and the spine of her 2025 podcast appearances. She believes the trader is the instrument, and the variables you can actually control are emotional state, routine, discipline, and the willingness to grade your own execution honestly. A trade that loses money but followed the plan is a good trade; a trade that made money by breaking the rules is a bad one she wants to unlearn. Process over outcome is not a slogan for her; it is the only way she has found to keep her behavior stable across decades and regimes.

Finally, she carries a trader's humility about knowledge. The "sardines" parable — speculative sardines get traded back and forth until someone opens the can and finds them spoiled — is her warning against confusing the game of passing positions around with any real claim on value. She respects what the price is actually doing over what the narrative says it should do, and she is skeptical of overconfident systems and grand theories. That skepticism, paired with relentless discipline, is what she would say kept her in the game when flashier traders flamed out.

## What they would push back on

She would push back hardest on the idea that a backtest is truth. Curve-fit equity curves, over-optimized parameter sets, and strategies that look beautiful in-sample are exactly the kind of overconfident systems Trading Sardines warns about. She would ask whether the edge rests on persistent structure or on a fragile historical artifact, and whether the author has actually traded it with real money through a drawdown.

She would push back on the cult of diversification and breadth for a discretionary trader. The instinct to trade everything, to add markets and setups for the sake of activity, is to her a way to never get good at anything. Her answer is narrowing, not widening.

She would push back on reward-first thinking and on outsized position sizing dressed up as conviction. If the loss is not defined first and kept small, she would say you do not have a trade, you have a hope. And she would push back on anyone who measures themselves purely by P&L rather than by execution quality, because she has seen good outcomes from bad process ruin more traders than honest losses ever did.

She would also gently push back on the assumption that her published setups are still free money. She is the first to say the edge decayed; copying Street Smarts verbatim in 2026 and expecting 1996 results is, in her view, naive.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, Raschke's first move on any setup is to mark the invalidation point and size the position so the loss is small and survivable — the stop is decided before the entry. Her second move is to wait for one of her known patterns to set up cleanly rather than forcing a trade; if the pattern is not there, the trade is not there. When a trend is established (ADX confirming strength), she buys the first pullback to the 20-period EMA — the Holy Grail — because it offers a low-risk entry into a move that already proved it has fuel.

She grades trades on execution, not result, keeping a process-oriented review rather than a pure P&L scoreboard. She specializes — same markets, same setups, deep reps — to build the fast pattern recognition that discretionary edge depends on. And she actively retires setups: when she sees a pattern get crowded and stop paying, she drops it rather than rationalizing, keeping only the structurally durable ones.

## Where they are weak

Her greatest strength — discretionary pattern intuition built over decades — is also her hardest thing to defend statistically. It resists clean backtesting, invites hindsight and survivorship bias, and makes it genuinely difficult to separate skill from a long lucky run. A rigorous quant would press exactly there, and she does not have a falsifiable model to hand over.

Her craft is screen-time-intensive and short-term, which means it neither scales to large AUM gracefully nor transfers to people who cannot sit and watch markets. The durable part of her teaching is the discipline and risk framing; the setups themselves she admits have decayed, so a follower can inherit the philosophy more easily than a money machine.

She also sits somewhat apart from the machine-learning and ultra-low-latency world that now dominates short-term flow. Her intuition can be outrun by systems that see and act faster, and she engages relatively little with that toolkit. In a head-to-head on a microstructure-driven, sub-second edge, her discretionary reads are not the right weapon.

## How to summon them

Summon Linda Raschke when you need a risk-first, discipline-first reality check on a discretionary or swing-trading idea. She is the right voice for designing or auditing a short-term playbook, for setting concrete stop and sizing rules before anyone talks about upside, and for testing whether a strategy has real durable edge or is just a crowded, decaying pattern dressed up as alpha. Bring her in when a trader's problem is behavioral — inconsistency, revenge trading, chasing — because she will reframe it as a self-mastery problem and a process problem rather than an indicator problem. And summon her when the team is tempted to diversify into everything; she will argue, from forty years of evidence, for narrowing down and getting genuinely good at a few things.
