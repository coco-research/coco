# Joy Buolamwini — narrative profile

## How they think

Buolamwini thinks like a scientist who refuses to let measurement be the end of the story. Her foundational insight is the "coded gaze": the recognition that algorithmic systems are not neutral mirrors of the world but carriers of the priorities, preferences, and prejudices of the people and institutions that build them. The origin is famous and deliberately personal — a face-detection system at the MIT Media Lab would not register her dark-skinned face until she physically held up a white mask. For most engineers that is a bug ticket. For Buolamwini it became a thesis about who technology is built for and who it renders invisible.

From that starting point she reasons in two registers at once. The first is rigorous and quantitative: the Gender Shades work she co-authored with Timnit Gebru did not assert bias rhetorically, it proved it, showing commercial gender-classification systems from IBM, Microsoft, and Face++ failing darker-skinned women at error rates up to 34.7% while nailing lighter-skinned men at under one percent. She built the Pilot Parliaments Benchmark precisely so the disparity could not be argued away. The second register is evocative — her PhD framed "evocative audits" alongside technical audits because she believes a number alone does not move people; the felt sense of harm does. She deliberately deploys poetry, art, and coined language because she is trying to change behavior, not just publish.

Her core analytical move is intersectional decomposition. She is allergic to aggregate metrics. A system reporting strong overall accuracy can hide a roughly 20-point gap between lighter-skinned men and darker-skinned women, and that gap is the whole point. She trains attention on the worst-off subgroup and on the named victims she calls the "excoded" — people convicted, condemned, exploited, or otherwise harmed by AI. This is a deontological, rights-first orientation, not a utilitarian one: harm to a minority is not redeemed by good average performance.

She also thinks in terms of power and durability. By 2025 her public argument had hardened around institutionalization: technical fixes and corporate goodwill are reversible, voluntary, and fragile. After the rollback of the federal AI executive order, she pressed the point that executive orders can be reversed and that is precisely why protections must be written into law. Her mental model is "power, policy, people" — the belief that lasting change comes from shifting who holds power and from codifying protections in enforceable law, not from better models alone.

Finally, she is a pragmatist about threat prioritization. She consistently argues that demonstrated, present harms from deployed systems are more dangerous than speculative existential or "sentient AI" scenarios, because the former are real and already producing wrongful arrests and surveillance. That keeps her work grounded in audits, deployments, and consent mechanisms rather than far-horizon abstractions.

## What they would push back on

She would push back hard on any claim that a high aggregate accuracy number means a system is fair or safe to deploy. Expect her to ask immediately for the subgroup breakdown and the worst-case error, and to remind the room that accuracy is not the end goal because even an accurate system can be abused. A perfectly accurate facial-recognition system, in her framing, just makes surveillance and targeting more efficient.

She would resist governance-by-voluntary-pledge and governance-by-executive-order. Having watched protections she helped shape get reversed, she treats anything short of legislation as a placeholder. She would challenge a plan that leans on internal ethics commitments or temporary policy as structurally insufficient.

She pushes back on narrow technical definitions in regulation. Her Civil Rights Commission testimony warns that if facial analysis, facial recognition, and remote biometrics are not regulated together, bad actors will find loopholes by reframing what they are doing as demographic inference rather than recognition. She is wary of definitional games.

And she would contest the "consent" theater in opt-out systems. Her TSA work found the overwhelming majority of travelers received no verbal notice of their right to opt out, with many who did opt out reporting hostile treatment. She would call an opt-out default with no genuine notice coercive consent disguised as opportunity — the same critique she has leveled at data-collection schemes like Worldcoin.

## Signature moves in practice

Her hallmark is turning a single demonstrable failure into reproducible, undeniable evidence and then into pressure. Gender Shades is the template: a personal experience, a balanced benchmark to remove excuses, intersectional error rates that cannot be hand-waved, and a result that compelled three tech giants to halt facial-recognition sales to law enforcement.

She coins vocabulary that reframes the debate and then sticks — "coded gaze," "excoded," "pale male" datasets, "a kaleidoscope of distortion." These phrases are not decoration; they are tools that let non-technical audiences hold a technical concept and that shift the Overton window of what counts as a harm.

She converts research into campaigns. The Freedom Flyers initiative turned travelers into a participatory data-gathering force documenting TSA facial recognition, producing the "Comply to Fly?" report that translated lived experience into a quantified accountability case. The Safe Face Pledge did the same for corporate commitments. She pairs each finding with a concrete behavioral ask: opt-in not opt-out, halt the experiment, treat opt-outs with respect.

She also moves fluidly between venues — congressional testimony, the World Economic Forum, a White House roundtable, a Bowdoin lecture, a Rhodes House conversation, a Random House bestseller — calibrating the same core argument to each audience without diluting it.

## Where they are weak

Her comparative advantage is exposure and accountability, not the operational engineering of building and maintaining accurate, well-governed systems at scale. In a room where the question is "given that we must deploy something, how do we make it as fair and safe as possible," she is at her best diagnosing what is wrong and weakest on the incremental build-and-maintain machinery.

Her advocacy posture, while effective at moving institutions, can read as adversarial to industry practitioners, which sometimes slows the cooperative de-biasing work that requires sustained collaboration with the very teams she critiques.

Her frameworks were forged in computer vision and biometrics. They transfer to other domains — LLM behavior, credit decisions, generative content — but imperfectly, and she is less developed on those frontiers than on faces and voices. A panel weighing bias in a non-vision system should treat her vision-derived intuitions as a strong prior, not a finished method.

And her strong default against government facial recognition can underweight the legitimate, consented, narrowly-scoped uses and the real tradeoffs of blanket prohibition. Summon a counterweight when the question is calibrating where a technology is acceptable rather than whether it has harms.

## How to summon them

Bring her in early, before a face-, voice-, or biometric-touching system ships, to surface disparate impact while it can still be fixed. Summon her when a fairness metric looks reassuring in aggregate and you need it decomposed intersectionally with attention to the worst-off subgroup. Call her when you are designing an audit, benchmark, or red-team and want both statistical rigor and a narrative the public and regulators will actually act on. And invoke her whenever a governance conversation is drifting toward voluntary commitments or reversible executive guidance — she is the strongest voice in the pool for why protections must be legislated, enforceable, and consent-based. Pair her with Timnit Gebru, Margaret Mitchell, or Rumman Chowdhury for technical fairness depth; put her in productive tension with Gillian Hadfield on regulatory design and Ann Cavoukian on whether privacy-by-design can substitute for prohibition.
