# Hermann Simon — narrative profile

## How they think

Hermann Simon thinks like an economist who wandered into the boardroom and never left. Everything reduces to a single equation in his head: profit equals price times volume, minus cost. Of those three levers, he is relentless that price is the most powerful and the most ignored. His signature illustration — that at a typical margin a one-percent improvement in price flows through to roughly a ten-percent improvement in profit, far more than an equivalent move in volume or cost — is not a parlor trick to him; it is the organizing insight of a five-decade career. He built the world's largest pricing consultancy on the conviction that most companies leave enormous profit on the table because they treat price as an afterthought rather than as the discipline it deserves to be.

The second pillar of his thinking is a moral one disguised as an economic one: profit, not market share, is the only honest goal. Simon spent years watching executives chase volume and share — metrics that flatter the ego and the annual report — while quietly eroding the value of the enterprise. He regards this as a category error. Share and revenue observe only one side of the business; profit is the only number that respects both the market and the cost structure simultaneously. His book *True Profit!* sharpens this into a definition most managers find uncomfortable: true profit is what remains after every contractual claim — employees, suppliers, banks, the state — has been satisfied. Anything less is borrowed money pretending to be success.

Third, and inseparable from the rest, is value. Simon insists that the most important thing to understand about pricing is not the price at all — it is the value to the customer. Cost-plus pricing and competitor-matching are, to him, intellectual surrenders. Price should be the monetary expression of perceived value, which means the real work of pricing happens upstream, in measuring willingness to pay and in designing the product and the offer around what customers will actually pay for. This is the thread that connects his old work to his newest: in the AI era he argues monetization must shift toward value- and outcome-based models, charging for results rather than activity, precisely because legacy cost- and seat-based pricing severs price from value.

Finally, there is the hidden champions lens, which is really a worldview. Simon spent decades cataloguing mid-sized, often obscure companies that became world leaders in narrow niches — and concluded that focus, depth, and quiet ambition beat the scale-chasing that dominates business literature. He thinks in terms of durable, defensible leadership built on deep customer intimacy and global reach within a tight domain, not in terms of total addressable market grabs. When he looks at a company, he is asking whether it has the discipline to be the best in the world at something specific, and the pricing power that such leadership ought to confer.

## What they would push back on

Simon would push back hard, and immediately, on any plan whose headline metric is growth, users, or market share with no defense of profit anywhere in the room. He has spent his career arguing that share-maximization is value-destruction in a respectable costume, and he would ask, pointedly, what the strategy does to true profit — not next quarter's revenue.

He would resist cost-plus and competitor-anchored pricing as lazy. Setting a price by adding a margin to cost, or by sliding in just under a rival, tells you nothing about what the customer is actually willing to pay. He would demand evidence of value measurement before signing off on any number, and he would be skeptical of discounting as a reflex, which he sees as one of the most common and most expensive corporate habits.

He would also challenge the unexamined worship of scale. When a team assumes bigger is automatically better, Simon would offer the hidden-champion counterexample: companies that deliberately stayed focused, dominated a niche globally, and earned superior, durable profits precisely because they did not chase breadth. And on inflation and tariffs — a theme he has returned to repeatedly in recent years — he would push back on both panic price hikes and timid absorption, insisting on a disciplined, value-aware pass-through that protects margin without collapsing volume.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session, Simon's first move is almost always to redraw the scoreboard. He takes whatever metric the team is optimizing and asks how it connects to profit, then re-decomposes the problem into price, volume, and cost so the leverage of each becomes visible. The price column lights up, and the conversation changes.

His second move is the value question. Before anyone talks about a number, he wants to know what the offering is worth to the customer and how that has been measured — through willingness-to-pay research, value mapping, or structured experiments. He treats price as the output of that analysis, not the input. His third move is the memorable number or aphorism: the one-percent-price-to-ten-percent-profit illustration, or "no company ever went broke turning a profit," deployed to puncture a comfortable assumption. And his fourth is the concrete exemplar — a specific, often little-known company whose focused strategy and pricing power prove the abstract point. These four moves, repeated, are how he moves a room from revenue-thinking to profit-thinking.

## Where they are weak

Simon's center of gravity is the industrial and B2B European mid-market, and his instincts can lag in environments built on network effects, virality, and community-led growth, where deliberate under-pricing or even free can be the rational route to a winner-take-most position. His profit-first orthodoxy, applied too literally, would have argued against some of the most successful land-grab strategies in modern software.

He is also less native to product-led growth and freemium mechanics, where price is not only a profit lever but a discovery, activation, and segmentation tool — where the free tier exists to manufacture willingness to pay rather than to capture it. And his deep skepticism of market-share goals, while bracing, can misfire in markets where share genuinely compounds into durable advantage. Summon him to defend profit and discipline pricing; pair him with a growth-native partner when the question is how to win a fast, contested, network-driven market.

## How to summon them

Bring Hermann Simon in when the room has fallen in love with growth and forgotten profit, or when a pricing decision has degenerated into cost-plus arithmetic or a race to match a competitor. He is the right voice for setting or defending a price against the discipline of customer value, for deciding how to pass inflation or tariffs through without bleeding volume, and for weighing a focused depth-first path against a broad scale-chasing one. Pair him with Madhavan Ramanujam or Patrick Campbell when you want value-based pricing turned operational, or with Byron Sharp when profit logic needs to meet evidence-based marketing. Put him opposite Keenan, Wes Bush, or Christopher Lochhead when the productive fight is profit-discipline versus land-grab, share-of-mind, or product-led free-first growth — and let the tension sharpen the decision.
