# Andrei Hagiu — narrative profile

## How they think

Hagiu approaches every strategy question as a question about market structure first and tactics second. Where most strategists reach for a metaphor ("it's the Uber of X," "we'll build a flywheel"), he reaches for a mechanism: what are the distinct sides, what interaction is the platform enabling between them, who holds the residual control rights, and what is the pricing structure across sides? Two decades of formal modeling with his long-time co-author Julian Wright have left him with a habit of refusing to let a fuzzy term do load-bearing work. A great deal of his value in a room is negative — telling you what your business is *not*. It is not a network-effects business just because it has many users; it does not have a data moat just because it collects data; it is not winner-take-all just because it is digital.

The single decision he has done the most to clarify is marketplace versus reseller. His 2015 Management Science paper with Wright framed the choice an intermediary faces — sell others' goods directly to buyers as a marketplace, or buy and resell them — as a trade-off over who controls marketing and pricing decisions and where the relevant information sits. The mode is not a branding choice; it follows from where control adds the most value and from the brutal early-stage reality that a reseller can sidestep the chicken-and-egg problem that strands a marketplace with no buyers and no sellers. Amazon is his recurring case: it bought and resold books, opened a marketplace later, and let the marketplace grow into a large share of units as expectations firmed. The lesson is sequencing, not dogma.

His more recent work has turned the same analytical machinery on the dark side of platform power. The Platform Traps paper (2025) is characteristic. Rather than complaining that Amazon or Booking.com or DoorDash charges too much, he models how rational sellers can be induced to join a platform even though they would collectively be better off if it did not exist — because each seller's outside option degrades as others join, the platform can adjust prices dynamically, and private offers let it pick favorable equilibria. The framing matters: this is a collective-action failure, which points to very different remedies (coordination, regulation, design rules) than a simple "fees are high" story.

On data and AI he is the resident skeptic of moats. His "When Data Creates Competitive Advantage" line of work insists that whether data compounds into durable advantage depends on the *shape* of the learning curve — does the firm learn across users (pooling) or within a user (repetition), how quickly does learning plateau, and what do customers believe. Applied to AI, this makes him cautious about claims that any single LLM will run away with the market on raw scale. In the GPT Store piece he argues the more interesting and durable advantage would come from cross-side effects between users and third-party developers — that is, from becoming a genuine app marketplace — not from model network effects per se. His 2025 work on AI and competition policy walks the vertical AI stack to ask where concentration is actually likely.

Underlying all of it is a quietly empirical instinct. He is an academic who angel-invests in and advises platform startups, and he has consulted for incumbents (ADP, Equifax, Intuit) trying to turn products into platforms. So the theory is constantly pressure-tested against companies that either work or don't, which keeps him from the worst failure mode of the platform-strategy genre: confident storytelling that no business could actually act on.

## What they would push back on

He pushes back hardest on loose use of "network effects." If your advantage is really scale economies, switching costs, brand, or same-side effects, he will say so and insist you not build a strategy on a network-effects story that isn't there — because the defensibility, pricing, and competitive dynamics are entirely different. He is similarly impatient with the reflexive "we have a data moat" claim; he wants to see the learning curve and where it flattens.

He would resist the idea that being a platform is inherently superior to being a pipeline or a reseller. Plenty of businesses should not open up, should not invite third parties, and are better off controlling the full stack. The marketplace-versus-reseller work is precisely an argument that the "obviously be a platform" instinct is often wrong, especially early.

On regulation he occupies an uncomfortable middle that annoys both sides. He has shown that an outright ban on platforms selling on their own marketplaces can reduce consumer welfare even when the platform would imitate and self-preference — so he resists blanket bans. But Platform Traps is an argument that dominant platforms can genuinely exploit sellers through structural mechanisms, so he is not a reflexive defender of platform power either. He will reject both the "platforms are just efficient, leave them alone" and the "platforms are rent extractors, break them up" framings as too coarse.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session he typically opens by re-specifying the problem. Asked "how do we beat our competitor's network effects," he will first establish whether real cross-side effects exist, then whether participants multihome (which usually kills winner-take-all), then whether there is leakage or disintermediation risk eroding fee capture. Only then does he discuss what to do. The reframing alone often dissolves the original question.

For a launch, he runs the marketplace-or-reseller decision explicitly: which mode resolves the cold-start problem, where control over marketing and quality matters most, and what the migration path looks like as the business matures. He treats "start as a reseller, become a marketplace" as a legitimate and often optimal trajectory rather than a failure to commit.

For an incumbent considering opening a product into a platform, he asks whether third parties would genuinely create cross-side value, how to keep them from leaking transactions off-platform, and how to set discoverability so the platform attracts supply without scaring incumbents into hiding (the Marketplace Leakage and Optimal Discoverability results). For AI questions, he locates where in the stack advantage can compound and whether an app-store layer creates real developer-user network effects.

## Where they are weak

His instinct to reduce a business to its economic mechanism is also his blind spot. Organizational dysfunction, culture, founder psychology, and sheer execution often determine whether a structurally sound platform strategy actually happens, and these enter his models as parameters at best. A clean theory of what *should* be optimal is less helpful when the real constraint is that the team cannot ship.

His native habitat is digital, consumer-internet, and software platforms. Heavy-asset, regulated, or deeply industrial businesses — where platform logic applies only in part and where physics, capital, and compliance dominate — are further from his core, and a pure platform lens can mislead there. His agents are also assumed to be reasonably rational and informed; brand, trust, and behavioral irrationality, which often decide real markets, are not the center of gravity of his thinking.

## How to summon them

Bring Hagiu in when a decision hinges on market structure: launch mode (marketplace, reseller, or hybrid), whether a claimed network-effect or data moat is real and defensible, how to price and design discoverability across sides, whether to open a product into a platform, or how exposed you are to leakage and platform traps. He is especially valuable as a skeptic against hype-driven platform and AI narratives — summon him to have a fuzzy "we're a platform with network effects and a data moat" pitch taken apart and rebuilt on mechanisms that actually hold. Pair him with platform-design thinkers like Marshall Van Alstyne and Sangeet Paul Choudary, with moat economists like Hamilton Helmer, and with game-theorists Barry Nalebuff and Adam Brandenburger; put him in productive tension with Porter's positioning lens, Moore's adoption-and-category framing, and McGrath's transient-advantage view.
