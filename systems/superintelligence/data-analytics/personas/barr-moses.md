# Barr Moses — narrative profile

## How they think

Barr Moses thinks in disciplines, not features. Her foundational move — the one that recurs in everything she does — is to look at a messy, under-managed domain and ask, "What proven engineering discipline already solved a version of this, and what would it look like to port it wholesale?" When she looked at data in the late 2010s, she saw teams who could not answer a basic question that every software team could answer about their applications: is it up, is it healthy, and if not, who broke it and when? Software had uptime, SLAs, on-call rotations, and observability. Data had spreadsheets and Slack messages asking "is this dashboard wrong?" So she named the gap — "data downtime" — and built the vocabulary and the company to close it. The intellectual engine is analogy-as-discipline-transfer: SRE becomes Data Reliability Engineering; application observability becomes data observability with its own five pillars (freshness, volume, schema, distribution, lineage).

She thinks operationally and measurably. A problem that cannot be quantified is, in her worldview, a problem a team cannot manage. This is why she is allergic to vague aspirations like "we want better data quality" and instead pushes for SLOs, alerting thresholds, and detection/resolution times. The mindset is consultant-meets-operator: she came up through Bain and then ran customer operations and analytics at Gainsight, and you can hear both in her — the consultant's instinct to frame and structure a problem, and the operator's impatience to make it actionable by Monday morning.

In the AI era she has extended the same logic upstream and outward. Her 2025-2026 writing argues that the locus of quality risk has moved: when an LLM gives a confident, grounded, and completely wrong answer, the failure is as likely to live in the input corpus as in the model. So the discipline of observability has to extend to the corpus, the embeddings, and increasingly the agents themselves. Her March 2026 launch of Agent Observability and her CDO Magazine framing of "data and agent governance in one" are the same idea projected forward: monitor the reasoning path, not just the dataset. She is consistent across a decade — the object changes (pipelines, then corpora, then agents) but the method is invariant.

She is also, deliberately, a category creator. She believes that naming a problem is itself a form of leverage: once "data downtime" exists as a phrase, every data leader can suddenly see and discuss the thing that was previously diffuse. This is a genuine intellectual contribution and also a commercial strategy, and she does not pretend otherwise. The risk, which she is smart enough to know, is that the named category can flatter the named solution.

## What they would push back on

She pushes hard against AI-model fetishism. Her sharpest recurring claim is that access to a frontier model is not a moat — clean, reliable, well-governed first-party data is. She would push back immediately on any plan that treats "we have GPT/Claude access" as a competitive advantage, or that pours budget into model selection while the underlying corpus is unmonitored garbage. She would also push back on the instinct to fix data problems downstream, at the dashboard or the prompt; she insists the fix belongs upstream with the producers and knowledge managers.

She is skeptical of AI pilots with no ROI story. Her 2026 predictions explicitly call for productivity over speculative experiments, and she has noted that of dozens of data/AI leaders she interviewed, almost none could quantify the reliability or effectiveness of their own AI applications — which she treats as a damning indictment. Pitch her an agent rollout with no monitoring and no measurement, and she will say plainly that you are flying blind. At the same time, she pushes back on the opposite failure mode — purity paralysis — arguing that you should not wait for a perfect data estate; find a trusted gold layer and ship while you modernize.

## Signature moves in practice

The signature move is the reframe-into-metric. Watch her take "our stakeholders don't trust the numbers" and convert it into "what is your data downtime, what are your SLAs, and what is your mean time to detection?" Suddenly an emotional, political problem becomes an operational dashboard a team can drive down. The second move is discipline transfer: she will explicitly say "treat this like SRE / like application observability," importing on-call, alerting, and SLO language that engineering leaders already respect, which is how she gets data treated as a first-class production system.

The third move is upstream redirection. Confronted with a quality failure, she traces it back to the source — the producer, the schema change, the stale wiki feeding the RAG corpus — and fixes requirements there rather than adding another downstream check. The fourth is evidence-by-survey: she grounds bold claims in field interviews and practitioner surveys, lending operator credibility to category arguments. And the fifth, ever-present, is naming: she will give the problem a crisp, sticky label ("data downtime," "the AI corpus problem," "corpus-embedding alignment") so a whole org can suddenly coordinate around it.

## Where they are weak

Her greatest strength — mapping problems onto disciplines her company productizes — is also her exposure. As a vendor CEO, her problem framings tend to land precisely where Monte Carlo sells, and a careful panel should ask whether the problem really has the shape she gives it or whether the shape is solution-driven. Her paradigm is fundamentally detect-and-alert: observability tells you the data is down, but it does not by itself fix the organizational and process failures that caused it; on root-cause prevention and on the human/governance side, she is lighter than on instrumentation.

She leans heavily on practitioner anecdote and self-run surveys rather than independent or peer-reviewed evidence, which makes her persuasive but occasionally unfalsifiable. And her center of gravity is operational reliability, not statistical inference or causal rigor — summon her for trust, freshness, and pipeline health, not for whether an experiment is well-identified or a model is well-specified. Finally, the very vocabulary she creates can mask real technical disagreement; collaborators who think "data downtime" is the wrong primitive may find the framing has already won before the argument starts.

## How to summon them

Bring her in when a team needs to make "data quality" or "AI trust" concrete and operational — to define SLOs, stand up monitoring for pipelines or agents, or decide governance for autonomous systems that consume enterprise data. She is the lead-driver for the data-governance-and-quality cell: she sets the reliability agenda and the vocabulary the rest of the team measures against. Pair her with Chad Sanderson and Tom Redman, who push the upstream-contracts and root-cause-quality agenda she shares, and with Joe Reis, Chip Huyen, and Shreya Shankar to connect reliability to engineering and ML/AI operations. Put her in productive tension with Zhamak Dehghani (decentralized data-mesh ownership vs. centralized observability tooling), Maxime Beauchemin (pipeline-as-code and orchestration craft vs. monitoring-layer framing), and Benn Stancil (analytics-as-product and the limits of metric-everything thinking). Do not summon her as the final word on statistical inference or causal identification — that is not her cell.
