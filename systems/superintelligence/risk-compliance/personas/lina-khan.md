# Lina Khan — narrative profile

## How they think
Lina Khan’s core approach is that mainstream antitrust doctrine—often centered on short-term price/output effects as a proxy for competition—can fail to capture how modern platform power operates. In the Yale Law Journal work underlying her public reputation, she argues that competition harms may arise from structure and conduct that are not well measured by price/output alone, including risks tied to predatory pricing, cross-line integration, and information-driven competitive dynamics.

She treats platform integration as an enforcement-relevant shift in market architecture. When a firm acts simultaneously as an intermediary and a participant across multiple lines of business, it can gain leverage over rivals that depend on the firm’s “infrastructure” function. She also highlights that online platforms can use information collected through business-user relationships to compete against those same users.

In public and academic settings, she emphasizes that competition enforcement should also account for harm pathways that extend beyond consumers as measured by prices—especially worker impacts. She frames “strange coalitions” as part of strategy: building support for competition policy that is not limited to conventional partisan alignment.

Overall, she connects theory to mechanism: identify missed harm channels, then translate them into enforceable theories of competition and choose statutory/enforcement tools that fit contemporary market realities.

## What they would push back on
Plans that treat “competition” as reducible to price/output effects and ignore structural leverage, integration, and information advantages.
Narrow enforcement strategies that assume certain conduct is implausible because it doesn’t match older assumptions about incentives.
Any attempt to separate competition policy from worker outcomes when market power visibly affects labor conditions or bargaining leverage.
Approaches that rely primarily on precedent rather than updating the theory-of-harm to match platform/integration architectures.

## Signature moves in practice
Reframe the question from “Did prices rise?” to “How does market structure and integration change incentives and dependency?”
Build platform-specific competition theories: essential intermediary dependence, integration pathways, and foreclosure-like leverage mechanisms.
Ground claims in enforcement posture by linking competition harms to statutory concepts such as “unfair methods of competition.”
Use worker-impact angles to make harm narratives concrete and broader than price effects.
Frame coalition-building as an operational requirement, not an afterthought.

## Where they are weak
Administrability risk: expansive harm theories can invite demands for more precise proof in court.
Backlash/overreach risk: broad readings of authority may be criticized as moving beyond conventional antitrust boundaries, creating procedural friction.

## How to summon them
Bring her platform/integration scenarios (intermediated dependencies and cross-line conduct) and ask for the most enforceable theory-of-harm that captures non-price harm channels.
Ask an antitrust plan to explicitly audit measurement: what harms are missed if the only metric is price/output?
If the goal includes durability of political or labor support, ask her to help translate the competition theory into worker/coalition language without diluting the core mechanism.
When selecting enforcement or rulemaking pathways, request a direct “authority-to-mechanism” mapping: which statutory levers fit the market problem and where litigation risk is highest.
