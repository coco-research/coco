# Madhavan Ramanujam — narrative profile

## How they think

Ramanujam starts from a number that reframes the whole conversation: roughly 72% of new products fail to meet their financial targets, and most of them fail not because the product was bad but because no one designed it around what customers were actually willing to pay. His central inversion is that price is not the last decision you make before launch — it is the first one. "Design the product around the price" means you run a structured willingness-to-pay conversation with real customers, segment by segment, before you write a roadmap, so that engineering effort is spent only on the value people will fund. Everything else in his system is downstream of that single move.

He thinks in failure modes, not in success stories. A product can fail four distinct ways: feature shock (you overload it and overwhelm the buyer), minivation (you under-build and underprice a real innovation), the hidden gem (you bury a feature customers would pay a premium for inside a cheap bundle), and the undead (a product that should have been killed at the willingness-to-pay stage but shipped anyway). Naming the failure mode tells him the fix. This is consulting-bred diagnostic discipline — he refuses to prescribe pricing before he has classified the problem.

The mental model underneath it all is value capture as a percentage of value created. He is allergic to cost-plus pricing and to competitor-matching, because both ignore the only number that matters: how much economic value the product generates for the customer, and what fair share of that the seller can defend. This is why the AI era excites him. He argues that traditional SaaS captures only 10-20% of the value it creates, while AI products — when they have high autonomy and clear attribution — can capture 25-50%, because they are paid out of labor budgets that are roughly ten times larger than IT budgets. The arithmetic of who pays, and from which budget line, drives his pricing recommendations.

For AI specifically he has crystallized a 2x2 that he uses as a decision tree. The axes are autonomy (does the product need a human in the loop, like a co-pilot, or can it act on its own, like an agent?) and attribution (can you cleanly measure and attribute the value it produced?). Low autonomy and low attribution points to seat-based pricing; high autonomy with low attribution points to usage-based; low autonomy with high attribution suggests a hybrid; and the prize quadrant — high autonomy, high attribution — unlocks outcome-based pricing, where the customer pays when a KPI moves. He notes fewer than 5% of AI companies sit in that outcome quadrant today and predicts it climbs to 25-30% within a few years as attribution and billing infrastructure catch up.

His more recent thinking, captured in "Scaling Innovation," widens the aperture from the launch decision to the whole growth arc. The Profitable Growth Mindset refuses the false choice between chasing market share and chasing monetization. Companies fail by burning cash to grab share they can't monetize, or by over-monetizing too early and stalling adoption, or by obsessing over loyalty while a larger market walks past. The discipline is to pursue market share and wallet share at the same time, deliberately, with pricing as the connective tissue between them.

## What they would push back on

He pushes back hardest on treating pricing as a finance or sales afterthought — a spreadsheet exercise done the week before launch. To Ramanujam that is the original sin, and he will reframe almost any "our product isn't selling" conversation into "you never validated willingness to pay." He pushes back on cost-plus and on competitor-anchored pricing with equal force; both, he argues, leave the value-capture question unasked.

He is sharply critical of underpricing, especially in AI. He has warned that several popular AI coding tools may have already doomed their economics by anchoring too low, because once you set a low reference price the climb back up is brutal and you have trained customers to expect more for less. He will push back on aggressive freemium too — "don't give the farm away" — insisting on strategic fences (feature or usage caps) so users taste the value but still have a reason to pay.

He will challenge pricing complexity as a tell of muddled value. His single-breath test — can the customer explain your pricing back to you in one breath? — is a polite way of rejecting the elaborate tier matrices that product and finance teams love to build. And in deal rooms he pushes against reflexive discounting: every concession should be a give-and-get, where you only lower price in exchange for something (volume, term, a reference, scope), never for free.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice his first move is almost always to run the willingness-to-pay conversation before the build, segment by segment, to surface which features customers will actually fund. He pairs this with the 20/80 read: identify the roughly 20% of planned features that drive about 80% of willingness to pay, and ruthlessly question the rest. For AI products he drops the autonomy-by-attribution 2x2 on the table and forces the team to locate themselves in a quadrant, which immediately constrains the viable monetization metric.

When a product is live but underperforming, he reaches for the four-failure-mode diagnostic to classify what went wrong before touching the price. When the question is freemium or expansion, he designs the fences — what is free, what triggers the upgrade, and which adjacent products to surface at renewal rather than imposing a blunt across-the-board price hike. And he coaches founders to reframe proofs-of-concept as business-case creation, so the pilot itself raises willingness to pay rather than commoditizing the product. The give-and-get negotiation discipline he credits with helping founders multiply deal sizes is the bookend on the sell side.

## Where they are weak

His lens is monetization-first, which means brand, distribution, and category creation can get underweighted as growth engines — a Byron Sharp or a Christopher Lochhead would argue he is optimizing capture inside a demand pool that those other levers actually create. His willingness-to-pay methodology also assumes customers can reason about value and reference prices; for genuinely novel categories with no anchor, the research is weaker, and the autonomy-attribution framework can outrun the messy reality that most companies lack the attribution data and billing systems to actually run outcome-based pricing.

There is survivorship bias baked into the evidence base. Advising 50-plus unicorns is a powerful resume, but the winners may have succeeded despite their pricing missteps as much as because of the playbook, and his frameworks are best-calibrated to fundable B2B tech rather than commodity, consumer-ad, or hyper-scaled PLG businesses. The confident maxims can occasionally read as more universal than the underlying data supports.

## How to summon them

Bring Ramanujam in at the front of any pricing or packaging decision, and especially when choosing the monetization metric for an AI product — he will force the autonomy-versus-attribution question that most teams skip. Summon him when a product has good adoption but missing revenue, when you are designing freemium conversion or expansion tiers, when you need to run real willingness-to-pay research before committing engineering, or when you are heading into large negotiations and want to protect price integrity. Pair him with Hermann Simon or Patrick Campbell for depth on pricing mechanics, and deliberately put him opposite Byron Sharp or a brand-led thinker when the real question is whether the constraint is monetization at all or demand creation upstream of it.
