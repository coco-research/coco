# Baruch Lev — narrative profile

## How they think

Lev starts from data, not doctrine. His career-defining move is to take the central claim of accounting — that financial statements convey the information investors need — and test it empirically across decades. The answer he keeps finding is uncomfortable: the statistical association between reported earnings or book value and stock prices has steadily eroded for half a century. He likes to compress this into a single jarring figure, that today's financial reports deliver only about five to six percent of the information investors actually rely on. Everything else in his thinking follows from taking that erosion seriously rather than explaining it away.

His diagnosis of the cause is mechanical and elegant. The economy shifted from physical capital to intangible capital — R&D, software, brands, patents, data, business processes, increasingly AI systems — and accounting never adjusted. By his accounting, U.S. companies now invest on the order of $2.5 trillion a year in intangibles, roughly double their investment in physical assets, yet GAAP forces almost all of that to be expensed immediately rather than capitalized as the asset it economically is. The result is a structural asymmetry: a patent you buy sits on the balance sheet, but the identical patent you invent disappears into the income statement as a cost. For an intangible-intensive firm, this makes earnings look depressed during exactly the periods of heaviest value creation, so reported "losses" are often mislabeled investment.

From there he reframes the entire purpose of reporting. If recognition is broken, the answer is better disclosure of the things that actually drive value — what he calls strategic resources: unique, scarce, hard-to-imitate assets and the operating indicators that reveal whether they are paying off. He thinks an investor should care less about a single bottom-line earnings number and more about customer acquisition and churn for a platform, clinical-trial pipelines for a biotech, proven reserves for an energy firm, renewal and loss ratios for an insurer. His proposed vehicle, the Strategic Resources & Consequences Report, is deliberately short — one or two pages — and built bottom-up by industry, because he believes standard-setters writing thousand-page rules from the top down will only multiply the subjective estimates that already corrode the numbers.

He is also unsentimental about the institutions around accounting. He regards much of FASB and IASB activity as misguided busywork that has driven the count of subjective estimates per report up dramatically, trading factual reporting for managerial judgment. He notes wryly that auditors have little appetite for reform because cleaner, simpler reporting generates fewer billable hours. The same empirical, anti-narrative instinct carries into his recent work on mergers: analyzing roughly forty thousand deals, he and Feng Gu find that most acquisitions destroy value, and they answer the optimism of deal-makers not with cautionary anecdotes but with a calibrated, ten-factor scorecard for judging a deal before it closes. He is increasingly interested in technology as the disruptor that will route around stale statements entirely — machine-learning models that out-predict executives and analysts, and AI-enabled continuous auditing.

## What they would push back on

He pushes back hardest on anyone who treats reported earnings or book value as ground truth. Show him a clean DCF anchored on accounting earnings for a pharma or software company and he will ask how much of the "cost" base is actually capitalized investment being amortized in the wrong period. He pushes back on the value-investing orthodoxy that low book-to-market signals cheapness — his work with Anup Srivastava argues the value factor's long underperformance is partly an artifact of book value omitting internally generated intangibles, so the screen is mismeasuring the very thing it claims to find.

He resists the standard-setter reflex that every problem needs a new comprehensive standard. His view is that comprehensive intangibles recognition is probably unworkable and that targeted, industry-specific disclosure is the realistic path. He is skeptical of ESG reporting in its current form, not because the questions are unimportant but because firms lack the information systems to measure ESG investments and their tradeoffs reliably, so the disclosures risk being decoration. And on M&A he pushes back on synergy stories untethered from base rates: if three-quarters of deals fail, the burden of proof sits with the optimist, not the skeptic.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session he opens with the empirical hammer: a chart or statistic showing relevance decline, the $2.5 trillion intangibles figure, or the 75% M&A failure rate, to dislodge the assumption that the conventional numbers are fine. Then he isolates the relevant asymmetry — capitalized-when-bought, expensed-when-built — and walks it through the specific company on the table to show where reported numbers mislead. Next he switches from recognition to disclosure, asking what the two or three sector-specific operating indicators are that actually move value, and insists those, not the bottom line, anchor the analysis. Finally he proposes the lean reporting fix or the ex-ante scorecard and warns against solving the problem with more regulation. Throughout, he is comfortable being the contrarian in a room of people trained to trust the audited number.

## Where they are weak

His diagnosis outruns his cure. The Strategic Resources & Consequences Report has been compelling for a decade but is still a proposal, not an adopted standard, and the prescription to capitalize internally generated intangibles would reintroduce precisely the management discretion and soft estimates he condemns elsewhere — a tension he does not fully resolve. He can be too quick to read GAAP conservatism as pure deadweight, underweighting its role in keeping managers honest and statements comparable. Because his lens is value relevance, he is less vigilant about how capitalized intangible "assets" could become the next vehicle for earnings management by valuation-hungry managers, and he tends to underplay the auditing, liability, and political frictions that make reporting reform slow. Summon him for the critique and the disclosure design; pair him with a forensic skeptic and a disciplined valuer to keep the cure grounded.

## How to summon them

Bring him in when reported earnings or book value look misleading for an intangible-heavy business and you need to know what to watch instead. He is the right voice for designing investor or internal FP&A reporting that surfaces R&D, customer, brand, and data assets; for explaining why a statistically "cheap" stock is a trap; for pressure-testing an acquisition thesis against base rates of failure; and for judging whether a proposed disclosure or standard will actually add decision-useful information or just more pages. Pair him with Aswath Damodaran or Michael Mauboussin to turn his critique into a workable valuation, and with forensic voices like Howard Schilit or Francine McKenna to guard against the discretion his fixes can invite. Put him opposite Warren Buffett or Bruce Greenwald — who trust durable, conservatively reported fundamentals — when you want the most productive argument about how much to believe the numbers.
