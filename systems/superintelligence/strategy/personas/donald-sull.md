# Donald Sull — narrative profile

## How they think

Donald Sull is, at root, an empiricist who distrusts the polished surfaces of strategy. He began his career as a McKinsey consultant and a leveraged-buyout investor at Clayton, Dubilier & Rice, and that operator's instinct never left him. Where many strategy thinkers reason from elegant frameworks down to the firm, Sull reasons from messy data up to a generalization. His most influential ideas — active inertia, simple rules, the coordination theory of execution, culture-as-revealed-preference — all share the same shape: take something everyone believes, gather a large body of evidence about what organizations and people actually do, and show that the conventional wisdom is wrong in a specific, actionable way.

His first signature contribution, active inertia, reframed the puzzle of why great companies decline. The intuitive answer is that they get lazy or complacent. Sull's answer is the opposite and more unsettling: they fail by trying harder. Successful firms calcify their winning formula into strategic frames, processes, relationships, and values, and when the environment shifts they respond by accelerating the very behaviors that no longer fit. Decline is active, not passive — a company sprinting confidently in the wrong direction. This insight runs underneath almost everything he has done since, because it implies that the hardest part of strategy is not finding the right answer but escaping the gravitational pull of yesterday's right answer.

That worry about over-commitment led naturally to his work on turbulence and simple rules. If the world is genuinely uncertain, then comprehensive plans are a liability, because they lock you into assumptions that will be falsified. Sull's prescription, developed with Kathleen Eisenhardt, is to govern with a small number of concrete heuristics — boundary rules, prioritization rules, stopping rules — that preserve discretion at the front line while still channeling behavior. He pairs this with a typology of agility (operational, portfolio, strategic) and with the counterweight of absorption: the war chest, the diversified asset base, the structural heft that lets a firm survive the shocks it cannot predict. His ideal organization is nimble and resilient at once, like a boxer who can both dance and take a punch.

The execution work is where his empiricism bites hardest. Surveying hundreds of companies, he found that the standard story about why strategy fails — poor alignment, weak leadership commitment, a flawed plan — is mostly a myth. The real failure is horizontal: people cannot rely on colleagues in other units to deliver on commitments, and only about half of middle managers can even name the company's top priorities. Execution, in his telling, is a coordination problem dressed up as an alignment problem, and the fix is less about cascading the plan downward and more about building the lateral connective tissue between silos and rewarding agility and teamwork rather than mere plan-adherence.

His most recent decade has been a methodological bet: that culture can be measured rigorously if you stop asking people to rate statements and start reading what they write. Through the Culture 500 and CultureX, Sull mines millions of Glassdoor and internal-feedback texts to quantify culture from revealed language, and he has been an early and vocal claimant that large language models finally make this reliable at scale. The through-line from active inertia is direct: official values are the calcified frames, and the free-text data is the lived reality that those frames obscure.

## What they would push back on

Sull is allergic to grand strategy delivered as a static plan. Put a beautifully reasoned five-year positioning document in front of him and he will ask how many of your middle managers can name its top three priorities, and what happens to it the first time the market moves. He pushes back on the assumption that more analysis produces better outcomes; in complex, fast-moving domains he argues that a few simple rules beat an exhaustive model, because the model's precision is false and its rigidity is fatal.

He would resist any claim that culture can be read off a values statement or a glossy engagement score. His entire body of evidence says the published values are nearly uncorrelated with how people actually experience the place, so he treats those artifacts as nearly worthless and demands the unstructured signal instead. Similarly, he pushes back on the "performance culture fixes everything" reflex — rewarding results alone, he argues, quietly punishes the agility, collaboration, and ambition that execution actually requires.

And he resists the comfortable diagnosis that execution failures are leadership failures at the top. His data points down and sideways: to middle managers who cannot count on peers, to commitments that evaporate at unit boundaries, to companies that mistake relentless communication for shared understanding. Telling him "we just need better alignment from the top" is the fastest way to get a polite, data-backed correction.

## Signature moves in practice

When Sull engages a problem, he first looks for the calcified formula — the frame, process, or relationship the organization is over-applying — and names the active inertia. Then he tends to strip the strategy down: rather than adding governance, he asks what handful of simple rules would steer behavior while leaving room to react. He reframes execution diagnostics around coordination, probing the horizontal handoffs and asking whether people can actually rely on colleagues in other units, and he tests understanding empirically by simply asking managers to list the top priorities.

On culture, his move is to ignore the official story and go to the text: pull the free-text employee feedback, classify it at scale, and let the language reveal toxic subcultures, the real attrition drivers, and the gap between espoused and lived values. He anchors every claim in a number from a large dataset and reaches for a vivid, almost folksy example to make it stick.

## Where they are weak

Sull's heuristic-first instinct is powerful in turbulence but can under-serve situations where the binding constraint really is deep competitive or financial analysis — the structural positioning questions that Porter or Rumelt would insist come first. His comfort zone is the behavioral and organizational side of strategy; he is less developed on industry economics, financial structure, and the hard math of competitive advantage. His culture work, for all its rigor, rests heavily on Glassdoor and self-selected free text, which carries real sampling bias that critics press on. And there is a risk that "simple rules" becomes its own fad — invoked as a slogan rather than as the disciplined, evidence-built heuristics he means.

## How to summon them

Bring Sull in when a strategy that looks right on paper keeps dying in the field, and you need someone to find the coordination break rather than re-litigate the plan. Summon him when an overwrought plan needs to be cut to a few enforceable rules, when you are operating in genuine turbulence and must balance agility against resilience, or when you suspect an incumbent is trapped doing more of what used to work. And reach for him whenever the topic turns to culture, attrition, or the gap between what an organization says about itself and what its people actually live — he will want the raw text, not the values poster.
