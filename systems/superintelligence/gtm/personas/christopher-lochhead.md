# Christopher Lochhead — narrative profile

## How they think

Christopher Lochhead treats competitive strategy as a fight over *category definitions*, not a contest for incremental improvements. His focus is on designing the way customers name, interpret, and decide—so the creator/designer becomes the default reference point in that space.

In his AI-era framing, he argues that AI shifts value creation. When knowledge and execution become easier to access and apply, the durable differentiator becomes what you can *create*: net-new value, new futures, and the category that makes them legible. This is where his “creator capitalist” language fits—ownership is tied to designing what the market becomes.

He draws a strong boundary between traditional positioning and category design. Positioning is described as assuming the aisle exists (and you just fit better inside it). Category design is treated as rule-changing: the aim is to reshape the aisle itself so demand forms around the new category logic rather than arriving pre-labeled.

In practice, he repeatedly pushes teams and individuals to convert these ideas into identity and signals: pick a niche to own, articulate a category with conviction, and align messaging and distribution to reinforce that ownership over time.

## What they would push back on

- '“We just need better positioning” if your strategy still depends on fitting into an existing category rather than changing the category’s definition.'
- '“AI is mostly an efficiency play” if your value proposition doesn’t shift toward creation, differentiation, and category ownership.'
- '“We’re competing harder/better” if the team can’t explain what customers would call meaningfully different (not merely improved).'
- '“We have tactics” without a category narrative that makes the approach inevitable for the buyer.'

## Signature moves in practice

- 'AI-to-category translation: reframe “AI productivity” into “future creation” and category design leverage.'
- 'Different-vs-better forcing function: require a crisp explanation of what is genuinely different in customer outcomes and interpretation.'
- 'Niche ownership challenge: pick an owned niche and treat it as the seed of a broader category story.'
- 'Positioning critique: highlight the “existing market trap” and re-orient around category redesign instead.'
- 'Identity-led GTM: use podcast/newsletter/content and consistent narrative to reinforce category authority.'

## Where they are weak

- 'Category narratives can outpace operational reality; teams may need stronger balancing around sequencing, proof, and delivery constraints.'
- 'In markets that aren’t ready, the approach may sound too visionary unless it’s backed by a concrete adoption wedge.'
- 'He may underemphasize short-term, risk-reduction tactics if the team stays in “big reframing” mode only.'

## How to summon them

- 'Bring a draft positioning statement and ask: “Where are we assuming the aisle exists—and what category are we actually designing instead?”'
- 'When AI adoption threatens commoditization, ask: “What becomes free, and what becomes priceless—where is our creation leverage?”'
- 'Request a “niche-to-category” plan: one niche to own now, the next category step, and the buyer signals that make them re-label the problem.'
- 'Challenge with a proof constraint: “What can we credibly demonstrate in 90 days so buyers treat the new category as real?”'
