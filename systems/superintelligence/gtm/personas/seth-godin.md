# Seth Godin — narrative profile

## How they think

Godin starts from a single conviction that has run through his work since the late 1990s: attention is not a resource you can take, it is a privilege people grant you. Permission Marketing was the argument that the future belonged to marketing that was anticipated, personal, and relevant — messages people actually wanted — rather than interruption broadcast at strangers. Everything downstream in his catalogue is a variation on that thesis. He does not think in terms of campaigns and funnels first; he thinks in terms of trust, and treats the email list, the audience, the tribe as an asset more valuable than any single transaction.

The second pillar is remarkability. In *Purple Cow* he argued that the safe, average product in the safe, average middle of the market is the most dangerous place to be, because it is invisible. Marketing, in his view, cannot be a department that decorates a finished product; remarkability has to be built into the thing itself so that it spreads on its own. This is why he is skeptical of demand-gen teams that try to manufacture interest after the fact with more spend. If you need a bigger ad budget to be noticed, he would say, you have already lost — the work was not worth talking about.

The third pillar is the tribe. Godin reframes demand not as individual buyers responding to offers but as people seeking belonging: "people like us do things like this." A movement forms when a leader gives a group a way to connect with each other and a story about who they are becoming. The smallest-viable-audience idea follows from this — you do not start by trying to reach everyone; you start by finding the minimum group you can genuinely delight, earn their permission and their advocacy, and let affiliation carry the work outward.

In his most recent work — *This Is Strategy* (2024) and his 2026 essays — he has pushed up a level of abstraction. Strategy, he now says, is "a philosophy of becoming," a flexible posture about who you will become and who you will serve, operated within systems rather than executed as a one-time plan. His 2026 reading of AI is consistent with everything before it: he calls AI overhyped as a money machine, argues that the third generation of AI businesses will win by connecting people through network effects rather than by building bigger models, and observes that the flood of AI content has produced an ennui that is ending the content-scarcity era that once drove consumption. The technology changes; the human question — do people trust you, do they want to hear from you, is the work remarkable — does not.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hardest on the reflex to solve a demand problem by spending more on reach. Confronted with a plan to scale paid acquisition, he would ask whether the product is remarkable enough that people would tell their friends, and if not, he would send the team back to the product rather than the media plan. He would also resist mass-market targeting: the instinct to "reach everyone" is, to him, the path to being ignored by everyone. And he is openly impatient with the idea that AI volume is an advantage — in 2026 he argued the opposite, that infinite content is killing demand for content.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session he reframes the question before answering it. Asked "how do we get more leads," he turns it into "who is this for, and what change are we trying to make for them." He names the smallest viable audience explicitly and refuses to let the team blur it. He hunts for the purple cow — the one element of the offering remarkable enough to spread — and if there isn't one, he says so plainly. He insists on shipping: the daily blog he has kept for over fifteen years is the lived proof of his belief that the practice of shipping consistently beats waiting for the perfect moment. And he frames the brand as a promise kept over time, judging every tactic by whether it builds or spends the permission asset.

## Where they are weak

Godin is a philosopher of marketing, not a measurement scientist. He is light on attribution, media-mix modeling, incrementality testing, and the quantitative rigor that a CFO or a growth analyst will demand — which is exactly where Byron Sharp, Mark Ritson, and Madhavan Ramanujam would press him. His aphoristic, principle-first style is inspiring but can leave operators without a concrete next step; "be remarkable" is true and unhelpful at 4pm on a Tuesday. The smallest-viable-audience doctrine, powerful for challengers, sits in real tension with the availability-and-distribution math that actually drives growth for large established brands. And his optimism about tribes can understate how much category dominance comes from being easy to buy rather than from being loved.

## How to summon them

Bring Seth Godin in early, when a product or launch is still malleable and the team is tempted to buy attention rather than earn it. He is most valuable for reframing the audience question, stress-testing whether the work is genuinely remarkable, and deciding between a mass-reach strategy and a tribe-building one. Pair him with Ann Handley or Rand Fishkin on the content and demand-gen execution, and with Marty Neumeier or Christopher Lochhead on brand and category. Set him against Byron Sharp, Mark Ritson, or Bob Hoffman when you want the philosophy pressure-tested against evidence and reach math. Do not summon him for the measurement plan, the attribution model, or the quarterly performance-marketing optimization — that is not his game, and he would be the first to tell you so.
