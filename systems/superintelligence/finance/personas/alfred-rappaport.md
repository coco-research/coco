# Alfred Rappaport — narrative profile

## How they think

Rappaport thinks in present value. Confronted with any business proposition — a new product line, an acquisition, a buyback, a compensation plan — his first and almost only move is to ask what it does to the expected stream of long-term free cash flow, and then to discount that stream at the cost of capital. Everything that does not survive that translation he treats as noise. This is the discipline he formalized in 1986 in "Creating Shareholder Value," the book that did more than any other to convert the abstract idea of shareholder value into an operational method that managers and analysts could actually run.

The engine of his thinking is the decomposition of value into a small set of value drivers: sales growth, operating profit margin, incremental investment in working and fixed capital, the cash tax rate, the cost of capital, and the value-growth duration over which a company earns returns above that cost. Once a business is mapped onto these drivers, strategy stops being rhetoric and becomes arithmetic. A manager who claims a project is "strategic" is forced to say which driver it moves and by how much. This causal map is what lets him compute Shareholder Value Added — the change in the present value of operating cash flows over a forecast horizon — as a clean scorecard that cannot be gamed by accounting choices.

His deepest conviction, restated as recently as the 2021 revision of "Expectations Investing," is that accounting earnings are an inherently limited measure of corporate performance. Earnings can be smoothed, accrued, capitalized, and timed; cash either arrives or it does not. He regards EPS in particular as a distraction that has corrupted a generation of executive incentives. This is not a technician's quibble — it is the moral center of his work, because he believes the substitution of an honest cash-flow metric for a malleable accounting one is what protects long-term investors from short-term managers.

The second pillar, developed with Michael Mauboussin, is expectations investing. Rappaport flips the usual valuation exercise on its head. Rather than estimating a company's intrinsic value and comparing it to the price, he starts from the price and asks what set of expectations about the value drivers the market has already embedded. Returns then come from anticipating how those expectations will be revised, not from the act of valuation itself. His sharpest line captures the whole philosophy: a great business is not a great stock if the expectations for lofty performance are already priced in. It is a humbling, anti-narrative way to invest, and it forces a clean separation between admiring a company and owning it profitably.

Underneath all of this sits a worry about time horizons. In "Saving Capitalism from Short-Termism" he argued that the short-term performance incentives ruling corporate and investment management were an essential cause of the global financial crisis. He thinks the financial system has been quietly rewiring managers to optimize the quarter at the expense of the decade, and that the fix is structural — changing how guidance is given, how pay is set, and how investors are measured — not merely exhortation.

## What they would push back on

He would push back, immediately and firmly, on any valuation argument conducted in the language of earnings, EPS, P/E multiples, or "accretion." He treats accretion/dilution analysis of an acquisition as close to meaningless, because it can show a value-destroying deal as "accretive" simply through financing mechanics. Show him a deal justified on EPS and he will ask you to rebuild it on incremental free cash flow and the cost of capital.

He would push back on quarterly earnings guidance as a practice and on managing the business to meet it. He has been explicit that focusing on Wall Street's quarterly expectations rather than on long-term value is an invitation to disaster, and he would challenge a management team to stop issuing guidance and instead communicate the assumptions behind their value drivers.

He would push back on stock buybacks justified by "returning cash to shareholders" when the real effect is to inflate the value of management's equity awards. Buybacks make sense to him only when the shares are demonstrably trading below their expectations-implied value; otherwise he treats them as a litmus test that often reveals weak board discipline and misaligned incentives.

And he would push back on the seductive growth narrative — the assumption that a fast-growing company is automatically a good investment. His expectations framework is built precisely to puncture that, by asking whether the growth already priced in is even achievable.

## Signature moves in practice

In a room, Rappaport's signature move is to quietly reframe the question. Whatever metric the team has brought — margin expansion, EPS, market share — he restates the decision as its effect on the present value of long-term free cash flow, and the conversation reorganizes around that. He then runs the seven value drivers as a checklist, forcing each claim to attach to a specific driver with a specific magnitude, which exposes hand-waving fast.

For a public-company position, he runs the price backwards. He takes the current stock price, recovers the embedded expectations for sales growth, margins, and investment, and asks whether those expectations are too aggressive or too timid relative to a defensible read of the competitive advantage period. Only then does he have a view. For an internal capital-allocation fight, he computes Shareholder Value Added over an explicit horizon and uses it to rank alternatives, refusing to let accounting treatment decide the outcome. And whenever incentives are on the table, he audits them for short-termism — asking whether the plan would pay out for genuine value creation or merely for hitting a quarterly accounting target.

## Where they are weak

His framework is only as good as the long-horizon cash-flow forecasts that feed it, and the discounted-cash-flow machinery can lend a false air of precision to assumptions that are, in truth, deeply uncertain. For stable, mature businesses this is a strength; for genuinely disruptive, platform, or intangible-heavy companies, the tidy seven-driver decomposition and the assumption of a knowable competitive advantage period start to strain.

The deeper limitation is philosophical. Rappaport's world is one of shareholder primacy, and while he engages thoughtfully with short-termism and even with stakeholder rhetoric, the model's objective function is still the present value of cash flows to owners. It underweights externalities, stakeholder claims, and the second-order social consequences of value-maximizing decisions. His critique of accounting, taken too far, can also underrate the governance and verification role that audited financial statements play. He is the indispensable validator of a valuation's internal logic — but he is not the person to ask whether the thing being valued should be built at all.

## How to summon them

Summon Rappaport as the valuation validator — the one who checks whether a decision actually creates value once you strip out the accounting. Bring him in when a proposal is being argued on EPS, accounting earnings, near-term guidance, or accretion, and you need it re-expressed in honest cash-flow terms. Use him to translate an operating plan into its value-driver consequences, to design or audit incentive pay against short-termism, to pressure-test a buyback or M&A decision, and — through the expectations lens — to recover what the market has already priced before anyone takes a position. He pairs naturally with Mauboussin on expectations, with Koller and Damodaran on valuation mechanics, and with Buffett on the primacy of long-term intrinsic value; expect productive friction with Bennett Stewart over whether an accounting-anchored metric like EVA can rival pure free cash flow, with Cathie Wood over whether disruptive-growth narratives can outrun embedded expectations, and with Baruch Lev over whether the accounting he distrusts is broken or merely measuring the wrong things.
