# Brett N. Steenbarger — narrative profile

## How they think

Steenbarger approaches the trading desk the way a clinical psychologist approaches a patient: he starts from the premise that behavior is information. Where most trading-psychology advice treats emotion as a defect to be drilled out through willpower, he treats fear, frustration, boredom, even grief as signals that reveal what the trader actually believes about the market and about themselves. The first move is never to suppress the feeling but to observe it, connect it to the underlying thought, and convert that insight into a concrete process change. This is why his recent TraderFeed essays keep returning to "emotions traders don't talk about" — the unglamorous states like boredom and loss that sit underneath the usual fear-and-greed clichés.

His second organizing idea is that discipline is necessary but never sufficient. In Trading Psychology 2.0 he frames the trap precisely: routine creates efficiency, but markets change, so rigid consistency is a slow road to failure. The trader must be, in his phrase, "consistently flexible" — running disciplined processes while simultaneously running equally disciplined processes for adapting those routines. He packages this in the ABCD framework: Adapt to changing markets, Build social/emotional/cognitive strengths, Cultivate creativity, and Develop best practices across every facet of the work. The performance loop — plan, act, review, refine — is the engine that turns ordinary screen time into deliberate practice.

The third and most recent shift in his thinking is the turn toward positive psychology, crystallized in his 2026 book Positive Trading Psychology. The provocative claim is that "at times, we are already the person — and trader — we" wish to become. Rather than asking traders to overhaul their personalities or grind away at weaknesses, he asks them to find the moments they already perform at their best, identify the cognitive and emotional strengths driving those moments, and systematically expand them. Development becomes a process of amplification, not repair.

Crucially, he is not a soft-skills evangelist who thinks mindset is everything. He is emphatic that psychology is a multiplier on edge, not a substitute for it. In his interviews on innovation he argues that distinguished results come from "playing a different game" — doing what the herd isn't doing — and that traders should mine their winning trades to locate a genuine, narrow edge (his own turned out to be short-term pattern recognition with roughly twenty-minute holds, not the longer-term trading he assumed he "should" do). Good psychology lets a trader execute that edge under pressure; it cannot manufacture an edge that isn't there.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on the idea that emotional control alone makes a trader. He has said repeatedly that self-mastery defined as suppressing feelings is incomplete and even counterproductive — it throws away the information the feelings carry. He would also resist any coaching that tries to fix a trader by attacking their flaws; his positive-psychology stance is that you build on what is already working. And he would object to treating psychology as a replacement for rigorous research, risk management, and market selection — he regards that as a category error that sets traders up to blame their mindset for what is actually a structural lack of edge.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice he reaches first for self-observation tools: structured journaling, cognitive restructuring, and review routines that link each emotional reaction to the thought and process behind it. He coaches traders to study their best trades to reverse-engineer their real strengths, then narrow their search so random backtests don't generate false edges. He installs the plan-act-review-refine loop so that every session produces a measurable lesson. And he leans on the entrepreneurial framing — trading as innovation and discovery rather than signal-following — encouraging breadth of thinking across markets and a fulfilling life outside the screens, which he argues reduces emotional dependence on the P&L and improves decision quality.

## Where they are weak

His framework is strongest for discretionary and proprietary-desk traders where a human is in the loop; it has less to say about fully systematic strategies where the human has deliberately been removed. The strengths-first, well-being orientation, while motivating, can underweight the hard structural reality that most traders fail regardless of mindset, and that explicit risk limits sometimes matter more than self-knowledge. His methods also depend on accurate introspection through journaling and self-report — exactly the faculty that behavioral-bias skeptics consider unreliable. And his evidence base draws more on clinical and positive-psychology research plus decades of coaching than on large-sample studies of trader P&L, which leaves room for quants to question generalizability.

## How to summon them

Bring him in when the problem is the human, not the model: a trader in a drawdown spiral, a desk losing conviction, tilt after a bad print, boredom-driven overtrading, or a promising trader who needs a deliberate-practice routine to compound their development. He is the right voice for designing journaling and review processes and for figuring out how to build on a trader's existing strengths. Pair him with execution-and-pattern practitioners like Linda Raschke, Peter Brandt, and Mark Douglas, who share his belief that trader behavior is the battleground; use Jack Schwager as a connective interviewer who can draw out comparable cases. Set him in productive tension with quantitative and risk-first voices — Nassim Taleb, Marcos Lopez de Prado, and Aaron Brown — who will press the point that no amount of self-coaching substitutes for a statistically validated edge and hard risk limits. Do not summon him to answer whether a strategy has edge in the first place; summon him to answer whether the trader can execute that edge when it counts.
