# Glenn Hopper — narrative profile

## How they think

Glenn Hopper thinks like a CFO who has been burned by technology promises and learned to demand evidence before belief. His starting premise is almost stubbornly unglamorous: before anyone talks about generative AI, autonomous agents, or AGI in the finance function, the data has to be in order. He says it bluntly — if your data isn't governed and clean, this is not a technology you can use yet. That single conviction organizes most of his reasoning. He treats data readiness as the gating constraint, and model capability as strictly downstream of it. Where a vendor sees a demo, Hopper sees the reconciliation and master-data work that has to happen first.

His second mental habit is incrementalism. Rather than pitching a sweeping automation of the finance function, he advocates for a series of small, measurable wins — projects narrow enough to ship, prove, and use as proof points to win organizational buy-in. This is a deliberately political reading of technology adoption: he understands that the hardest part of AI in finance is not the algorithm but the change management, and that trust is built one demonstrable result at a time. His 2026 advice distills to four moves: activate one AI capability already embedded in your existing systems, redesign one workflow around it, stand up basic governance tracking, and honestly assess what your people actually found useful in last year's pilots.

Third, he holds a clear-eyed model of the AI capability ladder — narrow AI (ANI), general AI (AGI), superintelligence (ASI) — and insists that today's finance applications live firmly in the narrow band. This lets him be simultaneously excited and disciplined: he will happily acknowledge the trajectory toward more capable systems while refusing to deploy as if that future has already arrived. He plans for the curve but builds for what works this quarter, favoring AI capabilities embedded inside the platforms a company already owns (Microsoft, SAP, Oracle, NetSuite) over net-new tooling.

Fourth, he is a "trust but verify" thinker. Generative AI, in his view, democratizes analysis — letting people query data in plain language instead of SQL or Python — but it also misunderstands instructions and fabricates plausible-looking output. So he wants finance professionals to retain enough technical literacy to validate what the machine produces. His repeated career advice is that the people who pair finance expertise with the ability to read and check code will be the standouts, precisely because they can oversee AI-generated work and catch its errors.

Finally, he thinks about the shape of the finance organization itself. He argues AI pushes work upward: the entry-level model-building and data-wrangling that used to season junior analysts gets automated, which forces those analysts to deliver strategic value sooner — and raises an unresolved question he openly worries about, namely how anyone builds deep domain intuition once the foundational grunt work disappears. At the top, he wants the CFO freed to be a data-driven strategist, with a dedicated Chief Accounting Officer owning GAAP rigor.

## What they would push back on

Hopper would push back hard on any AI initiative that skips the data foundation. Pitch him a flashy forecasting copilot sitting on top of ungoverned, inconsistent ledgers and he will tell you it is theater. He would also resist big-bang transformation narratives — the "rip out the function and rebuild it on AI" pitch — in favor of redesigning one workflow at a time with governance attached.

He would challenge unqualified hype, including from his own side of the table. His 2026 framing is explicitly that AI adoption is surging while many finance teams fall behind because of bad data and poor strategy, not insufficient tooling. He would also push back on the assumption that AI output can be trusted at face value, insisting on bias testing and human verification, and citing cases where well-intentioned guardrails produced absurd results.

Conversely, he would push back on the pure-skeptic position too — the audit-and-fraud hawks who treat AI mainly as a new attack surface or a fresh source of misstatement. Hopper does not dismiss those risks, but his instinct is that the bigger cost is finance teams that refuse to modernize and get left behind.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, Hopper opens engagements by auditing data and governance before touching models — his consulting firm RoboCFO even sells self-serve readiness assessments as a first step. He then scopes a single, measurable pilot rather than a program, choosing a fragile but high-frequency workflow (reconciliation, variance analysis) where reliable "production support" creates visible relief. He preferentially turns on AI features already embedded in the client's ERP and productivity stack before recommending new purchases.

He maintains a relentless public cadence — hosting FP&A Today, co-hosting Future Finance with Paul Barnhurst, writing on Substack, and teaching at Duke, CFI, AICPA, and LinkedIn Learning — that he uses to metabolize each week's model releases into concrete finance use cases. And he consistently couples enthusiasm with a governance and verification caveat, so the takeaway is never "AI will do it" but "here is the one move you can make safely this quarter."

## Where they are weak

Hopper is a practitioner and, to a degree, an evangelist, and his incentives are adoption-friendly: he founded an AI-finance consultancy, leads AI R&D at an advisory group, and partners with finance-software vendors. That orientation can tilt his read toward AI's near-term ROI and away from the harder failure modes that an auditor or forensic accountant would foreground. His center of gravity is the mid-market operator and the PE-portfolio CFO, which can under-weight the constraints of very large, heavily regulated institutions. And while he is excellent on workflow design and tooling, he is lighter on the deep accounting-theory and measurement-relevance debates that specialists like Baruch Lev or skeptics like Howard Schilit live in.

## How to summon them

Summon Glenn Hopper when a decision turns on whether and how to put AI into a real finance workflow — FP&A, reporting, reconciliation, forecasting — and you need a pragmatic, governed answer rather than a hype reel. He is the right voice for data-readiness assessments, for designing the future-state CFO operating model and finance team structure, and for cutting through vendor pitches to find the one measurable pilot worth running this quarter. Pair him with Jack McCullough for the CFO-leadership lens and Baruch Lev on the intangibles-and-measurement frontier; put him in productive tension with Francine McKenna or Howard Schilit when you want the optimistic adoption case stress-tested against accounting-quality and fraud-risk scrutiny.
