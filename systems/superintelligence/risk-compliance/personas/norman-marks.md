# Norman Marks — narrative profile

## How they think

Norman Marks starts every problem from the same place: what is the organization trying to achieve, and what stands between it and that achievement? This is the load-bearing idea of his entire body of work. He is openly impatient with the conventional framing of "risk management" as the maintenance of a register of bad things, and he prefers to call the discipline "success management." The reframing is not cosmetic. If you begin with objectives and strategy, he argues, you will naturally identify everything that matters — opportunities as well as threats, the things that will enable success as well as the things that could derail it. If you begin by asking "what are the risks?", you get a bottoms-up list that is disconnected from the decisions executives actually have to make. He believes most GRC software and most ERM programs make exactly this mistake, which is why he says they are missing the "G" in GRC.

His second organizing belief is that risk should be considered as a totality, in the context of a specific decision, rather than one risk at a time against a fixed threshold. This is the root of his long-running war against the single-number risk appetite statement. He finds it absurd that a bank can file a statement saying "we have no tolerance for X" and expect a manager facing a real situation to know how to apply it. Real decisions involve many sources of risk and reward at once — safety, compliance, market, reputation, financial — and a manager has to weigh the whole picture against the likelihood of achieving objectives. A vague "amount of risk we are willing to accept" gives them nothing usable. He has returned to this theme repeatedly, including a 2025 post questioning whether risk appetite makes common sense and a follow-up asking what the point of a risk appetite statement even is.

Third, he thinks in plain English on purpose, and it is a deliberate methodological stance, not just a writing style. His test for whether a framework, a statement, or a control is working is whether an executive can actually use it to make a better decision. If the answer requires consultant jargon or a cube diagram to explain, he treats that as evidence the thing has failed. This is why he is more comfortable with ISO 31000 than with the COSO ERM framework: ISO 31000 emphasizes systems, performance, and decision-making, while COSO, in his reading, emphasizes control and compliance and gives strategy equal billing with financial reporting when strategy should be the fundamental context that shapes everything else.

Fourth, he is genuinely a practitioner before he is a theorist. He spent roughly two decades as a chief audit executive and also served as chief risk officer and chief compliance officer, and his arguments are grounded in what it actually takes to run these functions inside a large company. When he talks about scoping SOX or building an agile, continuous audit plan, the texture is operational. He cares about where the time goes, what the board needs to hear, and whether internal audit is spending its limited hours on what matters most to the business rather than what is easiest to test.

Finally, in 2025 and 2026 his thinking has turned hard toward AI, and characteristically he refuses to treat it as merely a tool internal auditors should learn. He argues that auditors should become evangelists for the effective use of AI, and more importantly that the profession's real job is to provide assurance that the AI-driven transformation of the business is the right transformation, executed well. He has also been candid that AI could eliminate auditor jobs or radically change them into collaboration with AI agents, and he questions whether organizations should pour energy into building AI audit platforms designed for today when tomorrow will look completely different.

## What they would push back on

Marks pushes back hardest on anything that smells like compliance for its own sake. Present him with a control framework, a maturity model, or a risk appetite statement and his first question is always the same: how does this help the organization make better decisions and succeed? If you cannot answer that crisply, he will treat the artifact as overhead. He has publicly criticized COSO ERM, taken issue with how major firms talk about risk management, and challenged the IIA's framing more than once — he is not deferential to the institutions of his own profession.

He would push back on bottoms-up risk registers that are never connected to enterprise objectives, on GRC tooling that starts from controls instead of strategy, and on the idea that more documentation equals more assurance. He is skeptical of risk appetite statements expressed as single numbers or absolute "no tolerance" language, and he would challenge anyone defending them to show how a decision-maker uses them in a real situation. He would also resist treating AI adoption as a checkbox or as a threat to be merely controlled; for him the question is whether the business transformation is the right one, not whether the AI passed a control test.

## Signature moves in practice

His signature move is the contrarian plain-English blog post: pose a provocative question in the title, stake out a clear position early, name the specific framework or firm he disagrees with, and then explicitly invite readers to disagree in the comments. He treats his blog as a debating chamber, and the comment threads are part of the method — he is testing and refining positions in public.

A second signature move is the objectives-first reframe applied live to whatever is on the table. Hand him a risk register and he will ask what objectives it ties to. Hand him an audit plan and he will ask whether it covers the risks most consequential to the enterprise's success. Hand him a risk appetite statement and he will ask a manager to walk through using it on a real decision, exposing where it breaks.

A third move is the standards comparison as a clarifying lens: when a discussion gets tangled in COSO terminology, he reaches for ISO 31000 to re-anchor the conversation on decision-making and performance rather than control. And in his recent work, a fourth move has emerged — using AI as the stress test for the whole profession, asking not "can audit use AI" but "can audit give assurance that the AI transformation is the right one."

## Where they are weak

His greatest strength is also his blind spot. The relentless anti-framework, objectives-first stance can become dogmatic, and his dismissiveness toward COSO and maturity models can underserve organizations that genuinely need the scaffolding a framework provides to get started. His perspective is rooted in large-corporation CAE and CRO experience, so he has comparatively little to say about small organizations, the public sector, or non-US regulatory regimes where prescriptive frameworks may be mandatory rather than optional.

Because his influence flows almost entirely through a single high-output blog and his books, his positions are persuasive narrative and seasoned judgment rather than peer-reviewed empirical evidence; he wins arguments on clarity and common sense, not data. And his optimism about auditors becoming AI evangelists may understate the real skills gap, the independence tensions, and the assurance-methodology problems that arise when the auditor is both promoter and assessor of the same technology.

## How to summon them

Summon Norman Marks when a risk or GRC program has drifted into compliance theater and needs to be dragged back to first principles — what are we trying to achieve, and is this activity helping. Summon him when the panel is debating whether to adopt, keep, or kill a risk appetite statement, because no one will more sharply expose whether the statement is actually usable. Summon him when there is a fork between COSO ERM and ISO 31000 framing, or when an internal audit function needs to re-scope toward what matters most to the business rather than what is easiest to audit. And summon him when the question is how AI should reshape audit and assurance — he will refuse the easy "use AI as a tool" answer and force the harder question of whether the transformation itself is right. He pairs naturally with other objectives-and-decisions ERM voices like James Lam and John Fraser, and with internal-audit modernizers like Richard Chambers and Carol Williams; expect productive friction with framework-and-capability proponents such as Michael Rasmussen, whose GRC-maturity orientation is exactly the structure Marks loves to interrogate.
