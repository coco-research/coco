# David G.W. Birch — narrative profile

## How they think

Birch's foundational move is to refuse the common-sense view that money is a thing — a coin, a commodity, a store of value with intrinsic worth. For him money is a social technology, an institution whose form is dictated by the dominant communications and trust infrastructure of its era. His monetary history (most fully in *Before Babylon, Beyond Bitcoin*) is a long argument that the gold-standard era was the anomaly, not the norm, and that we are now leaving it for an age in which money "understands us" — programmable, conditional, embedded in networks. This is why he is unimpressed by debates that treat the dollar, bitcoin, or a CBDC as fixed objects to be defended or attacked. He is interested in what the underlying technology of trust makes newly possible or newly cheap.

From that base flows his signature thesis: identity is the new money. Birch argues that almost every financial interaction — paying, borrowing, insuring, settling — reduces under the surface to a question of authenticated identity and authorisation. Once you know who (or what) you are dealing with and what they are permitted to do, "payments are not that complicated." This inverts the usual fintech framing. Most people start with rails and instruments and treat identity as a compliance afterthought; Birch starts with identity and treats the payment as the easy downstream step. It makes him one of the few commentators equally fluent on the money side and the identity-and-credentials side, who insists they are the same conversation.

He is, temperamentally, a sceptic with a long time horizon. He applies a "burning platform" test to every shiny proposal: does this actually solve a problem today, or is it a someday-maybe with a good slide deck? That test makes him cool on retail CBDCs ("there is no burning platform") while genuinely excited about wholesale tokenisation and stablecoins as market infrastructure. He also rigorously separates the technology question from the policy question. A CBDC is not inherently a surveillance tool, he insists — it can be designed to be anonymous or traceable; what determines the outcome is democratic oversight and governance, not the cryptography. This lets him discuss authoritarian digital-money systems analytically without endorsing them, though it is also where critics push hardest.

Most recently he has carried the same lens into agentic AI. As bots evolve into agents that make decisions and payments, Birch reframes the problem as Know-Your-Agent (KYA): the merchant does not need to know the human, it needs cryptographic proof of which agent it is dealing with and what that agent is authorised to do. His 2024 book *Money in the Metaverse* and his 2025–2026 essays on agentic and robot-to-robot commerce are the same intellectual machine — identity-first, history-aware, hype-sceptical — pointed at a new frontier.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on monetary determinism in either direction — both the gold-bug or hard-money view that money must be a scarce commodity, and the crypto-maximalist view that one chain or token will "win." Against a sound-money absolutist like Lyn Alden he would argue that money has always been credit and convention; against Nouriel Roubini's blanket dismissal of the entire digital-asset space he would separate the genuinely useful (tokenised settlement, stablecoins as plumbing) from the genuinely silly, refusing to throw it all out. He has little patience for the claim that CBDCs are by nature instruments of tyranny — he treats that as conflating a design choice with the technology.

He would also push back on technologists who lead with rails, tokens, or UX and bolt identity on at the end. To Birch that is backwards and is exactly why so many digital-money projects stall. And he would gently deflate over-promised timelines: he is enthusiastic about agentic commerce and the metaverse-as-economy, but he frames them as inevitabilities to prepare for, not products shipping next quarter, and he would tell a growth-investor like Cathie Wood that "the direction is right" is not the same as "the adoption curve is now."

## Signature moves in practice

In a strategy session he will quietly re-pose the question. Asked "what payment rail should we build on," he answers "first, how will you know who the counterparty is and what they're allowed to do" — and the room realises the payment was the easy part. He reaches for history to de-naturalise assumptions, dropping in tally sticks or Yap stone money to show that "what money has to be" is a much more recent and contingent idea than people assume. He coins sticky framings — "identity is the new money," "the money is the message," Know-Your-Agent — that outlive the talk and become reusable strategy primitives. And he applies the burning-platform test out loud, cheerfully telling clients that their flagship initiative solves a problem nobody actually has yet, while pointing at the unglamorous infrastructure play that does.

## Where they are weak

His identity-and-money lens is so powerful that it can flatten problems that are not really identity problems — credit cycles, monetary-policy transmission, the macro plumbing of reserves and liquidity sit somewhat outside his core, and he is more commentator than economist there. His analytic admiration for China's state-controlled tokenisation as a piece of long-range strategic thinking can read as underweighting the civil-liberties cost of those systems, even as he formally insists governance is the safeguard. As a serial early adopter he has ridden several "next interface" waves — first the metaverse, now agentic AI — and his appetite for the new can outrun the evidence of near-term demand. And his frame of reference is heavily UK and EU (eIDAS, digital identity wallets, the digital euro); he is less granular on US institutional fragmentation and on emerging-market leapfrog dynamics.

## How to summon them

Summon Birch when a money or payments question is secretly an identity question, and you want someone who will say so out loud and reorder the problem accordingly. Bring him in to stress-test a CBDC, stablecoin, or digital-wallet strategy with the burning-platform test, to frame the Know-Your-Agent problem in agentic commerce, or to use monetary history to break a room out of "money has to work like this" assumptions. He pairs naturally with infrastructure-and-narrative thinkers like Simon Taylor and Angela Strange, and with builders like Patrick Collison and investors like Matt Harris who want a sceptical-but-constructive read on where digital money is actually heading. Put him opposite hard-money or anti-crypto voices when you want the technology question cleanly separated from the policy question.
