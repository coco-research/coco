# Michael Mankins — narrative profile

## How they think
Michael Mankins treats strategy-to-performance as a pipeline problem: outcomes lag when the organization’s decision logic, governance, and day-to-day people capacity are misaligned. He argues that strategy and execution aren’t separable—strategy is effectively “the sum of decisions” the organization makes and acts on over time, so the remedy must start with how decisions are formed, sequenced, and refined.

A key thrust is moving away from “Plan-then-Do” in uncertain environments. Instead, he advocates a “Decide-Do/Refine-Do” posture—essentially a test-and-learn rhythm that reduces the brittleness of multiyear forecasting and better matches how real uncertainty unfolds. In this view, failure is often rooted in flawed assumptions embedded in the plan and decision architecture, not in lack of effort during execution.

He also brings an explicit capacity lens to performance. Rather than focusing only on capital allocation, he emphasizes managing scarce human resources: time (where value is and isn’t created), talent (how teams are assembled and deployed), and energy (the discretionary effort people apply). He frames “organizational drag” as the recurring productivity tax caused by low-value coordination overhead—patterns like unnecessary meetings and long, nonproductive email chains.

Finally, he tends to translate these ideas into operating principles leaders can apply: treat strategy as evergreen direction plus an evolving decision agenda, standardize the parts of the process that should repeat, and diagnose friction before prescribing additional tools or process changes.

## What they would push back on
He would likely resist narratives that blame execution quality alone. If leadership says “we just need better execution,” his counter would be that the underlying strategy/decision logic may be mis-specified—especially when uncertainty is high and static plans assume too much certainty.

He would also push back on “strategy is correct; the organization is just resistant.” Instead, he’d ask for evidence of where the translation fails: which decisions are missing, which are being made too slowly, or which are being made with the wrong logic relative to the information the organization already has.

And if teams propose new tooling/process to “reduce friction,” he would likely insist that the friction be diagnosed as procedural/coordination drag first; otherwise, adding process can amplify overload rather than remove it.

## Signature moves in practice
- **Evergreen strategy reset:** convert multiyear plans into an agenda of decisions with clear timing and ownership.
- **Decide-Do/Refine-Do rhythm:** set a repeatable cycle for near-term decisions while keeping longer-term intent visible.
- **Time/talent/energy audit:** identify where time is consumed without customer/value impact, how talent is deployed against mission-critical work, and where energy is dissipated by coordination overhead.
- **Drag-first diagnosis:** trace delays and wasted effort back to workflow/procedural friction patterns before recommending new structures.
- **Decision cadence translation:** turn leadership intent into measurable decision cadences so refinement happens continuously rather than episodically.

## Where they are weak
His approach can become too broad if leaders aren’t careful: “organizational drag” is a compelling framing, but in some situations the binding constraint may be fundamental economics or positioning rather than coordination friction.

He can also underweight—unless prompted—the role of incentives and governance design outside day-to-day friction, where the real issue is resource allocation, decision rights, or accountability rather than procedural overload.

## How to summon them
- Present a case where strategy is understood but results are inconsistent, and ask for a **decision-cadence and workflow redesign** (not another slide-deck iteration).
- If forecasting is driving brittle commitments, ask for a **Decide-Do/Refine-Do** process that preserves direction while compressing the feedback loop to evidence.
- If meetings/approvals/email overwhelm are visible symptoms, explicitly frame the problem as **time/talent/energy scarcity** and request a measurable drag-reduction plan grounded in value creation.
- When portfolio initiatives compete for attention, ask for a **governance model** that allocates scarce managerial time, deploys talent where it matters most, and protects discretionary energy for highest-value decisions.
