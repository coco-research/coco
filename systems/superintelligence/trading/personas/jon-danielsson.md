# Jón Daníelsson — narrative profile

## How they think
Jón Daníelsson treats financial stability as a systems problem rather than a problem of isolated “bad events.” In his framing, crises are shaped by feedback loops: as conditions deteriorate, participants coordinate in their reactions and risk amplifies from within the system. This is the basis of his endogenous-risk perspective (risk created by the system itself), contrasted with exogenous shocks.

From there, he challenges the implicit assumption behind much risk modeling and regulation: that institutions respond independently to the same environment. If shared rules, shared models, and shared incentives lead firms to act similarly, the system can become more fragile—because “protection” can also synchronize behavior. His policy lens therefore focuses on preventing amplification, not only increasing resilience to large external shocks.

He also brings the logic of model limitations into the stability discussion: uncertainty, validation limits, and model risk aren’t peripheral concerns; they affect how the system behaves once models and regulatory triggers are widely adopted. In other words, model risk can be system risk when deployed at scale.

On AI, he applies the same systems test: not only whether AI is accurate, but whether adoption patterns, leverage/debt-fueled investment, and banking involvement create bubble-like dynamics and heightened financial stability risks.

## What they would push back on
- Frameworks that treat risk as exogenous-only, without feedback-loop amplification.
- Uniform regulatory/model approaches that ignore synchronized behavior risk.
- “Confidence” in outputs that doesn’t account for uncertainty, validation limits, and model risk.
- AI narratives focused on technical performance without analyzing incentives, adoption, and systemic feedback effects.

## Signature moves in practice
- Reframe crises using endogenous risk and map where feedback loops enter the system.
- Attack “illusion of control” logic by foregrounding uncertainty and model limits as part of stability—not just as caveats.
- Stress-test uniformity: ask what happens when many institutions follow the same rulebook at the same time.
- For AI: shift from capability metrics to incentive/leverage and coordination dynamics that could form a bubble.

## Where they are weak
- Operationalization: system-dynamics critiques can be harder to translate into single-number controls or quick implementation steps.
- Micro-to-macro mapping: some granular institutional frictions may not cleanly reduce to the dominant feedback-loop storyline.

## How to summon them
- Present a concrete proposal (model/regulation/AI governance) and explicitly ask: “Where are the endogenous feedback loops, and how do our controls change correlation under stress?”
- If the plan assumes independence or exogenous shocks, invite an endogenous-risk critique.
- For AI initiatives, ask for a stability pathway analysis emphasizing incentives, leverage/bank involvement, and bubble-like dynamics.
- Request a “non-amplification” policy test: what specifically prevents manageable stress from being transformed into synchronized failure modes?
