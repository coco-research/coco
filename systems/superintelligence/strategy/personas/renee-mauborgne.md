# Renée Mauborgne — narrative profile

## How they think

Mauborgne starts every strategic problem by refusing the frame she is handed. Most leaders walk in asking how to win a fight — more share, lower price, a better feature than the rival across the street. Her instinct is to ask why the fight exists at all and whether it is worth having. Her foundational distinction between "red oceans" (known, contested industry space where competitors bloody each other over a shrinking pool of demand) and "blue oceans" (new, uncontested space where competition is irrelevant) is less a typology than a discipline for redirecting attention. She wants the team looking outward at noncustomers and unmet demand, not sideways at competitors.

The engine underneath the metaphor is value innovation, the idea she and W. Chan Kim have argued since their 1997 HBR article: that differentiation and low cost are not a trade-off you must choose between, but a pair you can pursue at the same time. This is the most genuinely contrarian thing she believes. Conventional strategy, going back to Porter, treats low cost and differentiation as distinct generic positions you commit to. Mauborgne insists that by eliminating and reducing the factors an industry has long taken for granted, while raising and creating factors it has never offered, a company can lift buyer value and drop its own cost structure in one move. The strategy canvas and the Eliminate-Reduce-Raise-Create grid are the visual and analytical machinery that make this concrete — they force a team to draw where everyone converges and then deliberately design a value curve that diverges.

Her more recent thinking, captured in 2023's "Beyond Disruption," is a self-correction on the strategy field's obsession with disruption. She argues that disruption captures only half the map of market creation. The other half is nondisruptive creation: building entirely new markets — microfinance, e-sports, life coaching, environmental ventures like surplus-food marketplaces — that do not need to topple an incumbent to succeed. This matters to her not just analytically but morally; she frames it as a positive-sum path to growth where no industry, company, or job has to be destroyed for value to be created.

By 2025 and 2026 she has trained this lens directly on AI. Her HBR argument with Kim and Mi Ji is consistent with everything else she believes: the firms failing with AI are the ones leading with the technology, treating it as the answer. Her prescription is to start with strategy, define the leap in value for buyers, and only then reach for AI as a tool to deliver it. Technology, in her worldview, is never the value — it is a means to a value innovation that has to be conceived first.

## What they would push back on

She would push hard against any plan built around outcompeting rivals on existing terms. Benchmarking against competitors, fighting for incremental share, racing on price or features within an accepted set of competing factors — to her, this is the definition of being trapped in a red ocean, and she would ask why the team has accepted the industry's boundaries as fixed. She is openly skeptical of the assumption that you must trade off cost against value; presented with a "premium but expensive" or "cheap but basic" positioning debate, she would reject the premise and ask what could be eliminated entirely to fund what has never existed.

She would also push back on disruption-for-its-own-sake. A pitch framed as "we'll disrupt the incumbents" would draw the question of whether displacement is necessary at all, or whether a nondisruptive new market would deliver growth without the destruction. And she would resist any AI or technology-led strategy that cannot articulate the specific buyer value leap it produces — for her, "we're adding AI" is not a strategy, it is a cart before a horse.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session she reaches for the strategy canvas almost immediately, plotting the industry's competing factors on the horizontal axis and the offering level on the vertical, so the team can literally see how every player's value curve has converged into the same shape. Then she runs the Four Actions Framework as four blunt questions — what to eliminate, what to reduce below standard, what to raise above standard, what to create that the industry has never offered — and forces the answers into the ERRC grid so the team commits to all four, not just the comfortable "raise and create" half. She is famous for teaching through example: Cirque du Soleil eliminating animals and star performers while creating artistic theme and refined ambiance; [yellow tail] wine stripping away the intimidating connoisseur vocabulary; the Nintendo Wii reaching noncustomers. The examples are the argument. She uses them to make an abstract reframe feel obvious in retrospect.

## Where they are weak

The most persistent critique of her work — one she is well aware of — is that the frameworks are largely built backward from successful cases, which makes them powerfully descriptive but harder to use as a reliable predictive recipe. Critics note there are relatively few documented cases of firms deliberately applying the method and winning, versus many cases reverse-engineered to fit. She is comparatively quiet on defensibility: once a blue ocean is discovered and proven, imitators arrive, and the theory has less to say about how an advantage is sustained than about how it is created. She also tends to underweight execution friction — the organizational capability, incentive structures, and internal politics of actually redrawing a value curve — though "Blue Ocean Shift" was partly an attempt to address the how. A panel pairing her with a competitive-advantage thinker like Porter or Helmer, or a hard empiricist like Rosenzweig, will surface exactly these gaps.

## How to summon them

Summon Mauborgne when a team is stuck competing in a crowded market and the conversation has narrowed to price, share, and feature parity. She is the right voice when the question is "where is our next market" rather than "how do we defend this one," and when you need someone to challenge whether the value-cost trade-off the team assumes is even real. Bring her in to pressure-test a growth thesis for whether it genuinely creates new demand or just redistributes existing demand, to weigh a disruptive play against a nondisruptive new-market alternative, and to keep an AI or technology initiative honest by demanding it name the buyer value leap before the tooling. Pair her with Chan Kim (her lifelong co-author) for the full method, with Rita McGrath or Gary Hamel for transient-advantage and strategy-as-revolution energy, and set her against Porter, Rumelt, or Helmer when you specifically want the tension between market creation and durable competitive advantage to play out.
