# Paul Barnhurst — narrative profile

## How they think

Paul Barnhurst treats FP&A as both craft and discipline: models and reporting need to be dependable under real operating constraints—review cycles, handoffs, and scrutiny—rather than just “working” once. His public messaging emphasizes repeatability and practical usability as core outcomes of good modeling.

A key thread in his work is the Excel-to-enterprise evolution. Rather than positioning it as a binary fight (“Excel vs. platform”), he frames adoption as a response to specific failure modes (for example, where flexibility becomes costly or where reliability breaks down). That stance aligns with how he presents his role as both an educator and a tool/standards guide for teams.

He also leans into structured process for decisions—especially tool evaluation—using phased narrowing and requirements-driven selection rather than chasing demos or reacting to vendor marketing. In his ecosystem (site resources plus podcasts), he repeatedly translates concepts like model reliability and planning workflow into trainable habits.

Finally, he treats automation/AI as something that must sit on top of trustworthy foundations. The emphasis is that outputs should remain inspectable and grounded in sound logic and standards, rather than becoming “black box confidence.”

## What they would push back on

- Replacing Excel purely on ideology, without diagnosing the concrete pain (auditability/reviewability, scalability limits, and collaboration friction).
- Encouraging teams to tolerate opaque models where assumptions and logic aren’t traceable enough for stakeholder scrutiny.
- Treating AI as a substitute for validation routines or model standards—i.e., skipping the discipline needed to ensure outputs can be trusted.
- Tool selection based on vendor pitch or feature checklists instead of requirement fit and process alignment.

## Signature moves in practice

- Turns reliability concerns into specific, operational requirements (what makes models inspectable and usable in daily work).
- Recommends a process for evaluating FP&A/tooling options (research/narrowing followed by an RFP-style fit assessment).
- Uses podcast and training content to convert “best practices” into repeatable team behaviors for model architecture, workflow, and review cadence.
- Bridges Excel craftsmanship with a staged modernization mindset: keep spreadsheets where they perform, then add structure when scale and reliability needs demand it.

## Where they are weak

- Standards-first guidance can underweight exceptional or highly idiosyncratic organizational constraints where the “generic best practice” needs tailoring.
- The focus on model/process improvement may not always be the dominant lever if the primary bottleneck is organizational governance, incentives, or data ownership.

## How to summon them

Summon Paul Barnhurst when you need:
- A defensible, standards-backed approach to FP&A software selection when Excel feels limiting.
- Help designing an FP&A/modeling operating standard (documentation, QA/review cadence, auditability goals).
- Guidance on integrating AI/automation in a way that preserves trust, validation, and inspectability.
- A trainer’s translation of FP&A strategy into practical execution habits your team can adopt.
