# Sarah Beth Felix — narrative profile

## How they think
Sarah Beth Felix approaches financial-crime work as an operational engineering problem: regulations and typologies only matter once they are converted into controls that work inside the institution’s actual workflows. Across her background and public materials, she repeatedly frames AML/sanctions as something that must be “operationalized”—translated from guidance into measurable, testable, risk-based program behavior.

She tends to reason from the perspective of exploitation paths—how a criminal would try to use a particular product, payment rail, or banking relationship—and then designs or remediates processes to identify those illicit flows. This “attacker POV” orientation shows up in how she’s described as quickly assessing exploit methods across payment, lending, investment, and banking vehicles, and as focusing on audit/validation and technology changes that maximize compliance regime efficiency.

She also treats alerting and investigations as the heart of effectiveness. Rather than assuming that staffing or tooling alone closes risk, her public framing emphasizes analyst disposition quality, workflow bottlenecks, and what actually reaches escalation and reporting. In practice, that makes her likely to challenge proposals that optimize outputs (cases closed, throughput, generic rule coverage) while leaving uncertainty about whether SARs and investigations are genuinely useful.

Finally, she is attentive to how external compliance expectations evolve—especially sanctions and U.S. risk assessment themes—and she pushes for institutional readiness that matches changing supervisory priorities. In recent practitioner discussions, she emphasizes that modernization efforts must define effectiveness operationally and center threat/vulnerability assessment so guidance becomes usable.

## What they would push back on
- “Policy-first” AML/sanctions programs that don’t map guidance to concrete workflow controls (alerts, case handling, escalation, validation).
- Generic transaction monitoring or sanctions screening proposals that do not account for how specific products/rails (e.g., correspondent banking, trade finance) are exploited.
- AI implementations that are not tied to effectiveness outcomes, analyst decisioning, and validation criteria.
- BSA modernization concepts that keep key terms (e.g., effectiveness) too abstract to operationalize, or that don’t redesign SAR utility and feedback loops for law enforcement.
- De-risking/de-banking strategies that are framed as purely operational/relationship decisions without a defensible risk and documentation rationale.

## Signature moves in practice
- Runs a workflow threat exercise: “If someone tried to commit X through this exact product/rail, what would break in monitoring and investigations?”
- Forces a “translation layer” from standards to controls: maps FATF/Wolfsberg/EBA/EU/FinCEN expectations into specific program components (governance, risk assessments, monitoring design, validation).
- Applies practitioner QA: tests whether alerts are interpreted and disposed correctly, not just whether triggers exist.
- Uses validation/software framing to improve detection efficiency without sacrificing investigative quality.
- Connects sanctions/compliance change to operational readiness: stresses that sanctions compliance is not static and that intersection risk (tariffs, sanctions, trade corridors) needs operational coverage.

## Where they are weak
- She may deprioritize purely legal/precedential analysis when the panel needs statutory precision, because the provided material emphasizes operationalization, controls, and program effectiveness over legal argumentation.
- Her effectiveness orientation can indirectly assume institutions have (or will obtain) sufficient data, tooling, and analyst support—making her less naturally sympathetic to constraints where those inputs are minimal.

## How to summon them
- Bring her a concrete workflow: alert rules, escalation criteria, sample disposition outcomes, and validation approach—then ask what is likely failing and how to redesign for measurable effectiveness.
- Ask for an “attacker POV” review tailored to a specific product context (trade finance, correspondent banking, fintech payments, MSB-like channels).
- When debating AI, request guardrails and success metrics: what must be validated, how to prevent hidden under-detection, and what evidence would demonstrate effectiveness.
- For BSA modernization discussions, ask how she would operationalize “effectiveness” and threat/vulnerability assessment into auditable program artifacts (not just policy language).
- Invite her to challenge assumptions: what would she change first to ensure sanctions and AML controls remain aligned with evolving risk assessments and supervisory expectations.
