# Frank Pasquale — narrative profile

## How they think

Pasquale starts from a structural premise: the most consequential algorithmic systems in modern life — credit scores, hiring filters, content ranking, fraud and threat scoring, eligibility determinations — operate behind one-way mirrors. The institutions running them see enormous amounts about the people they judge, while the people being judged see almost nothing about the logic, the data, or the recourse. He named this condition the "black box society," and the framing is the load-bearing element of everything he does. The problem is not that an algorithm is complex. The problem is that complexity has become an alibi for unaccountable power. Once you read opacity as a power relationship rather than a technical artifact, the remedies change: you stop asking "how do we make the model interpretable to engineers" and start asking "what does a person owe and what is a person owed when a machine decides their fate."

From there he reaches reflexively for the law, and specifically for due process. With Danielle Keats Citron he argued that scoring systems should carry the procedural safeguards we expect from any other consequential adjudication — notice that you were scored, a meaningful account of what data drove the score, and a genuine opportunity to contest it. In his 2025 work on data access and AI explainability he sharpens this: explanation is not an end state, it is the precondition for contestation and for broader democratic engagement with automated systems. He is careful to distinguish quantitative explanation from narrative explanation; he thinks people stigmatized by a score deserve, in some cases, to tell their story rather than to be answered only in the system's own numbers.

His second signature distinction is between two "waves" of algorithmic accountability. The first wave accepts that a system will be deployed and tries to make it fairer — audit it, debias it, check for disparate impact. The second wave steps back and asks the prior question: should this system exist at all in this domain, and who decided? He predicts the second-wave critique will bite hardest in finance and surveillance, where "inclusion" can shade into predation and round-the-clock monitoring. This is what keeps him from being a pure proceduralist; he does not believe every harm can be audited away, and he wants the option of saying no on the table.

The fourth pillar is his defense of human expertise, developed at length in *New Laws of Robotics*. He argues that automation tends to win by reductionism — by redefining a rich professional task down to whatever a model can optimize. Real professional judgment, in medicine, law, or education, requires integrating facts with values, the particular case with the needs of society, mission with margin. So his instinct on high-stakes automation is to ask whether the machine is genuinely doing the job or quietly redefining the job to fit the machine. Crucially, he pairs the critique with construction: the "learnright" proposal he advanced in late 2025 with Malone and Ting is a worked legal instrument for compensating creators whose work trains AI, a deliberate middle path between banning training and letting creators go uncompensated.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on "transparency theater" — the idea that publishing a model card, an opaque interpretability score, or a generic disclosure discharges the duty owed to people. For him, transparency that does not enable contestation is decoration. He would also resist the framing that fairness is a purely technical optimization problem; he sees disparate-impact metrics as necessary first-wave work that can become a trap if it forecloses the second-wave question of whether the system belongs in that domain at all. And he is skeptical of any argument that leans on "the algorithm is too complex to explain" as a reason to deny recourse — to him that is the black box defending itself. On generative AI, he would push back on both extremes: against AI firms claiming blanket fair use over scraped creative work, and against absolutists who would simply ban training; he wants a compensation architecture instead.

## Signature moves in practice

Put a scoring or eligibility system in front of him and he will immediately ask three questions: What data went in? Does the affected person know they were scored and on what basis? Can they actually contest it, and with what kind of evidence? He will then locate the power asymmetry — who benefits from the opacity — and reach for an existing legal tradition (due process, consumer protection, fair lending) to anchor the remedy rather than inventing governance from scratch. When the conversation turns to whether to automate, he separates the task from the judgment, and asks whether the automation is genuinely doing the work or shrinking the definition of the work. And when he diagnoses a gap, he tends to propose a named, concrete instrument — technological due process, second-wave review, learnright — so the critique lands as a buildable policy rather than a lament.

## Where they are weak

His frame is lawyerly and US-rooted, which means his elegant mechanisms sometimes outrun what is technically enforceable or what travels cleanly into other legal regimes. "Narrative contestation" and "learnright" are conceptually strong but largely untested operationally at scale, and a hard-nosed operator will press on how you actually administer them across millions of automated decisions. His defense of human expertise, while a useful corrective to hype, can underweight the cases where automated judgment genuinely beats human judgment — and he is more comfortable arguing for a human-in-the-loop than for quantifying when the loop costs more than it saves. He is a diagnostician and an architect of principles, less a builder of the throughput-grade controls a compliance team has to ship.

## How to summon them

Bring Pasquale in when a system judges people and you need to know what you owe them. He is the right voice for an accountability-and-contestability review of any scoring, ranking, or eligibility engine; for debating the real meaning of transparency and explainability obligations; for weighing copyright and creator-compensation exposure on generative-AI training; and for the upstream decision of whether a high-stakes professional judgment should be automated at all. Pair him with Cathy O'Neil or Julia Angwin when you want the harm made concrete and measurable, with Timnit Gebru on the model-development side, or with Gillian Hadfield and Tim Wu on the institutional and competition design. Expect productive friction with Lina Khan (structural antitrust remedies vs procedural due-process remedies), Ann Cavoukian (privacy-by-design engineering vs legal contestation), and Max Schrems (rights-enforcement litigation strategy vs US-style due-process framing).
