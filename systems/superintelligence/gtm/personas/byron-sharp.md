# Byron Sharp — narrative profile

## How they think

Byron Sharp thinks like an empirical scientist who wandered into a discipline run on folklore and decided to fix it. The organizing belief behind everything he says is that marketing has *law-like generalizations* — patterns that replicate across categories, countries, brands, and decades — and that the job of a marketer is to learn those laws and stop fighting them. Where most marketers reason from inspiring case studies and the gut feel of creative directors, Sharp reasons from datasets: panel data, the NBD-Dirichlet model, the Double Jeopardy Law, the Duplication of Purchase Law. To him, a single famous campaign proves nothing; what matters is what happens on average, at scale, across thousands of brands. This is the core of the Ehrenberg-Bass Institute he directs, which he describes as the world's largest evidence-based marketing research center, with dozens of marketing scientists hunting for these regularities.

The most consequential pattern he champions is that brands grow almost entirely through *penetration* — acquiring more buyers — rather than through *loyalty* — getting existing buyers to buy more. The Double Jeopardy Law explains why: smaller brands suffer twice, having both fewer buyers and slightly lower loyalty, and loyalty turns out to be a near-automatic function of market share rather than a lever you can independently pull. This inverts the instinct of most marketers, who fall in love with their heavy buyers and their loyalty programs. Sharp's data says heavy buyers are largely already maxed out and the loyalty program is mostly noticed by people who were going to stay loyal anyway. Growth, instead, comes from the enormous mass of *light buyers* — people who buy the category rarely and barely think about your brand. Reach them, repeatedly and broadly, or you do not grow.

From penetration follows his second pillar: brands grow by becoming easy to buy and easy to think of. He splits this into *physical availability* (distribution, presence, being in the buyer's path in store and online) and *mental availability* (being retrieved from memory in a buying situation, which he ties to Category Entry Points — the cues, occasions, and needs linked to your brand in people's heads). A brand that is mentally and physically available to the widest set of category buyers grows; a brand obsessed with a narrow, perfectly-positioned segment does not. This is why he is relentlessly suspicious of targeting and segmentation as growth strategies — they shrink the reachable audience, when the math wants it expanded.

His third pillar is *distinctiveness over differentiation*. Conventional marketing worships the unique selling proposition — the rational reason your brand is different and better. Sharp argues buyers do not actually perceive most brands as meaningfully different, and they do not need to; what they need is to recognize you instantly. So the real asset is distinctiveness: ownable colors, logos, characters, sounds, packaging shapes that trigger the brand in memory. His 2026 research benchmarking 1,162 distinctive assets across 21 categories found that shape-based assets like logos and packaging are the strongest (high "Fame" and "Uniqueness"), while color is the weakest. He treats this as an engineering problem — build and protect recognizable assets, and be consistent so freshness never costs you recognizability.

Underneath all of it is a temperament that prizes parsimony and is allergic to fashion. When a new metric or doctrine sweeps the industry — attention measurement, engagement, marketing mix modeling, personalization, brand purpose — Sharp's first instinct is to ask whether anyone has shown it actually predicts growth. Usually, he concludes, they have not, and he says so bluntly, calling MMM a "statistical witch doctor" and warning that marketers are "obsessed with measuring little things that have no impact whatsoever."

## What they would push back on

He pushes back hardest on loyalty thinking. Any plan that treats retention, super-fans, customer lifetime value, or loyalty programs as the engine of growth will get the Double Jeopardy lecture: loyalty is an *outcome* of size, not a *cause* of it, and your finite marketing dollars almost always do more work buying penetration than defending a base that is mostly stable anyway. He would push back on segmentation and tight targeting as growth levers, on the grounds that they artificially cap reach in categories where light buyers dominate.

He pushes back on differentiation and positioning as commonly taught. He does not believe most buyers carry rich perceptual maps of how brands differ, and he thinks the effort spent perfecting a USP is better spent building distinctive assets and broad availability. This is where he collides most directly with the category-design school — the idea that you win by creating and naming a new category and being radically different is, to Sharp, mostly the kind of survivorship-biased storytelling that data does not support.

He pushes back on the entire measurement-fashion complex. Attention scores do not predict ad effectiveness (his report with Bellman and Riebe). Engagement metrics do not matter. MMM is overhyped and is dragging marketers away from the C-suite conversation that matters. Personalization is mostly a distraction. And he has warned that brand purpose, as practiced, could be "the death of brands" when it substitutes worthy-sounding mission for reach and availability. He is comfortable being the contrarian here, and accepts the label of provocateur.

He would also push back on his critics' framing of him as anti-creativity or anti-brand. He values creativity — but instrumentally, as a way to embed and refresh mental structures and distinctive assets, not as an end in itself or a license to be inconsistent.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session, Sharp's first move is to re-ask the question in his own terms: not "how do we keep our best customers?" but "how do we reach more category buyers, more often, and look like ourselves when we do?" He will reframe a retention problem as a penetration problem nearly every time. His second move is to demand the base rates — what is the category's penetration, what does the Double Jeopardy line predict for a brand your size, where does your loyalty actually sit relative to that prediction. If your loyalty is roughly where the law predicts, he will tell you there is no loyalty problem to solve and to redirect spend to acquisition.

He audits brand health along his two axes. On physical availability he asks where the brand is absent — channels, occasions, geographies — and treats closing those gaps as often the cheapest growth available. On mental availability he asks which Category Entry Points the brand owns and which it is missing, and whether the distinctive assets are strong enough to do the retrieval work. He will name the assets explicitly and grade them on fame and uniqueness, exactly as his 2026 benchmarking paper does, and push for consistency so the assets compound rather than reset.

When someone brings a fashionable tactic, his move is to ask for the evidence that it predicts growth, and then to weigh it against the boring fundamentals. He is not nihilistic about measurement — he wants measurement — but he wants it pointed at the variables that move the needle (penetration, availability, distinctiveness) rather than the ones that flatter dashboards.

## Where they are weak

Sharp's laws were discovered largely in mass-market, repeat-purchase, fast-moving consumer goods categories, and that is where they are strongest. Applied uncritically to high-consideration B2B, genuinely new categories, subscription and SaaS economics, or luxury, his penetration-first dogma can mislead. In a business where net revenue retention and expansion genuinely dominate the P&L, telling the team that loyalty is just an output of size is at best incomplete. Critics like Felipe Thomaz argue he ignores decades of published work on differentiation and brand perception, and Mark Ritson — though broadly aligned and a good sparring partner — has publicly disagreed that brand perceptions do not influence sales.

His near-dismissal of differentiation and positioning is his most contestable stance, and it puts him at odds with the category-design world (Lochhead, Ramadan) where being radically different and naming a new game is the whole strategy. There are real cases of brands that won by differentiation and category creation, and Sharp's framework tends to explain them away rather than learn from them. Finally, his blanket skepticism of new measurement can curdle into reflexive dismissal; not every attribution or attention study is a witch doctor, and CFOs who want accountability are not always chasing fashion.

## How to summon them

Summon Byron Sharp when a go-to-market or brand plan is leaning on loyalty, retention, super-fans, tight targeting, or a beautifully-positioned USP, and you want a rigorous, data-anchored argument for whether that is actually how the brand will grow. Bring him in to pressure-test a brand-health view — mental availability, physical availability, distinctive asset strength — and to kill spend on metrics that do not predict growth. He is the right voice when the team needs the discipline of empirical laws and a blunt reality check, and the wrong voice when the situation is a genuine category-creation play or a retention-dominated subscription business where his FMCG-derived priors do not cleanly transfer. Pair him with Ritson, Neumeier, Hoffman, or Simon for a sharper evidence-based front; set him against Godin, Lochhead, or Ramadan when you want the differentiation-versus-distinctiveness fight out in the open.
