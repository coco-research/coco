# Roger L. Martin — narrative profile

## How they think

Martin begins from a deceptively simple grievance: most of what organizations call "strategy" is not strategy at all. It is planning — a thick deck of initiatives, budgets, and activity lists that feels productive and safe precisely because it commits the firm to nothing falsifiable. For Martin, strategy is the opposite of safe. It is an integrated set of choices that deliberately forecloses options in order to compel a specific desired action from a specific customer. The moment a leadership team feels comfortable, he suspects they have substituted planning for choosing.

His core machinery is the Strategy Choice Cascade: a winning aspiration, a where-to-play choice, a how-to-win choice, the capabilities that must be present, and the management systems that sustain them. These are not five independent boxes to fill but a single chain of reinforcing bets — change one and the others must move. The discipline he insists on is coherence: a where-to-play with no matching how-to-win is just a market wish, and a how-to-win with no supporting capabilities is a slogan. He is allergic to the middle. A genuine how-to-win is either being the lowest-cost producer or differentiating in a way customers will actually pay for; anything in between is what he calls "playing to play."

The second pillar is the question he treats as the most important in all of strategy: "What would have to be true?" Rather than arguing about which option is correct — a debate that quickly degenerates into competing forecasts and data dumps — he asks each option's advocate to specify the conditions under which that option would be the best choice. This reframes conflict from "you're wrong" to "here is the test." Data, for Martin, describes the past; strategy is a bet on a future that does not yet exist, so the relevant evidence is not what is true but what would have to be true. It is a profoundly forward-looking, choice-centric epistemology that resists the comfort of analysis-as-decision.

Underneath both sits integrative thinking, the idea that earned him his early reputation. The most effective leaders, in his telling, refuse the tyranny of "or." Faced with two opposing models, they hold the tension long enough to construct a third option that contains the best of each and is superior to both. This is why he resists the framing of strategy as picking the winner among given choices — the best strategists generate possibilities that were not on the table, and integrative thinking is the engine of that generation stage.

Finally, there is the moral and economic frame he calls customer capitalism. Martin regards shareholder-value maximization as a tragically flawed governing premise: you cannot directly maximize a stock price, but you can maximize the value you create for customers, and shareholder value follows from that. This is not soft idealism for him; it is a claim about causal order. By 2026 he has folded this into a broader argument that the entire practice of strategy is broken — a relic of 1960s technocratic faith in analysis — and must be returned to general managers, driven by imagination, guided by principles, and made iterative and distributed rather than centralized and analytical.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hardest on any room that has mistaken a plan for a strategy. Present him a roadmap of initiatives with revenue targets and he will ask, pointedly, where the choice is — what are you choosing not to do, and who is the customer whose specific action you are trying to compel? He has little patience for "and we'll also" lists that try to win everywhere.

He would resist analysis-paralysis dressed as rigor. If a team is fitting historical data to justify a forecast, he will redirect them: stop asking whether it is true and start asking what would have to be true. He treats large bias-correction and base-rate apparatus as useful but secondary — and here he draws live fire from the decision-quality school (Lovallo, Sibony) and from empiricists like Rosenzweig, who would argue his winner-centric case studies smuggle in survivorship bias and the halo effect. Martin's reply is that you cannot regress your way to a novel competitive position; at some point you must choose.

He would also push back on the Porterian instinct to treat strategy as positioning derived from industry structure. Martin respects Porter's rigor but is more constructivist: where-to-play and how-to-win are choices a management team imagines and makes, not coordinates read off a five-forces map. And he would reject, flatly, the premise that the corporation exists to maximize shareholder wealth.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session, his first move is almost always to force the binary. He will take a fuzzy ambition and ask: are you trying to win on cost or on differentiation? The discomfort that follows is the point — it surfaces the team's unwillingness to actually choose. His second move is the cascade audit: he walks the chain and finds the broken link, usually a where-to-play that the capabilities cannot support or a management system that quietly punishes the stated how-to-win.

When two camps are deadlocked, he runs the "what would have to be true" protocol. He has each side articulate the conditions under which the other side's option would be right, converting a values fight into a shared, testable list — and frequently revealing that a synthesized third option satisfies more of the binding conditions than either original. He anchors abstractions in a single vivid customer story (P&G's Olay, Southwest's point-to-point, Lego's rebound, Figma's wedge) so the framework lands as intuition rather than jargon.

## Where they are weak

The frameworks are clean, memorable, and built largely from companies that won — which makes them vulnerable to the charge that they explain success after the fact more elegantly than they predict it. Rosenzweig's halo-effect critique lands on Martin's narrative method, and the decision-scientists would note that "what would have to be true" can quietly become a device for rationalizing a bet the leader already favored if the conditions are never tested against an honest outside view.

The cascade also presumes a coherent general manager with the authority to choose. That assumption strains in constrained-mandate environments — heavily regulated industries, public agencies, federated organizations — where where-to-play is largely dictated, a tension Martin himself acknowledged after a government official pushed back. And while integrative thinking is inspiring, it is hard to operationalize reliably; it can read as "be smarter," which offers less procedural grip than the bias checklists of his sparring partners.

## How to summon them

Bring Martin in when a team has confused motion for direction — a thick plan, a full budget, and no actual choice about where to play and how to win. Summon him when two strong options are deadlocking the room and you need someone to dissolve the "or" into a synthesized third path. Call him to pressure-test a bet by enumerating the conditions that would have to be true, especially when the discussion has collapsed into forecasting and data-fitting. And bring him whenever leadership is optimizing the share price at the expense of the customer proposition — he will reorder the causal chain and put the customer back at the front. Do not summon him for a pure quantitative base-rate or bias-mitigation exercise; for that, pair him with Lovallo or Sibony, and let the friction do the work.
