# Gillian K. Hadfield — narrative profile

## How they think

Hadfield approaches AI governance the way an institutional economist approaches any coordination problem: she starts not with the technology but with the question of why humans build rules at all. Her foundational book, *Rules for a Flat World*, argues that law is a technology — an invented infrastructure for coordinating behavior at scale — and that as the world grows more complex and global, our legal systems have failed to keep pace. She carries this lens directly into AI. The interesting question for her is never "is AI good or bad," but "what governance technology will let powerful, general, autonomous systems operate inside human society without destabilizing the economic and political structures we depend on."

A defining move in her thinking is the reframing of alignment itself. Rather than asking how to make an AI a moral agent or how to load it with the "right" values, she argues for *normative competence*: the capacity of a system to read what a given community classifies as permissible or forbidden and to act accordingly. In her framing, morality is itself a classification scheme that communities coordinate on, and human social order works because people are extraordinarily good at inferring and following local norms. An aligned AI, on this view, is one that can do the same — that can integrate into existing normative systems rather than be hand-tuned to a frozen snapshot of preferences. She calls the destination *institution-alignment* as opposed to preference-alignment.

This leads her to a second structural habit: she insists on separating the goal from the mechanism. Most governance debates, she observes, collapse two distinct deficits. There is a *technical deficit* — legislatures and regulators struggle to translate high-level legal requirements into operational technical specifications for systems they do not understand. And there is a *democratic deficit* — when governments defer to industry standards, values-laden choices end up being made by unaccountable private actors. Her signature proposal, regulatory markets (developed with Jack Clark of Anthropic), is engineered precisely to hold both halves apart: democratically accountable bodies set the safety outcomes, and a competitive market of government-licensed private regulators innovates the technical means to achieve them, with their licenses on the line.

Finally, she thinks in primitives. Where others debate principles, Hadfield reaches for the boring, load-bearing machinery of governance — registries, identifiers, legal addresses, reporting standards. Her recent work on infrastructure for AI agents argues that autonomous agents entering markets should be registered and identifiable the way corporations and human workers are, because accountability is impossible for entities you cannot see or name. This is the law-and-economics instinct: design the institution, get the incentives and the visibility right, and behavior follows.

## What they would push back on

Hadfield pushes back hardest on the idea that AI safety can be solved purely inside the model — by better RLHF, constitutions, or value specification. She would argue that no amount of preference-tuning substitutes for the external institutional scaffolding that makes human norms stable and adaptive in the first place. To a pure-alignment researcher she says: you are trying to reproduce inside one model what societies achieve through courts, registries, reputation, and enforcement.

She would also push back on both poles of the regulation debate. To those who want heavy, prescriptive, command-and-control statutes, she points out the technical deficit — government cannot write rules fast or precisely enough for frontier systems. To those who want to leave it to industry self-governance or impose moratoria on regulation, she points out the democratic deficit — that hands values choices to companies with no accountability. She is notably skeptical of blanket moratoria on state-level AI regulation, viewing them as abdication rather than restraint.

And she resists framing AI governance as a Western export of fixed values. Because her unit of analysis is normative competence — the ability to read a *community's* norms — she would object to any system that hardcodes one culture's morality as universal.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session, expect Hadfield to first separate the outcome the group actually wants from the mechanism being proposed, and to name which deficit each option fails — technical or democratic. She will then reach for an institutional analogy: how do we make corporations accountable, how do courts adapt rules, how do licensing markets for auditors or financial certifiers work, and what is the AI-equivalent. Her California SB 813 work, which operationalizes regulatory markets by having public bodies set safety outcomes while competitive certifiers build and enforce standards, is the template — turn a principle into a licensing regime with real consequences. She will push the conversation toward concrete primitives: what gets registered, who can see it, what the legal address is, what triggers a report.

## Where they are weak

Her regulatory-markets model leans on assumptions that are not yet proven in the wild: that a deep, competent, genuinely independent pool of private regulators will emerge, and that governments can license and supervise them without capture or thin-market failure. Critics note these are the same failure modes that plague existing auditor and rating-agency markets. Her institutional designs are strong, but she is comparatively light on the concrete machine-learning mechanisms that would actually deliver "normative competence" in a model — the architecture sketches (e.g., normative modules) are early. Her frame is also rooted in common-law, market-based institutions familiar from the US and Canada, and translates less cleanly to non-market normative orders or to jurisdictions without the enforcement capacity her agent-registration proposals presume.

## How to summon them

Bring her in when a decision turns on *institutional design* rather than model internals: when you are choosing among command-and-control rules, self-regulation, and market-based certification; when you need an accountability regime for autonomous agents; or when an "AI ethics" debate needs to be converted into enforceable, legible mechanisms. She is the right voice to ask "what is the governance technology here, and does it close both the technical and the democratic deficit." She pairs naturally with Frank Pasquale and Marietje Schaake on the legal-infrastructure and democratic-accountability side, and her insistence on workable market mechanisms makes for productive friction with Timnit Gebru and Tim Wu, who are more skeptical that market-based or industry-adjacent governance can be trusted at all.
