# Dan Lovallo — narrative profile

## How they think

Dan Lovallo thinks like a cognitive scientist who wandered into the boardroom and decided that the most expensive errors companies make are not failures of analysis but failures of mind. His intellectual lineage runs straight through Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky: from his 1993 *Management Science* paper "Timid Choices and Bold Forecasts" with Kahneman onward, his career has been a sustained argument that human judgment under uncertainty is predictably distorted — optimistic, overconfident, narrowly framed — and that those distortions show up at industrial scale inside corporations. Where a classical strategist asks "what is the right move?", Lovallo asks a prior question: "given how human beings actually forecast, how much should we trust the number we just produced?"

The organizing idea is the distinction between the **inside view** and the **outside view**. The inside view builds a forecast from the specifics of the plan in front of you — your team, your assumptions, your roadmap — and it is reliably too optimistic because it ignores the base rates of how similar efforts actually turned out. The outside view, operationalized as **reference-class forecasting**, throws away the seductive internal narrative and instead asks: what is the distribution of outcomes for the class of projects this one belongs to, and where on that distribution should we honestly place ourselves? Kahneman's famous curriculum-design anecdote — where insiders estimated 18–30 months while the base rate was seven to ten years — is the canonical illustration Lovallo returns to. This is his deepest move: convert a confident point estimate into a sobering distribution.

A second pillar is that **the decision process matters more than the analysis**. In his and Olivier Sibony's study of over a thousand major business decisions, the quality of the process was roughly six times more predictive of good outcomes than the thoroughness of the underlying analysis. That finding reorganizes his entire practical agenda: instead of demanding more data, he designs the *architecture* of how decisions get made — pre-mortems, structured dissent, debiasing checklists, the deliberate generation of genuine alternatives rather than a single momentum case dressed up with a strawman. He is one of the rare scholars who treats "how to decide" as a more tractable lever than "what to decide."

Third, and increasingly central in his recent work, is that the cognitive story is not only about bias. Lovallo distinguishes the "dark side" of cognition — heuristics and biases that degrade choices — from a "bright side": the generative cognitive acts (imitation, framing, analogical reasoning, abductive reasoning, mental simulation) through which good strategic options get *created* in the first place. His 2026 *Strategic Management Journal* review with Matteo Cristofaro frames behavioral strategy as a coevolving, socially situated system and explicitly puts AI, heuristics, and nudges on the research agenda — signaling that he sees the next frontier as how machines and humans jointly generate and evaluate options.

Underneath it all is a temperament: empirical, calm, and skeptical of conviction-as-evidence. He trusts distributions over stories, processes over heroes, and base rates over enthusiasm.

## What they would push back on

Lovallo pushes back hardest on **confident forecasts that have no reference class**. Present him a hockey-stick projection or a "this time is different" narrative and his first question is, "Compared to what?" He distrusts the inside view almost reflexively, and he will treat unexamined optimism as a defect to be corrected, not a virtue to be celebrated.

He pushes back on **narrow framing of risk**. The argument of "Your Company Is Too Risk-Averse" (with Koller, Uhlaner, and Kahneman) is that managers evaluate each project's downside in isolation and, because their careers ride on every bet, kill positive-expected-value projects that the corporation could easily absorb in aggregate. He would challenge any leader who reflexively prefers "safe" incremental moves and cost-cutting over a diversified portfolio of bolder bets.

He pushes back on **decision theater** — the appearance of rigor without the substance. A meeting that surfaces one option and a token alternative, an analysis that confirms what the sponsor already wanted, a forecast produced entirely from the inside: these draw his fire. He would also resist the idea that intuition is reliable in unstructured, low-validity environments; he has co-argued (with Kahneman, Klein, and Sibony) about precisely when gut instinct can and cannot be trusted, and his answer is "less often than executives believe."

What he is *less* inclined to push back on, and where a competitive-advantage hawk would prod him, is the content of strategy itself — moats, scarcity, positioning. He cares more that you forecast and decide well than that you have picked a structurally advantaged game.

## Signature moves in practice

The reference-class forecast is the move he is known for: define the class of comparable initiatives, gather their actual outcome distribution, and place the current project on it before trusting any internally generated number. The pre-mortem is its prospective cousin — assemble the team, assume the project has already failed two years out, and have everyone independently write down why; this licenses dissent that hierarchy normally suppresses. The portfolio reframe takes a project that looks too risky in isolation and re-evaluates it against the corporate book of bets, exposing where natural diversification makes the risk acceptable. The debiasing checklist — the twelve questions of "Before You Make That Big Decision" — operationalizes all of this for a recommending team and the executives who must ratify their work. And the option-generation discipline forces a real set of alternatives into the room, countering the single-momentum-case pathology. Across all of them, the meta-move is the same: he intervenes on the *process and the framing* rather than arguing the substance, trusting that a better-architected decision will find the better answer.

## Where they are weak

Lovallo is a diagnostician and a process designer, not a vision-caster. His toolkit is superb at preventing predictable failures but quieter on what actually wins — durable competitive advantage, the asymmetric founder bet, the moat that makes a forecast moot. A Rumelt would tell him that good debiasing of a strategically empty plan still yields an empty plan; a Helmer would note that no pre-mortem manufactures a power.

The reference-class method has a real boundary condition: it presumes a usable distribution of analogues exists. For genuinely novel, first-of-kind, or category-creating moves, the "right" reference class is contestable, and choosing the wrong one can anchor a team on misleading base rates. Pushed mechanically, the outside view can over-correct into timidity — the very risk-aversion he elsewhere decries — by treating every bold bet as just another draw from a discouraging historical distribution.

His process rituals also carry an organizational cost. Pre-mortems, structured dissent, and checklists can slow decision-making or calcify into box-ticking if leaders adopt the form without the intent. And because his instinct is to translate conflict into a forecasting or process question, he can under-weight the moments when strategy genuinely requires conviction and speed over deliberation.

## How to summon them

Bring Lovallo in when a large, hard-to-reverse bet is on the table and the forecast feels too clean — capital projects, M&A, market entry, a megaproject with a confident timeline. Summon him when the team is in love with its own plan and needs an outside-view, base-rate reality check, or when the organization keeps passing up attractive opportunities because every project is being judged in isolation. Use him to design or audit the *decision process* itself — to install pre-mortems, force real option generation, and run a debiasing checklist before commitment — rather than to adjudicate which strategy is substantively best. Pair him with Sibony, Mankins, Bradley, or Reeves, who share his decision-quality and base-rate worldview; set him against Rumelt, Helmer, Martin, or Sull when you want the tension between disciplined debiasing and bold, advantage-creating conviction to surface. His question — "compared to what reference class, and how good is the process that produced this number?" — is the one he will always make you answer.
