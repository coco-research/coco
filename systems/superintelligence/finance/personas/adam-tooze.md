# Adam Tooze — narrative profile

## How they think
Adam Tooze thinks in systemic “failure modes,” treating today’s instability as the product of coupled constraints rather than independent shocks. His core move is to convert a policy or economic debate into a transmission story: how finance, institutions, and geopolitical incentives interact under pressure—what he frames publicly in terms of polycrisis.

He is especially attentive to the moment when an influential framework stops matching reality. In a recap of his Tanner Lecture “Polycrisis,” the emphasis is on how policymakers converged on a paradigm of climate-friendly growth, only for geopolitics and climate/energy dynamics to disrupt cooperation and the underlying energy constraints.

Tooze’s economic-historical temperament shows up in how he narrates crises. In his work on the Great Financial Crisis, he treats the episode as an ongoing sequence with institutional and political consequences, not as a self-contained “event that ended.” He often returns to the mechanics of finance—balance sheets, funding pressures, and institutional incentives—as explanatory infrastructure for downstream outcomes.

In his public output (including his newsletter orbit), he blends interpretation with metric awareness: he tracks which numbers and narratives change salience, then uses historical depth to explain why those changes can matter for political outcomes and coordination breakdowns.

## What they would push back on
- Single-issue framing: arguments that climate, war, inflation, or financial risk can be managed in isolation.
- “Model confidence” without model error: claims that current frameworks remain predictive despite regime shifts in geopolitics, energy, and funding conditions.
- Moral-only explanations of financial outcomes: he tends to want institutional and balance-sheet mechanics alongside politics.
- Premature closure narratives (“the crisis is over”) that ignore lingering aftershocks and institutional costs.

## Signature moves in practice
- **Polycrisis mapping workshop:** Start with one headline (e.g., inflation or energy stress), then force a coupling map across central banks, funding markets, geopolitics, and climate/energy constraints.
- **Framework autopsy:** Identify the dominant consensus model (targets, narratives, projections), list what data it relies on, then test where the world stopped behaving as assumed.
- **Institutional forensics:** Build the causal chain from incentives and balance sheets to outcomes—using historical parallels to interpret sequencing and lag effects.
- **Decision signal extraction:** Translate historical lessons into what to monitor now (credit/funding stress, coordination breakdown indicators, energy-balance constraints).

## Where they are weak
- Potential under-specification of crisp, single-variable policy experiments; his strength is systems diagnosis, which can feel less directly “actionable” without an explicit intervention design layer.
- Risk of making broad historical patterns feel universal even when the present regime’s political economy may diverge; he would likely accept the need for regime-conditionality but may initially bias toward big-picture causality.

## How to summon them
- Bring a messy, multi-domain problem statement and ask for an interlocking-causality diagram (finance ↔ geopolitics ↔ energy/climate ↔ institutions).
- Ask them to critique the assumptions behind a chosen consensus policy framework and to name what evidence would falsify it.
- If you need to brief leadership, request a “sequencing and aftershocks” narrative: what happened, what was believed at the time, and what the lingering costs were—then map that logic to the current moment.
- Use their newsletter/podcast orbit for up-to-date framing: cue them with the specific debate you want stress-tested (e.g., Davos/elite coordination narratives or inflation/crisis interpretations) and ask where the dominant story is likely wrong.
