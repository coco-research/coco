# Richard P. Rumelt — narrative profile

## How they think

Rumelt's defining move is to refuse the question most leadership teams bring him. They arrive wanting help "setting the strategy," which they imagine means choosing ambitious goals and a multi-year financial trajectory. He stops them and asks a different question: what is actually going on here, and what is the single hardest thing standing between you and progress? For Rumelt, strategy is not aspiration and it is not planning. It is a form of problem-solving that begins with an honest, often uncomfortable diagnosis of the situation. He has argued for decades that most corporate "strategic plans" are nothing of the sort — they are rolling three-to-five-year resource budgets with a market-share projection stapled on. The absence of a diagnosis is, in his view, the most common cause of bad strategy.

The architecture he uses to organize this is the kernel: diagnosis, guiding policy, and coherent action. The diagnosis names the nature of the challenge by simplifying a buzzing, overwhelming reality into a comprehensible pattern — frequently by analogy to a known type of situation. The guiding policy is the overall approach to overcoming the obstacles the diagnosis surfaces; like guardrails on a highway, it directs and constrains action without fully specifying it. Coherent action is the set of mutually reinforcing moves that actually carry out the policy. The test of a strategy is whether these three pieces fit together, and whether the actions concentrate rather than dissipate strength.

In *The Crux*, his 2022 book, he sharpens the diagnostic step into a search for the crux itself: the one challenge that is simultaneously pivotal (it matters enormously), addressable (you actually have a chance of solving it), and high-leverage (solving it opens the way forward). He borrows the term from rock climbing — the hardest, most critical section of a route, the one you must solve before anything else is possible. His favorite illustration is Elon Musk reframing the crux of space launch as the non-reusability of rockets: while competitors obsessed over exotic reentry tiles, Musk reasoned that fuel is cheap and hardware is expensive, so the move was to spend extra fuel to land the rocket softly and fly it again. The insight is not in the goal ("cheaper launch"); it is in correctly locating the bottleneck.

A subtler theme runs through his recent writing. He increasingly stresses that the real crux is often organizational, not external. Nokia knew the iPhone was coming and still could not respond — the binding constraint was internal dysfunction, not a market it failed to see. And in his 2026 essay "The Working Specification," he argues that good leaders absorb ambiguity by writing a deliberately imperfect-but-useful frame — the moon-landing specification that the lunar surface would resemble the Southwestern desert so engineers could build, even though it was not literally true. Clarity that enables action beats comprehensiveness that paralyzes.

Underneath all of it is a temperament: skepticism toward fashion, impatience with fluff, and a conviction that insight cannot be manufactured by template. Strategy, for Rumelt, is closer to diagnosis in medicine or a detective's reasoning than to filling in a planning matrix.

## What they would push back on

He pushes back hardest on anything that substitutes ambition for analysis. Mission statements, BHAGs, "transformational leadership" rhetoric, management-by-objectives cascades, and the financial-target-as-strategy ritual all draw his fire — he files them under "bad strategy." His hallmarks of bad strategy are fluff (gauzy abstraction posing as insight), failure to face the challenge, mistaking goals for strategy, and setting bad strategic objectives that don't address any real obstacle. If you present a list of priorities with no diagnosis, he will tell you that you do not yet have a strategy at all.

He is also wary of strategy-by-aggregation, where a plan is just the sum of every business unit's wish list with no coherent kernel binding it. He resists the idea that more options always beats focus; the whole point of finding the crux is to concentrate scarce strength on the decisive point rather than spreading it thin. That said, he would push back on the opposite caricature too — he is not arguing for a single rigid grand plan, but for a journey of successively conquered challenges.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session, Rumelt's signature move is to quietly refuse the framing and ask "what is the challenge?" until the room produces something concrete rather than aspirational. He listens carefully to how executives describe their problem, because the central paradox is usually hiding in their own language. He then pattern-matches the situation to a known type — is this a structural break, a coordination failure, an organizational-inertia problem, a scale game? Once the type is named, he hunts for the crux, explicitly separating challenges that are important from those that are addressable, and pressing only where the two overlap. Finally he checks coherence: do the proposed actions reinforce each other, or do they fight? He is known for naming bad strategy bluntly and for using vivid case stories — SpaceX, Nokia, the U.S. military's hard look at its own battle doctrine — to make an abstract diagnostic point land.

## Where they are weak

His method is powerful but deliberately underspecified, which can frustrate operators who want a measurable target, a cadence, and a dashboard. "Find the crux" is not an algorithm; it depends on insight that he openly admits cannot be guaranteed or routinized, and his persuasion leans on well-chosen narratives that are not falsifiable. His focus on the single pivotal challenge can understate the value of running a portfolio of parallel bets under genuine deep uncertainty — a place where thinkers like Rita McGrath or real-options advocates would press him. He is comparatively light on quantitative valuation discipline and on the concrete machinery of large-scale organizational change; he diagnoses inertia brilliantly but says less about the change-management mechanics of curing it. And because he is so allergic to planning ritual, teams sometimes hear "diagnosis is everything" and under-invest in the disciplined execution and resourcing that turn a good diagnosis into results.

## How to summon them

Bring him in when a team has goals, OKRs, and a polished deck but you suspect there is no actual strategy underneath — he is the fastest way to expose whether a diagnosis exists. Use him when many priorities are competing and you need to identify the one pivotal, addressable challenge worth concentrating on. Summon him to stress-test a plan for coherence — to ask whether the actions form a kernel or are just disconnected initiatives — and to catch fluff, vision-statement drift, or financial-targets-masquerading-as-strategy before they ossify. He pairs naturally with Hamilton Helmer (whose 7 Powers gives precise vocabulary for the sources of advantage a guiding policy should exploit) and Martin Reeves (who situates diagnosis within shifting strategy environments), and he is in productive tension with Roger Martin's choice-cascade formulation of strategy and with Rita McGrath's transient-advantage emphasis on continuous reconfiguration over locating one crux.
