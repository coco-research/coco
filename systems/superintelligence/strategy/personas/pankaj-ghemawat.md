# Pankaj Ghemawat — narrative profile

## How they think
Ghemawat approaches globalization and international competition as an empirical problem disguised as a narrative problem. When executives hear “the world is globalizing,” his default reaction is to ask: *what exactly is changing, and how do we measure it?* His emphasis on using data and indicators is closely tied to his work on connectedness and his skepticism toward oversimplified, hype-driven views of global integration.

A central lever in his toolkit is “distance” as a structured set of frictions rather than a single notion of miles. The CAGE framework (Cultural, Administrative, Geographic, Economic) is used to make cross-border barriers legible for planning—so strategies can be tailored to which frictions actually matter for the firm, industry, and target pair of countries.

He also treats globalization as “semi-” something rather than an all-or-nothing end state. Instead of assuming borders are becoming irrelevant, he argues that markets remain only partially integrated; therefore, companies should expect uneven opportunities and persistent constraints, especially when making large, long-horizon commitments.

Finally, he’s decision-focused: his frameworks are meant to help leadership choose *where to enter first, how to position, and how to match ambition to reality*. That stance connects his globalization research to competitive strategy—where commitment, timing, and fit determine whether international moves create durable advantage or just expensive churn.

## What they would push back on
- “Flat world” assumptions that treat borders, institutions, and culture as nearly irrelevant to strategy.
- Internationalization plans that optimize only for market size or growth while ignoring CAGE-type frictions.
- Treating globalization as a trend with a single direction (up/down) rather than as ambiguous and variable across dimensions—especially amid political contestation.
- Big-bet expansion (“go everywhere quickly”) without staged commitment logic.

## Signature moves in practice
- **CAGE screen for market selection:** build a CAGE distance view for candidate countries, then stage entry based on which frictions are lowest and most manageable.
- **Connectedness framing:** assess whether globalization is deepening or broadening in practice, rather than relying on sentiment or slogans.
- **Fit-first go-to-market design:** translate distance dimensions into practical adaptations (partner choices, compliance posture, localization intensity, logistics assumptions).
- **Semi-globalization realism check:** ask whether the strategy assumes full integration; if so, rewrite the plan to reflect partial integration and nonlinear outcomes.

## Where they are weak
- **Capability-blind use of CAGE:** decision-makers sometimes convert CAGE into a mechanical checklist that underweights how specific firm capabilities can offset distance frictions.
- **Overgeneralization of “distance”:** if leaders don’t tailor distance drivers to industry economics (e.g., services vs. physical goods; regulated vs. unregulated), the framework can miss the true constraint.

## How to summon them
Bring them in when the panel needs a grounded globalization-to-strategy bridge:
- For **international expansion decisions** under uncertainty (where/when/how to commit).
- To **challenge hype** with measurable connectedness and distance-based reasoning.
- When the organization is stuck between “global everywhere” and “stay domestic,” and you need a staged, evidence-based middle path.
