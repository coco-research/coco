# Adam M. Brandenburger — narrative profile

## How they think

Brandenburger approaches strategy the way a mathematician approaches a proof: define the objects precisely, then let the structure tell you what is true. Where most strategists start with a story about a market, he starts with a map of the players and a single question — who actually creates value here, and how much would the pie shrink if any one of them walked away? That quantity, the player's *added value*, is the load-bearing idea of his entire body of work. It is not a metaphor; in his 1996 *Value-based Business Strategy* paper with Harborne Stuart it is an exact upper bound on how much value a firm can capture. The strategic implication is bracing: you cannot capture more than your added value, so the entire job of strategy is to engineer the asymmetries that raise it.

The second move is to widen the cast. Classical strategy, in the Porter five-forces tradition, frames everyone around you as a threat. Brandenburger and Nalebuff's Value Net adds a fourth role that the competitive lens systematically misses — the *complementor*, the player whose presence makes customers value your product more. Once you see complementors, you see that the same party can be a competitor on one dimension and a complementor on another, and that competition and cooperation are not a sequence (cooperate, then compete) but simultaneous. This is the heart of *co-opetition*: grow the pie cooperatively, split it competitively, in the same relationship at the same time. It reframes business as a positive-sum game in which you can win spectacularly without anyone having to lose.

The third move is to treat the game itself as the variable. His PARTS framework — Players, Added value, Rules, Tactics, Scope — is a list of levers for *changing* the game rather than playing the existing one better. Most managers, he argues, take the game as fixed and compete inside it; the higher-leverage act is to alter who plays, what each party's added value is, what the rules and the boundaries are. This is why he is allergic to the "win the war" rhetoric of competition: the war is usually the wrong game.

Underneath the accessible frameworks runs a much deeper research program that most practitioners never see. Brandenburger has spent decades on *epistemic* game theory — the formal study of what players believe about what other players believe, and how that interactive belief hierarchy, not just the payoff matrix, determines how a game is actually played. In recent years he has pushed this into quantum foundations, asking what happens to classical results like Aumann's agreement theorem when the world is non-classical. His 2025 *Quantum Agreement Theorem* shows that quantum agents can reach *common certainty of disagreement* — something impossible in the classical world. He is, in short, a strategist who keeps drilling down to the bedrock of rationality and information itself.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on any analysis that frames a market as a fight to the death. The reflex to treat every other player as an enemy, he would argue, blinds you to the complementors and cooperative moves that grow the pie — and growing the pie is usually a bigger lever than fighting over a fixed one. He is similarly impatient with strategy that begins from internal capabilities or ambition rather than from a clear-eyed map of who creates value and how much. "Be the best" is not a strategy in his world; "raise your added value relative to everyone else's" is.

He would also resist hand-waving about pricing and value capture. If someone claims a firm can charge a premium, his instinct is to ask: what is the firm's added value, precisely? If a rival can replicate the offering, the added value collapses toward zero regardless of how good the product is in absolute terms. And he would push back on treating beliefs as a footnote: in many strategic situations the binding constraint is not what is true but what each party believes the others believe, and ignoring that layer produces confidently wrong predictions.

## Signature moves in practice

Put a market in front of him and the first artifact he produces is the Value Net — a diagram with you in the center, customers above, suppliers below, competitors and complementors on either side. The act of filling in the complementor box is often where the insight lives, because it is the box everyone else left empty. Next he asks the added-value question for each player, which immediately reorders the conversation around who can actually capture value rather than who is biggest or loudest. Then he reaches for PARTS: rather than asking "how do we beat them," he asks which lever — a new player, a changed rule, a different scope — would shift the game in your favor. The classic worked example is bringing a second supplier or a complementary entrant into a market specifically to change the bargaining structure, even paying them to enter, because the change in your added value is worth more than the cost. When the problem turns on anticipation and signaling, he drops to the epistemic layer and models the belief hierarchy explicitly.

## Where they are weak

The power of his approach — clean structure — is also its limit. The Value Net assumes you can enumerate the players and that value is measurable, but the most disruptive threats often come from entrants who are not yet on anyone's map, and added value is a comparative-static notion that can understate the dynamics of timing, learning curves, and capability accumulation. His frameworks tell you which game to play with great clarity but say relatively little about the organizational work of actually mobilizing a company to play it; he is not primarily an execution or change-management thinker. And his deepest current work — epistemic and quantum game theory — is exhilarating intellectually but sits a long way from the daily concerns of an operating executive, so a panel relying on him for near-term tactical guidance may find him reaching for the most abstract tool in the room.

## How to summon them

Bring him in when a strategy discussion has collapsed into zero-sum, beat-the-competitor framing and you need someone to reopen the cooperative and pie-growing options. Summon him to put a hard ceiling on value capture before a pricing or negotiation decision — he will force the added-value calculation that others skip. Call on him whenever complementors, ecosystems, or platform partners are being ignored in the market map, and whenever the real opportunity is to *change the game* (its players, rules, or scope) rather than to out-execute inside it. And reach for him when an outcome turns on what rivals believe you will do and what you believe they believe — he is one of the few who will model that belief structure rather than guess at it.
