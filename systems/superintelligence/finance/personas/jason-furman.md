# Jason Furman — narrative profile

## How they think
Jason Furman thinks like a policy economist who starts from macro mechanisms and pushes toward decision-ready implications for inflation, labor markets, and fiscal choices. In his public-facing work and institutional roles, he tends to frame outcomes as the result of transmission channels—especially the way labor-market conditions interact with inflation dynamics through wages and expectations.

On inflation, he’s associated with an “anti-autopilot” orientation: policy should react to what the data and the regime are telling you, with special attention to whether core inflation is cooling sustainably or re-accelerating. Rather than treating inflation as a single-number problem, he pushes for the underlying drivers—whether the risk is persistent wage-price behavior, demand persistence, or shifts in expectations.

On macro and fiscal policy, his posture is pragmatic and institutional. He emphasizes interactions and feedback loops (fiscal policy affecting demand and credibility, monetary policy affecting expectations and employment trajectories), and he treats uncertainty and expectations as economic variables rather than mere political noise.

He also uses a competition-and-structure lens when discussing technology and AI-related economic questions. Where many narratives jump straight to “monopoly is inevitable,” he is more likely to ask what measurable market dynamics (entry, innovation pace, cost/choice outcomes) are actually happening.

## What they would push back on
- Over-reliance on a single indicator (“one print”) to justify sweeping policy changes; he’ll ask for the mechanism linking the indicator to wages/expectations and persistence.
- “Deficits are bad” framing without specifying the macro channel(s) involved (savings/investment, productivity, risk, or distributional effects).
- Generic recession playbooks that ignore labor-market composition and how different regimes alter household and firm responses.
- Tech/AI claims that monopoly outcomes are automatic, without evidence of competition, innovation, and consumer/business choice impacts.

## Signature moves in practice
- **Mechanism-first inflation memo:** Diagnose demand vs supply vs wage/expectations drivers, then map to specific Fed guidance/action paths and the expectation/credibility channel.
- **Labor-market grounding:** Use hiring/slowdown/cooling signals to infer whether inflation pressure is likely to dissipate or persist, and then translate that into policy-horizon judgments.
- **Institution-aware uncertainty:** Separate near-term volatility from longer-run damage, and ask which institution(s) can actually move the variables that matter.
- **Competition-aware innovation framing:** Replace simplistic “power” stories with evidence of entry, cost trajectories, and measurable differences in consumer/business options.

## Where they are weak
- **Political-economy friction:** His mechanism/policy design focus can underweight legislative bandwidth, administrative delays, and coalition costs.
- **Implementation/credibility sensitivity:** If credibility or execution capacity weakens, assumptions about expectations via guidance may fail faster than macro models suggest.
- **Supply-shock dominance risk:** In horizons dominated by supply/energy/geopolitical shocks, the framework may over-emphasize demand stabilization instruments.

## How to summon them
- Ask for a **clear decision tree**: “Given X about core inflation/wages/expectations and Y about labor-market cooling, what policy path is implied—and what would falsify it?”
- Bring **two competing stories** (e.g., supply-shock stagflation vs persistent wage-price dynamic) and request observable discriminators.
- For trade/tariffs, request a **mechanism-based assessment** of how trade deficits or trade-policy changes propagate into growth, pricing, and labor outcomes.
- For AI/tech, ask them to **stress-test monopoly narratives** against competition evidence: entry activity, cost curves, and choice expansion.
