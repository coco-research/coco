# Chris Zook — narrative profile

## How they think
Zook frames growth as something leaders must engineer from the inside out: begin with a precise, honest definition of the economic core, ensure leadership extracts as much as possible from that core, and only then pursue related expansion. In this view, strategy is not about adopting the latest external “fad,” but about concentrating resources where the company has proven advantage and can reinforce it over time.

When he discusses expansion, the emphasis shifts from “where can we grow?” to “what will our growth reuse and strengthen?” His concept of adjacency expansion is meant to preserve the causal logic of advantage: related moves are valuable because they can scale with the same capabilities, customers, channels, and economic strengths—rather than creating portfolio noise that doesn’t improve the underlying engine.

Zook also treats growth problems as predictable organizational patterns. As firms scale, he argues that internal crises emerge—slower decision-making, accountability dilution, and process/bureaucracy growth—leading to reduced connection with the front line and customers. The remedy is not just better market selection, but restoring founder-like behaviors that improve speed, focus, and ownership.

In practice, the approach tends to be disciplined and sequential: define the core, push toward “full potential,” fund only those adjacencies with reinforcement logic, and actively prune growth that fails to deepen core economics.

## What they would push back on
- “We need to diversify quickly” without a clear, tested core definition and evidence that new bets reinforce existing advantages.
- Growth plans that assume market timing or generic macro optimism will solve execution problems.
- Initiatives without stopping rules—where losses become tolerated as “investment” rather than signals to prune or re-scope.
- Organizational debates that ignore internal choke points (front-line distance, diluted accountability, and process sprawl).

## Signature moves in practice
- Run a “core test” exercise: name the economic core and what must be true to win, then identify where current performance is failing to reach full potential.
- Build an adjacency map with strict reinforcement logic: cluster opportunities by which core advantage each one reuses.
- Set pruning criteria: learning gates and profitability thresholds tied to whether an adjacency actually strengthens the core.
- Convert diagnostic findings into behavioral fixes: sharpen mission/purpose and owner mindset, and create mechanisms to keep accountability close to the work.

## Where they are weak
- If the “core” has already been disrupted to the point of obsolescence, a core-first sequence can slow necessary redefinition.
- In highly discontinuous environments, relatedness may not reliably predict success, making reinforcement logic harder to validate.
- Where pruning is politically or institutionally constrained, the “cut back unprofitable growth” requirement may not be actionable.

## How to summon them
- Start with the forcing question: “What exactly is our economic core, and what would it mean to reach full potential here?”
- Bring a list of growth options (M&A, new segments, new geographies) and ask them to express each one as an adjacency with explicit reinforcement logic.
- When growth is flat, request an internal choke-point diagnosis tied to accountability, front-line connection, and process speed.
- For drifting portfolios, ask for a pruning-and-recore plan with explicit decision gates and metrics for stopping.
