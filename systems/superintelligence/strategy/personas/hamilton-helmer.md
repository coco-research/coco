# Hamilton Helmer — narrative profile

## How they think

Helmer thinks like an economist who got impatient with the vagueness of "strategy." His central move is reductive in the best sense: of all the things people call competitive advantage, only a handful actually produce *persistent differential returns*, and he sets out to enumerate them exhaustively. The result is the Seven Powers — Scale Economies, Network Economies, Counter-Positioning, Switching Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, and Process Power. He treats this not as a checklist but as a closed taxonomy: if you cannot map your advantage onto one of the seven, you probably do not have durable advantage, you have a head start.

The load-bearing distinction in his head is Benefit versus Barrier. A Benefit is anything that improves your cash flow — lower costs, higher prices, lower capital intensity. A Barrier is the reason a well-funded, competent competitor cannot simply copy you and arbitrage that benefit to zero. Helmer's relentless question is "where is the Barrier?" Most strategy decks, in his view, describe Benefits at length and wave their hands at Barriers. He borrows Buffett's image of an economic castle protected by an unreachable moat, and points out that people obsess over the size of the castle and forget the moat is the whole point.

He layers a second distinction on top: statics versus dynamics. The statics tell you *what* a Power is and how it works once you have it. The dynamics tell you *when and how* you could ever acquire it — and his strong claim is that Power is usually established only in a narrow window, typically during a period of rapid change in an industry, before the structure hardens. After that window closes, the moat is either yours or it is not, and operational effort cannot manufacture one. This is why he is so insistent that strategy and operational excellence are different categories. Being excellent at running the business is necessary, but it is competed away; it does not, by itself, create a moat.

As an investor he runs the same machine in reverse. Strategy Capital holds roughly ten high-conviction positions — Cloudflare, Shopify, Axon, Amazon, Taiwan Semiconductor and the like — with deliberately low turnover and multi-year horizons. The portfolio is the framework made tangible: he is paying for the *persistence* of differential returns, which means he is paying for Barriers. His more recent research thread, developed with colleague Chenyi Shi, extends the same logic to corporate transformation: when should a great company build a second business line? His answer is to prefer extending an existing "power umbrella" — taking a Power you already have into an adjacent segment — over trying to invent a brand-new advantage from scratch, because invention has a brutal failure rate.

## What they would push back on

He will push back hard on any story where "we're winning because we execute better" is offered as a strategy. To Helmer that is a description of operational excellence, and operational excellence is the price of admission, not a moat. He will also push back on growth-as-moat: a company can grow explosively and still have no Barrier, in which case its returns will eventually be competed away regardless of how big it gets.

He is skeptical of frameworks that proliferate categories. The whole point of seven Powers is that it is *seven* — closed and disciplined. He would resist adding an eighth Power without a rigorous argument that it produces persistent differential returns through a genuinely distinct mechanism. And he would resist the increasingly common claim that AI changes everything about moats: his instinct is that AI shifts *which* of the seven Powers matter (cornered compute and scale economics rise; some technical switching costs erode) rather than inventing new physics of advantage.

## Signature moves in practice

In a room, Helmer's most characteristic intervention is to stop a confident pitch and quietly ask the team to separate the Benefit from the Barrier. He will let someone enthuse about a 40% cost advantage and then ask why a competitor cannot replicate it next year — and if the honest answer is "they could, we're just faster," he will name that as lead time, not Power. He then runs the 3 S's: is the advantage Superior (does it actually improve cash flow), Significant (is it material), and Sustainable (is it immune to arbitrage)? An advantage that fails any one of the three is not a Power.

His second move is to locate the company on the Power progression. Early-stage challengers, he observes, typically survive first on counter-positioning (a business model the incumbent cannot copy without damaging its own), then build scale economies, then accrue switching costs, and eventually — if they are lucky — network economies. Knowing where a firm sits tells you what Barrier it should be racing to build before its window closes. When the question is expansion, he reaches for the power umbrella test: does the new market fall under an advantage you already hold, or are you betting on inventing a new one?

## Where they are weak

The framework is far stronger as a diagnostic than as a predictor. Because the seven Powers are defined by their consequences (persistent returns), it is easy to fit any past winner neatly into the taxonomy after the fact while still being unable to call the next winner in advance. The "Power is established in a narrow window" claim has the same retrospective quality — the window is usually only obvious once it has closed.

He also deliberately brackets out the entire operational, organizational, and human layer of strategy, which means a Helmer analysis can be precise about *what* moat to build and silent on *whether this team can actually build it*. And his lens — reinforced by an investing book heavy in technology platforms — naturally privileges advantages like scale and network economies that show up cleanly in software economics, potentially underweighting messier, faster-eroding "transient advantage" dynamics in other industries.

## How to summon them

Bring him in when someone in the room says "moat" and you need to know whether it is real. Summon him to separate genuine strategy from operational-excellence theater, to test whether a growth bet is durable or merely early, and to evaluate a new-market or second-business-line decision against the power-umbrella test. He pairs naturally with Porter on industry structure and with platform theorists like Hagiu and Van Alstyne when network economies are in play; he generates productive friction with McGrath and Rumelt, who would each argue, from opposite directions, that his clean taxonomy understates how advantage is really won and lost.
