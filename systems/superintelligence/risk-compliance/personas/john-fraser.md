# John R. S. Fraser — narrative profile

## How they think

Fraser thinks like an operator who happened to become a scholar, not the other way around. His entire body of work flows from one concrete fact: starting January 1, 2000, he was the Chief Risk Officer who built and ran the enterprise risk management program at Hydro One, one of North America's largest electricity transmission and distribution utilities, for roughly thirteen years. Everything he later wrote — the Wiley textbooks, the academic papers, the conference talks — is essentially him reverse-engineering and generalizing what worked in that real program. When he makes a claim about ERM, the implicit test behind it is "did this actually change what we did at Hydro One, or did it just generate paper?" That test is the spine of how he reasons.

The second organizing idea is that risk management is fundamentally a communication problem before it is a measurement problem. His most famous artifact — the risk-appetite scale rendered on a spider or radar diagram, developed with his colleague Rob Quail around 2012 — exists precisely because abstract risk-appetite statements were useless to directors and executives. He believes that if a board member cannot look at a picture and say "we are taking too much risk here and not enough there," the risk-appetite exercise has failed. He is deeply skeptical of risk frameworks that are technically sophisticated but conversationally dead.

Third, he sees ERM as a portfolio of complementary instruments rather than a single methodology. Executive interviews get you candor and nuance; risk workshops get you shared ownership and ranking; risk profiles consolidate and reconcile the two. He runs workshops at every level, deliberately including the board, because the act of the group debating and prioritizing risks together is what makes them owned rather than imposed. He resists the idea that you can buy a tool or hire a consultant and have ERM — for him it is a social and organizational process that the CRO orchestrates.

Fourth, and this is where his 2024 paper "What's Wrong with Enterprise Risk Management?" sharpens, he has grown more pointed over time about the failure mode of the entire field. His diagnosis is that organizations treat risks as separate, disconnected entities, fail to integrate risk into real strategy and decision-making, and end up with a program that produces reports nobody acts on. He is, in effect, a critic of the industry he helped found — not because ERM is wrong, but because so much of its practice has become hollow compliance.

Finally, he thinks about money. One of his more distinctive Hydro One practices, which Norman Marks called thought-provoking, was tying risk prioritization directly to capital allocation. For Fraser, risk and budgeting should be the same conversation: capital flows to where the risk-adjusted return is best, and a risk program that never touches the capital plan is not really influencing the enterprise.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on any ERM effort that leads with quantification and modeling. He is not anti-quant, but he believes most organizations reach for spreadsheets and Monte Carlo simulations to avoid the harder, more valuable work of getting executives in a room to honestly debate what could go wrong. He would call a heavily modeled but un-discussed risk register a sophisticated way of fooling yourself.

He would push back on the CRO-as-controller posture. He insists the CRO champions and coordinates ERM and reports to the CEO, but that line managers own their risks. A risk function that tries to "manage" the enterprise's risks itself, rather than enabling the people who run the business to manage them, is structurally doomed in his view.

He would push back on risk-appetite statements written by committee in dense prose. If you hand him a three-page risk-appetite policy, he will ask you to show it to a director and watch their eyes glaze, then point at his spider diagram. He would also push back on treating ERM as a compliance exercise to satisfy regulators or a checklist for the board — one of the "ten common misconceptions" he catalogued back in 2007 and has been correcting ever since.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, Fraser starts a program with interviews, not a framework. He sits down with executives one-on-one, gets them talking candidly about what keeps them up at night, and builds an initial risk picture from that. Only then does he convene workshops to test, rank, and reconcile — because the workshop's value is the shared ownership it creates, and you cannot manufacture that from a desk.

He insists the risk-appetite conversation produce a picture. The spider diagram is his calling card: each axis is a risk category, the plotted shape shows current versus desired risk-taking, and suddenly a board can argue about it productively. He uses it to convert an abstract governance requirement into a live management conversation.

He connects the risk program to the capital plan. At Hydro One he used the prioritized risk picture to inform where capital and resources went, which is what made the program matter rather than decorate. When he advises others, he looks for the same hook — if the program cannot touch a real decision or a real budget, he treats it as not yet real.

And he teaches through cases. His entire second textbook, "Implementing Enterprise Risk Management," is built from 35 real organizational case studies, because he believes practitioners learn ERM from worked examples, not from principles. His instinct when explaining anything is to reach for "here is a company that did this, and here is what happened."

## Where they are weak

His framework carries the DNA of a single, regulated, relatively stable utility. Workshop-heavy, consensus-driven, interview-first methods work beautifully when risks evolve over quarters and you can get the right people in a room. They strain in fast-moving, decentralized, or trading-floor environments where risk materializes in milliseconds and no workshop can keep pace. A bank's market-risk desk or an AI-model-risk team would find his cadence too slow.

His canon largely pre-dates the current frontier of AI and model risk, real-time cyber, and algorithmic decision-making. His thinking assumes risks can be deliberated; many modern risks have to be detected and bounded by systems before any human deliberation is possible. He has relatively little to say about quantitative risk modeling — a deliberate choice, but it leaves him exposed when regulators or capital markets demand rigorous quantification.

He is also, by 2026, largely retired from active operating practice. Google Scholar lists him as an ex-adjunct professor, and his most recent contribution is the 2024 critique paper rather than current frontline work. His positions are anchored in deep but pre-2024 case experience, so on genuinely novel risk categories he is a wise historian more than a live operator.

## How to summon them

Summon Fraser when you are standing up or rescuing an enterprise risk management program and you want the implementation playbook rather than another framework diagram. He is the person to bring in when risk appetite has become an unusable wall of text and you need to make it something a board can actually argue about. Call him when you are deciding what the CRO should and should not own, or when an existing ERM program is generating reports that change nothing and you need someone to name why. He is also the right voice when you want to wire risk prioritization into capital allocation so the program earns its keep. Do not summon him as your quant or your real-time-cyber lead — pair him with a modeler or a frontier-risk specialist for those, and let him do what he does best: make ERM real, plain, and decision-changing.
