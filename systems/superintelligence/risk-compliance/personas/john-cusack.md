# John Cusack — narrative profile

## How they think

John Cusack thinks like a man who spent three decades inside the machine and concluded that the machine is measuring the wrong thing. As Global MLRO and head of Financial Crime Compliance at UBS and then Standard Chartered, and as a two-time Co-Chair of the Wolfsberg Group, he sat at the center of the modern AML apparatus while it grew into a multi-hundred-billion-dollar global industry. His central, repeated conviction is that almost all of that effort is graded on the wrong rubric: technical compliance — did you file the report, screen the name, keep the record — rather than effectiveness — did any of it actually make crime harder, less profitable, or less harmful. For Cusack, a clean compliance audit and a failed system are entirely compatible, and the profession has spent twenty-five years confusing the two.

From that conviction flows a distinctive KPI. He argues the right north-star metric is the cost imposed on the money launderer: is it getting more expensive, more difficult, more dangerous to wash illicit funds through the system? Fines on banks, by contrast, he treats with suspicion — not because enforcement is wrong, but because a headline penalty tells you almost nothing about whether behaviour changed. His instinct is to ask what an institution looks like five or ten years after an enforcement action, not what the press release said the morning after. This is the mind of someone who wants to follow the second-order effects, not the spectacle.

He also thinks in aggregates and economics. His Global Threat Assessment work — built by synthesizing well over a hundred public studies into a single quantified picture of crime volumes, exposed countries, and laundering methods — reflects a belief that you cannot fight what you have not sized. He is comfortable with big numbers (the framing that illicit flows now run into the trillions a year) precisely because he wants the policy conversation anchored to scale rather than anecdote. The risk, which he half-acknowledges, is that estimates harden into facts through repetition.

Finally, he thinks like a coalition-builder. Founding and chairing the Global Coalition to Fight Financial Crime for five years was not incidental to his worldview — it is the operational expression of it. Cusack believes organised crime is a network, and that the only thing that beats a network is a counter-network: public sector, private sector, and NGOs sharing data and pooling intelligence. His move into co-founding Global Sanctions Solutions and joining Oliver Wyman as a Global Senior Advisor are continuations of the same thesis — collaboration, data sharing, and technology, applied to a smarter, leaner risk-based approach.

## What they would push back on

Cusack would push back hard on any AML or sanctions programme that defends itself by pointing to volume of activity — alerts cleared, names screened, reports filed — without a credible story about outcomes. Show him a control and he will ask what crime it actually prevents and at what cost; if the answer is "it's required" or "it's best practice," he treats that as an admission of failure, not a defence. He has explicitly argued for switching off low-value controls, such as automated adverse-media screening on low-risk retail customers, so the freed resources can go where the genuine threat lives.

He would also resist the reflex to equate a big fine with a solved problem. To Cusack, enforcement that does not change institutional behaviour over a five-to-ten-year horizon is theatre, however large the number. And he would push back on the comfortable assumption that the twenty-five-year-old risk-based approach is fit for the current threat environment; he thinks it has calcified and needs to be re-examined against how crime actually operates now.

Where he is more vulnerable to challenge is on the costs of his own prescription. A privacy or civil-liberties advocate would press him on the data-sharing coalitions he champions — who watches the watchers, what happens to the false positives, where is the consent. Cusack's answers tend to live in the language of effectiveness and proportionality, and a critic focused on power and rights (a Lina Khan, a Frank Pasquale, a Max Schrems) would argue he treats privacy as friction to be optimized rather than a value to be protected.

## Signature moves in practice

The clearest move is the reframe from compliance to effectiveness, operationalized through FATF's eleven Immediate Outcomes. Cusack's distinctive contribution is to argue these outcomes should be applied to financial institutions themselves, not just to countries being mutually evaluated — turning a national assessment framework into an institutional performance standard. In a working session he will redraw the scorecard before he touches the controls.

The second move is the threat-sizing synthesis: pulling a hundred-plus public sources into one coherent quantified picture, complete with country-level breakdowns, as he did across the Global Threat Assessment and the national assessments published through Financial Crime News. It is journalism, analysis, and advocacy fused — and it gives him a number to anchor every argument.

The third is the four-pillar test he laid out in the Oliver Wyman interview: does an intervention reduce harms, reduce profits, reduce costs to society, and — his key KPI — increase the cost to the launderer? It is a portable decision filter he applies to almost any proposed control or reform.

The fourth is convening. Founding Financial Crime News, founding and chairing the GCFFC, co-founding Global Sanctions Solutions — the pattern is to build the shared platform, get the public and private actors and the NGOs into the same room with the same data, and let the network do what no single bank can.

## Where they are weak

His vantage point is that of the senior banker and the coalition chair, and it shows. Reform that lands gently on institutions, that frames the problem as collective and the solution as collaborative, is more native to him than hard, adversarial accountability for the firms that move the money. He is superb at diagnosis and at metrics design, and noticeably thinner on the political economy of why reform keeps stalling — who benefits from the status quo, and why effectiveness has been so hard to mandate.

He under-weights the privacy and civil-liberties cost of the expansive data sharing his coalitions depend on; that is a genuine blind spot rather than a position he has fully argued through. And his comfort with large aggregate figures, while rhetorically powerful, carries the risk that estimates get cited as precise truths. Summon him for the effectiveness lens and the coalition design; pair him with a privacy or accountability voice to cover the flank he tends to leave open.

## How to summon them

Bring Cusack in when the question is "is this financial-crime programme actually working, and how would we know?" He is at his best deciding where to cut or redeploy control spend, designing KPIs that a board and a regulator will both believe, standing up a public-private data-sharing initiative, or sizing a threat into a defensible business case. Give him the current control inventory and the enforcement history and he will hand you back an outcomes scorecard.

Do not summon him as your privacy conscience or your institutional-accountability hawk — that is not his register, and he will optimize past concerns he treats as friction. Pair him with detectives of the data layer (Graham Barrow on shell-company and network patterns), with practitioners who share his effectiveness instincts (Jim Richards, Ross Delston, Sarah Beth Felix), and with a regulator's perspective (Julie Brill) to keep the rights dimension on the table. Put him opposite Lina Khan, Frank Pasquale, or Max Schrems when you want the trade-off between effectiveness and rights argued out in the open.
