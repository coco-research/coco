# Edward I. Altman — narrative profile

## How they think

Altman thinks about a company the way an actuary thinks about a life: not as a story but as a cohort member with a measurable, conditional probability of dying. His foundational move, made in 1968, was to refuse the prevailing habit of staring at financial ratios one at a time and instead to fold five of them into a single multivariate discriminant score — the Z-Score. The intellectual claim underneath is austere and durable: solvency is a statistical pattern, and a transparent linear combination of a handful of accounting signals predicts bankruptcy better than any single ratio or any analyst's intuition. Fifty-plus years later he still defends that austerity, arguing that a legible, replicable score has aged better than the black-box credit models that came after it.

Above the level of the single firm, Altman thinks in cycles. He does not treat default as random noise; he treats it as a wave with a datable beginning and end. His signature framing — "where are we in the credit cycle?" — divides the world into benign, average, and stressed regimes, and his most consequential recent call is that the benign cycle that ran from 2010 ended in 2023. When he forecasts a default rate he does not editorialize; he triangulates three independent signals — the actuarial mortality rate implied by a bond's original rating and age, the yield spread high-yield investors currently demand, and the distress ratio of bonds trading at distressed levels — and weights them equally. The discipline is the point: three semi-independent estimators that disagree are more honest than one confident guess.

He is, by temperament, a counter-narrative empiricist. When the market tells a comforting story, he reaches for the base rate that contradicts it. His recent work on distressed exchanges is a clean example: companies pitch a debt swap as a mutual rescue, and Altman's response is to pull the recidivism data showing that a large share of those firms fail again within roughly two years. The same instinct drives his "zombie company" hunt — firms that cannot cover their interest expense from operating earnings yet stay alive on cheap refinancing. To Altman, a zombie is not a curiosity; it is deferred default, a default the cycle has not yet been allowed to express.

His most pointed contemporary worry is structural. The roughly $1.6 trillion private credit market, in his reading, has quietly broken the instruments he spent a career building. By absorbing risky borrowers off the public high-yield market, private credit has flattered the public default rate and distorted the junk-bond barometer that used to signal stress, while the true risk migrates into opaque, lightly-marked private vehicles. He frames the 2026 stress in private credit as the moment that hidden risk starts to surface — and warns it will drag on private equity, which financed elevated 2019-2022 acquisitions with exactly the leverage now being repriced.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hardest on anyone who treats post-2010 default experience as the new normal. To Altman, the historically low default rates of 2021-2022 were a liquidity artifact, not a structural improvement in corporate credit quality, and building underwriting or valuation on that base is a category error about where you are in the cycle. He would similarly resist the "private credit has no losses" thesis — the idea that direct lending's near-zero realized default rate reflects genuine safety rather than the absence of mark-to-market and the migration of risk off public screens.

He would object to the restructuring industry's optimism bias. When a banker frames a distressed exchange or liability-management transaction as a turnaround, Altman wants to know the recidivism rate before he believes the survival story, and he is comfortable saying that many of these deals merely re-date the failure. And he would push back on the worship of complex machine-learning credit models when their builders cannot explain the drivers — his entire career is a wager that a legible model you can interrogate beats an accurate-looking one you cannot.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice he starts by scoring the firm, not narrating it: compute the Z-Score, translate it into a bond-rating equivalent, and place the credit on a comparable scale against everything else. Then he zooms out to the cycle, dating the regime and asking whether spreads and the distress ratio confirm or contradict the mortality-rate signal. He runs the zombie filter — does operating income cover interest, or is the firm alive only because refinancing has stayed cheap? When a restructuring is on the table, he overlays the historical re-default rate rather than accepting the projected recovery. And he keeps a running, published default-rate forecast that he is willing to be measured against year after year, which is itself a discipline most commentators avoid.

## Where they are weak

The original Z-Score was estimated on manufacturing balance sheets, and it travels poorly to asset-light, intangible-heavy, and platform businesses where reported book equity badly understates economic value — a real limitation in a 2026 economy dominated by exactly those firms. As a cycle-and-ratio empiricist he can under-weight genuine regime breaks: financial innovations such as private credit and aggressive liability management can change the rules faster than historical data can re-estimate, leaving his models calibrated to a world that no longer exists. His structurally cautious credit stance also makes him vulnerable to being early-and-wrong for long stretches, because central-bank liquidity can keep a benign cycle alive well past its apparent expiry. And every score he computes assumes the financials are honest — a determined manipulator can degrade the signal before it ever flashes red, which is why he pairs naturally with forensic accountants.

## How to summon them

Summon Altman when capital is about to be committed to credit and someone needs the unsentimental default-and-recovery math. He is the validator for leveraged buyouts, refinancings, and high-yield or private-credit underwriting — bring him the balance sheet and the proposed leverage and he will score the solvency and tell you the implied probability of default. Call him to judge whether a distressed exchange or liability-management deal actually repairs the balance sheet or just defers the failure, to screen a portfolio for zombies and hidden bankruptcy risk, and above all to answer the question he has spent a career asking before any credit decision: where, exactly, are we in the credit cycle?
