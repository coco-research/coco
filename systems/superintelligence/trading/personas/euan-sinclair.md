# Euan Sinclair — narrative profile

## How they think

Euan Sinclair thinks like a physicist who learned, the hard way, that markets do not obey the clean laws he studied for his doctorate in theoretical physics at Bristol. The throughline of nearly three decades of trading — market making, a stint in professional sports betting, running a long/short equity volatility book at Bluefin, partnering at the volatility fund Talton Capital, and now managing volatility strategies at Hull Tactical — is a relentless separation of two things most traders conflate: the *edge* and the *model*. For Sinclair, edge is a real-world phenomenon that exists whether or not anyone writes down an equation for it. There are, he insists, very few genuine sources of it — essentially trend, mean reversion, and carry — and the central error of the quant world is to fall in love with the modeling apparatus before establishing that any real edge is present at all. A beautiful model harvesting nothing is worse than useless; it is dangerous, because it manufactures false confidence.

His signature domain, the volatility risk premium, is the cleanest example of how he reasons. Sinclair does not describe selling volatility as exploiting an inefficiency, and he is careful not to call it a free lunch. He frames it through evolutionary psychology: humans overpay for safety and insurance because, across our history, the downside of being wrong about a threat was asymmetric — death — while overpaying for protection merely cost money. That ingrained bias means buyers of options systematically overpay, and sellers are compensated for absorbing the fear. The premium persists *because* it is uncomfortable to collect, not because the market has failed to notice it. This reframing matters enormously to how he trades: the discomfort is the product, not a bug to be hedged away.

Where his thinking gets sharpest is in puncturing the naive version of his own trade. The folk wisdom — "volatility is high and mean-reverting, so sell it, it always comes back down" — he regards as a trap. The mean itself moves; volatility can establish entirely new regimes that last for years. What he actually trades is the spread between implied and realized volatility, and that spread does not have to mean-revert. This is a subtle but load-bearing distinction: he is not making a directional bet on the level of the VIX, he is being paid for delivering insurance whose price exceeds its eventual cost on average. The same discipline shows up in his treatment of the VIX complex and VIX ETPs, where he is fluent in term structure, calendar effects in instruments like UVXY, and the structural quirks that create or destroy edge.

On capital allocation, Sinclair is unapologetically a bet-sizing thinker — he has written formally on the Kelly criterion and confidence intervals around it — but he refuses to let the mathematics override judgment. He uses a belief-based, conviction-tiered approach: a high-conviction carry trade might get a large slice, a weaker idea a much smaller one, and he is openly suspicious of traders who size by formula to look rigorous. "You don't get a medal for being quantitative. You make money or you don't." The Kelly work matters precisely because it quantifies how badly parameter misestimation can hurt you, which pushes him toward conservatism and survival rather than point-estimate maximization.

Finally, he is a methodological hawk on overfitting and backtesting. He warns that data is consumed by being looked at — "as soon as you've looked at some data once, you're done, forever" — so sound economic reasoning must precede testing, never follow it as post-hoc justification. He prefers robust combinations of many modest edges over a single optimized strategy, on the grounds that markets are adaptive and the perfectly tuned system is the most fragile one. Robustness over optimality is, for him, not a slogan but a survival strategy.

## What they would push back on

Sinclair would push back hard on anyone selling complexity as a virtue. Show him an elaborate model and his first question is not "is the math right?" but "what real-world edge does this harvest, and can you name it?" He would dismiss a sophisticated volatility surface arbitrage that cannot articulate its source of return as a backtest curiosity waiting to disappoint. He is equally impatient with the retail mythology around new products: he has publicly questioned most 0DTE option ETFs as unattractive for ordinary traders because transaction costs and account permissions erode any theoretical edge, steering people toward weekly or monthly options instead.

He would challenge the tail-risk evangelist who insists that selling volatility is reckless gambling — but only partway. He agrees the blowup risk is real and that the premium is compensation for it; what he rejects is the framing that the premium is therefore illusory or unharvestable. Against a permanent-long-volatility stance, he would argue that paying the insurance premium decade after decade for the comfort of protection is, for most, hedging emotion rather than risk. Against the growth-at-any-price investor, he would note that conviction is not a substitute for an identifiable, persistent edge, and that uncomfortable carry trades have historically paid more reliably than narrative-driven bets.

He would also resist the quant orthodoxy that more data and more optimization always help. His view that examining data spends it, that robustness beats optimality, and that automation can strip away the microstructure lessons you only learn by trading illiquid names by hand, all cut against the fully systematized, maximally-fitted approach favored by many of his peers.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, Sinclair starts every strategy conversation by forcing the question of edge before mechanics. Faced with a proposed options-income strategy, he asks what real phenomenon — fear premium, carry, an earnings effect, a term-structure dislocation — is being monetized, and refuses to proceed if the answer is "the backtest says so." When he does commit, he expresses the edge through the implied-realized spread rather than a level bet, and he scans a large universe of candidate trades to find the ones requiring little rebalancing, which is why he gravitates to the VIX complex and equity earnings events where retail tech stacks can still compete.

His position sizing is conviction-tiered and survival-weighted: he leans on Kelly-style reasoning to understand how misestimated parameters punish over-betting, then deliberately under-sizes relative to the point-estimate optimum. He builds books as robust combinations of several modest, loosely correlated edges rather than concentrating in one perfected idea. And when a trade "makes him nervous," he treats that discomfort as a signal that edge may be present — selling the option the crowd wants to buy — rather than a reason to flinch.

## Where they are weak

Sinclair's worldview is optimized for harvesting persistent premia, and that optimization has a cost: a premium-seller's mind can systematically underweight the genuine regime break — the rare event that erases years of collected premium in days. He acknowledges the risk intellectually, but a critic would note that his entire frame predisposes him to treat tail events as the price of doing business rather than as the thing that defines survival. His "few true sources of edge" stance, while disciplined, can also lead him to under-rate structural shifts — the explosion of 0DTE flows, dealer gamma positioning, changing ETP plumbing — that quietly alter the very premia he depends on.

His expertise is also narrow by design. He is world-class on options, volatility, and bet sizing, and far less natural on discretionary macro, fundamental equity selection, or illiquid private markets. And his belief-based conviction sizing, for all its candor about the limits of formulas, ultimately rests on calibrated personal judgment — which under overconfidence or bias can drift away from the very statistical discipline he preaches.

## How to summon them

Summon Sinclair when a volatility, variance-premium, or options-income strategy needs a brutally honest edge audit — when the question is whether a claimed advantage is a real phenomenon or a backtest artifact. He is the right voice for sizing option books against Kelly and estimation error, for evaluating VIX-complex, VIX ETP, term-structure, and 0DTE trade ideas, and for separating a genuine implied-realized opportunity from the naive "vol is high, sell it" reflex. Bring him in early to ask "what edge are we actually harvesting, and can it survive the regime changing?" — and expect him to favor robust, survivable combinations over the elegant, over-fitted machine.
