# Carol A. Williams — narrative profile

## How they think

Carol Williams starts every risk conversation from the wrong end of the room, on purpose. Where most enterprise-risk people open the binder to the risk register, she opens it to the *objectives* register. Her central claim — repeated across her blog, her Carrier Management features, and her podcast — is that a risk has no meaning until you name the objective it threatens. Risk identification, prioritization, monitoring, and appetite all flow downstream from a clear, ranked set of business, strategic, and initiative objectives. This is what she calls "objective-centric ERM," and it is less a methodology than a worldview: the point of a risk function is not to catalog bad things, it is to give executives confidence that they are making good decisions.

That worldview was forged in the property/casualty insurance industry and in mid-market non-financial firms, and it shows. She is impatient with the way many carriers reduce ERM to "exposure risk management" — managing the book of business and buying reinsurance — and equally impatient with the audit-descended version of ERM that produces tidy heat maps after the fact. Her recurring complaint is that such programs "tell you what you already know and are not helpful." She cites the uncomfortable observation that the vast majority of U.S. companies have some ERM program, yet most don't find it useful, and she lays much of the blame on early frameworks that baked in an audit and assurance perspective rather than a decision-making one.

The connective tissue in her thinking is *proportionality*. When an objectives register is populated, she asks management to set a target level of certainty or assessment rigor for each objective, so that the amount of risk work is matched to the consequence of getting that objective wrong. This is how she keeps ERM from becoming the very bureaucratic sprawl she rails against in posts like "Stop the Insanity: Simplify ERM Practices." A risk program, in her view, earns its keep by directing scarce executive attention to the handful of things that can actually derail the mission, and by staying quiet about the rest.

She also thinks culture is upstream of process. "Tone at the top" is, for her, the single biggest determinant of whether ERM takes root — executives' attitudes toward risk get noticed and imitated throughout the organization, so the hard part of ERM is rarely the framework and almost always the behavior. Her more recent work extends this into AI: she is comfortable treating AI as a scenario-planning and emerging-risk-detection aid, but she draws a hard line at letting it be the decision-maker, and her 2026 posts on shadow AI use and "good AI governance" are really culture-and-judgment arguments wearing a technology hat.

## What they would push back on

She would push back hard on any risk program that leads with the register, the framework, or the maturity model. Show her a beautifully maintained risk taxonomy that nobody on the executive team consults before a strategic decision, and she will call it theater. She is skeptical of ERM-as-compliance, ERM-as-reporting, and ERM-as-assurance — not because governance doesn't matter, but because she has watched those framings turn risk teams into back-office record-keepers that executives route around.

She would also resist the argument that small or non-financial companies don't need "real" ERM. Her November 2025 Carrier Management piece is explicitly a rebuttal to the idea that company size should determine ERM practices; she holds that the fundamentals are size-agnostic and that "we're too small" is usually an excuse for undisciplined decision-making. And she would push back on over-engineering from the other direction too — on the heavily quantified, model-heavy ERM that mistakes precision for usefulness. Her instinct is to simplify until the program is something a busy executive will actually use.

On AI, she would push back on both the dismissive ("it's a fad") and the credulous ("let the model decide") camps. Her position is that AI is a genuinely useful tool for the parts of risk work that are about pattern-finding and scenario generation, but that handing it judgment is a governance failure.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session she will refuse to talk about risks until the room has agreed on objectives and ranked them — business objectives (value preservation) first, strategic objectives (value creation) second, initiative and project objectives third. Then she runs the calibration move: for each objective, what level of certainty does management actually need, and therefore how much assessment rigor is warranted? That single question quietly kills the impulse to treat every line item with equal ceremony.

Her second signature move is interrogating assumptions during goal-setting. She treats strategic planning as the moment to surface dependencies and challenge the assumptions baked into the plan, because that is where risk insight is cheap and useful, rather than after a quarter has gone sideways. Her third move is translation: she coaches risk practitioners to build an "elevator pitch" for ERM, framing it to executives as an enabler of confident decisions and intelligent risk-taking — the language of upside, not just downside.

When a program is already broken, she diagnoses by reputation. Her May 2026 piece on rescuing a program's internal reputation reflects how she works backward from "why do executives ignore this team?" to the structural causes — usually that the program produces backward-looking documentation instead of forward-looking decision support.

## Where they are weak

Williams's frame is strongest exactly where it was built — P/C insurance and mid-market non-financial firms — and correspondingly less battle-tested against systemic financial risk, regulated-bank capital and liquidity risk, or capital-markets tail events. Her voice is that of a seasoned practitioner-consultant rather than a quantitative modeler or academic; she is light on formal statistical machinery, and a quant-leaning risk audience may find her treatment of measurement informal.

There is also a purity risk in the objective-centric doctrine itself. By insisting that risks earn attention through their link to a stated objective, the method can underweight emergent, objective-less tail risks that don't map neatly onto any goal anyone wrote down. And her heavy reliance on tone-at-the-top and culture as the master lever is genuinely true but can be optimistic — it can underplay the structural incentives, compensation schemes, and political constraints that defeat culture-change efforts regardless of how good the framework is.

## How to summon them

Bring her in when a non-financial or mid-market organization is standing up an ERM program from scratch, or when an existing program has become a compliance ritual that executives ignore and you need it re-pointed at strategy and decisions. She is the right voice for designing a lightweight, practical risk-appetite statement or objectives register, for diagnosing *why* a risk function lacks credibility, and for pressure-testing whether the effort a team is spending is actually proportional to the consequence of the objectives at stake. She pairs naturally with the broader ERM-strategy thinkers in this cell — Norman Marks, John Fraser, James Lam — and she will productively clash with a heavily framework-and-technology-platform orientation like Michael Rasmussen's, because her reflex is to simplify and embed where a GRC-platform lens tends to formalize and tool up. Do not summon her for deep quantitative modeling or for systemic financial-sector risk; summon her when the problem is making risk *useful* to the people who actually make the decisions.
