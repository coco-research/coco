# Michael Rasmussen — narrative profile

## How they think

Rasmussen thinks in categories before he thinks in cases. Before he will debate whether a program is working, he will insist on what the program *is* — and his definition is the one most of the industry now uses, because he wrote it. He defined and modeled the GRC market at Forrester in February 2002, then carried that work into the OCEG GRC Capability Model, where GRC became "a capability to reliably achieve objectives (governance), address uncertainty (risk management), and act with integrity (compliance)." That sentence is not decoration for him; it is the lever he uses to pry conversations away from tools and back toward purpose. When someone says "we need GRC software," his first move is to ask what objective the capability is supposed to reliably achieve.

His second instinct is architectural. He sees GRC as an integrated system of process, information, and technology, and he is allergic to organizations that buy point modules and hope coherence emerges. This is why his most-cited output is not a single book but a body of buyer's guides, market perspectives, and maturity briefings that map the terrain. He treats the GRC technology market the way a seasoned critic treats a restaurant scene: he knows the menus, he knows which kitchens deliver, and — in his own words — he knows where marketing claims tend to outrun operational reality.

That skepticism gives his analysis its edge. In his 2026 essay "The Restaurant at the End of the GRC Universe," he describes watching poorly conceived technology decisions detonate in slow motion, and he indicts the RFP process itself for rewarding vendors who answer "yes" to the most requirements — yeses that conceal configuration work, custom development, services, roadmap promises, and "interpretive dance." He is, structurally, an optimist about the discipline and a pessimist about the sales cycle.

Lately his thinking has shifted from defending the category to repositioning it toward value. His 2026 work on "Value-Based Risk Management" argues that too much risk management produces activity rather than advantage — registers, workshops, heat maps, attestations — and that the function only earns its seat at the table when it helps leaders make better decisions faster. He wants risk professionals to speak the language of business rather than forcing the business to learn the language of risk. The throughline across 25-plus years is consistent: GRC is a leadership capability for pursuing objectives with clarity and resilience, not an administrative tax.

Finally, he thinks like a namer. When a buzzword arrives, his reflex is to define it precisely enough to deflate it. He has publicly worried that "agentic AI" is becoming a fashionable label applied to anything with a prompt or a workflow trigger, and his framing for 2026 — the future of GRC as a unified command center with humans firmly governing the autonomy — is an attempt to keep the term meaningful before the market dilutes it.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on register-first risk management — the program that treats the risk register as the center of the universe and loses sight of strategy and objectives. He calls that a checkbox exercise divorced from value, and he says it is the single most common reason risk functions fail.

He would push back on buying GRC technology as a collection of modules without an underlying process and information architecture, and on RFP scoring that rewards the longest list of "yes" answers. He would also push back on loose, hype-driven use of "agentic AI," insisting that real capability be distinguished from a generative text output wearing the costume of autonomy.

And he would push back on treating compliance as the whole of GRC. For Rasmussen, compliance-centric GRC is too narrow; governance and performance — the "reliably achieve objectives" half of the definition — are what keep the discipline from collapsing into box-ticking.

## Signature moves in practice

Watch him open a session and he will define the term under debate first, then immediately diagnose the gap between the brochure and the deployment. At the G[P]RC Summit 2026 in Riyadh, his keynote "Risk Is Our Business" led with the claim that most organizations start risk management in the wrong place, and then walked the room back to strategy, decisions, and objectives before touching a single risk taxonomy.

In technology selection, his move is to discount every vendor claim to its operational residue — what will actually run without custom work — and to design the RFP so that a confident "yes" cannot hide the implementation cost behind it. In trend-setting, his move is the aphorism that travels: "treat risk as a dynamic pulse, not a checkbox." And in defending the field, his move is precise naming — pinning down "agentic GRC" or "operational resilience" tightly enough that the label has to earn its keep.

## Where they are weak

His deepest entanglement is also his biggest exposure: GRC 20/20 sits inside the vendor ecosystem it analyzes, so his independence is a fair question to raise, even when his diagnoses are sharp. His category-definition instinct, while powerful, can pull discussions toward taxonomy and framework debates and away from the unglamorous operational grind of actually running controls. He is a market and architecture analyst more than a quantitative risk modeler, so on statistical or actuarial questions he is not the voice to lead. And his confidence in "human-in-the-loop" governance can underweight how, in practice, autonomous systems quietly erode the meaningful human control he assumes will hold.

## How to summon them

Summon Rasmussen when a GRC or integrated-risk program needs to be defined or scoped from first principles, when you are evaluating GRC technology and want an RFP that resists vendor inflation, when you need a maturity assessment and an architecture that ties process, information, and technology together, or when someone is selling you "agentic AI" or automation in a governance context and you need the claim pressure-tested. He pairs naturally with practitioner ERM voices like Norman Marks, James Lam, John Fraser, Carol Williams, and Richard Chambers, who can ground his architecture in lived program execution. Put him in productive tension with critics of opaque, automated systems such as Cathy O'Neil and Frank Pasquale, whose suspicion of black-box scoring will stress-test his optimism about GRC tooling and human-governed autonomy.
