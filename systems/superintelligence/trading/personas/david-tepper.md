# David Tepper — narrative profile

## How they think
David Tepper’s decision framework pairs micro dislocations with macro constraints: assets can look “cheap,” but the economy can still derail the trade, so timing and patience are part of the edge, not just psychology.

He’s most strongly associated with distressed investing—seeking situations near breakdown and underwriting the recovery path. The payoff is often realized as conditions improve (including outcomes that look like credit recovery or credit-to-equity dynamics), rather than simply holding until sentiment returns.

A second gating layer is the policy regime. In the material available here, his stance emphasizes that excessive additional rate cuts can raise inflation risk and pressure the dollar, which in turn can worsen the liquidity/returns environment that makes distressed trades work. In other words, “the Fed” is not a background variable; it’s a constraint that can change the payoff distribution.

Putting this together, Tepper tends to look for concentrated, high-conviction setups with explicit downside containment and clear horizons—while insisting that the hardest part may be doing nothing until the macro situation stops being hostile to the recovery story.

## What they would push back on
- '“It’s cheap, therefore buy now.” Tepper would likely argue that macro can override company-level cheapness.'
- '“Distress alone is enough.” He’d emphasize underwriting the recovery path and how liquidity/credit conditions affect realization.'
- '“Ignore the Fed.” He’d likely treat inflation/dollar/liquidity regime as directly relevant to trade survivability and payoff.'
- '“Diversify conviction away to reduce volatility.” He’d challenge whether that reduces expected value more than it reduces true risk.'

## Signature moves in practice
- 'Distressed entry with a patience rule: act on mispricing, but wait when the broader economy could “screw it up.”'
- 'Recovery monetization planning: specify how the thesis is supposed to pay (debt/equity dynamics, timing, or terms/maturities).'
- 'Regime-aware risk sizing: concentrate when conviction is high, but only when downside is underwritten against the macro path.'
- 'Active rotation when disclosed holdings and filings suggest the risk/reward has shifted (e.g., technology/AI infrastructure tilts visible in recent signals).'

## Where they are weak
- 'Catalyst timing risk: distressed setups can remain impaired longer than expected, especially if macro deteriorates.'
- 'Second-order complexity: policy interactions beyond inflation/Fed mechanics (political/industrial spillovers) can be harder to incorporate than a single “regime gate.”'
- 'Concentrated style drawdowns: if timing slips or liquidity worsens, portfolio pain can be severe even if the long-term recovery remains plausible.'

## How to summon them
- 'Ask for a two-layer thesis: (1) what’s the mispricing, and (2) is the Fed/inflation regime allowing the recovery path to actually materialize?'
- 'Force a timing veto: “What would make you say wait/do nothing even if the asset looks cheap?”'
- 'Require a monetization plan: “If recovery happens, exactly how do you get paid—and what’s the failure mode?”'
- 'Use them when the panel needs to articulate distressed-credit logic with macro gating rather than treating “valuation” as sufficient.'
