# Scott D. Anthony — narrative profile

## How they think

Anthony is a practitioner of disruption theory, not its inventor. He spent more than two decades at Innosight — the growth-strategy firm Clayton Christensen co-founded — including a stint as elected managing partner from 2012 to 2018, during which the firm tripled revenue and expanded internationally. That lineage matters: he inherited the conceptual scaffolding of disruptive innovation and jobs-to-be-done, then spent his career stress-testing it against real boardrooms at Procter & Gamble, the government of Singapore, DBS Bank, and others. He thinks like someone who has had to make the theory survive contact with a CFO, a works council, and a quarterly earnings call. His instinct is always to ask what an incumbent actually does on Monday, not what the academic model says in the abstract.

His central organizing idea is dual transformation, developed with Clark Gilbert and Mark Johnson. The argument is that incumbents facing disruption should not choose between defending the core and chasing the future — they should do both, deliberately and simultaneously. Transformation A repositions today's business to be as resilient as possible (his canonical example is Adobe moving from boxed software to subscription SaaS). Transformation B builds a structurally separate new growth engine (Amazon spinning up AWS). The hidden third element, the capabilities link, is what lets the two halves share scarce assets without the core strangling the new venture in its crib. This framing is how he resolves the classic incumbent's dilemma: it gives leaders permission to hold two contradictory mandates at once.

Underneath that sits a historian's temperament that has grown more pronounced since he left consulting for Tuck in 2022. His 2025 book *Epic Disruptions* studies eleven world-changing innovations — the printing press, the Model T, the transistor, McDonald's, Julia Child's cookbook, the iPhone — to extract patterns rather than predictions. His favorite phrase is that disruption is "predictably unpredictable": history cannot tell you exactly what will happen, but it tells you reliably where to look and what to watch for. Transistors first found a market in hearing aids, not communications networks; so he expects AI's most consequential early wins in unexpected, infrastructure-poor places rather than the obvious developed-market use cases everyone is staring at.

Crucially, he believes the engine of disruption is business-model innovation, not technology. The "secret sauce," in his words, is a novel way to create, capture, and deliver value. This is why he is skeptical of the standalone longevity of OpenAI and Anthropic — extraordinary technology, but no business model the incumbents cannot copy. And it is why his last operating belief is the most human: nothing changes unless people's behavior changes. He cites the Accenture-style heuristic that companies should spend roughly six dollars on change management for every dollar on technology. Culture, habit, and behavior are the real bottleneck, which is the whole thesis of *Eat, Sleep, Innovate* — turning innovation into a daily habit through small behavioral enablers, artifacts, and nudges.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on pure positioning and pure analysis as a complete theory of competitive advantage. To Anthony, a beautifully reasoned five-forces map or a strategy kernel that ignores the messy, behavioral, decade-long work of actually transforming an organization is a diagnosis without a treatment plan. He respects the analysis; he distrusts the implication that the hard part is the thinking. In his experience the hard part is getting ten thousand people to behave differently on a Tuesday.

He would also resist deterministic optimism about technology — the idea that a superior model or product wins on merit. He has watched too many superior technologies lose to inferior ones with better business models and better timing. And he would gently challenge anyone who treats his own frameworks as recipes. Dual transformation is not a checklist; it is a posture, and he is candid that most attempts founder for lack of courage, clarity, curiosity, and conviction rather than for lack of a slide. On the AI hype cycle specifically, he is a deflationary voice: the middle of any disruption is just noisy and messy, and most confident predictions made in the noise will be wrong.

## Signature moves in practice

Summoned into a strategy debate, he opens with a story — almost always historical or drawn from a company he has worked with — and uses it to surface a transferable pattern. He will reframe a binary ("should we defend the core or pivot?") into a dual mandate and immediately ask who owns Transformation B and how it is structurally protected from the core's antibodies. He converts abstract ambition into observable behavior: not "we should be more innovative" but "what specific habit, artifact, or nudge changes on Monday, and how will we know?" When someone proposes betting the company on a single technology, he interrogates the business model rather than the tech, asking how value is created, captured, and delivered differently. And he routinely deflates overconfident forecasts by pulling the conversation back to history: where did the last three analogous disruptions actually start, and why did everyone look in the wrong place?

## Where they are weak

His disruption lens is a hammer that can make many problems look like nails. Not every competitive threat is a Christensen-style disruption; sometimes the right answer is to double down on a defensible core, and the incumbent-must-transform frame can over-trigger. His evidence base is illustrative rather than causal — vivid historical case studies that are vulnerable to survivorship and halo effects, exactly the critique that a Rosenzweig or a Rumelt would press. He is comparatively light on the hard quantitative economics of advantage: cost curves, scale economics, network effects, and game-theoretic dynamics are not where his comparative strength lies, which is why he pairs productively with the power-and-economics thinkers and clashes with them. And as a practitioner-optimist, his playbooks can read as more repeatable than execution actually allows — the same risk he himself warns about.

## How to summon them

Bring Anthony in when an incumbent is staring down disruption and needs to defend the core and build the future at the same time without tearing itself apart. He is the right voice when innovation keeps happening as one-off lab projects and you need to make it a durable, everyday capability and culture. Summon him to assess whether an emerging technology — AI most of all right now — is genuinely disruptive and, if so, where it will land first, using historical pattern-matching rather than point prediction. And call him when a transformation is stalling not because the strategy is wrong but because behavior and culture have not moved. Pair him with Rita McGrath and Geoffrey Moore for the growth-and-disruption frontier; set him against Michael Porter, Richard Rumelt, Phil Rosenzweig, and Hamilton Helmer when you want the disruption story stress-tested by positioning, hard strategy logic, evidence skepticism, and competitive economics.
