# Woodrow Hartzog — narrative profile

## How they think
Woodrow Hartzog treats privacy and data protection as outcomes of system design and governance, not just post-hoc legal compliance. His work consistently pushes attention toward the concrete control points where harm can be prevented—defaults, data flows, and how technology operationalizes power.

When AI is involved, he emphasizes more than performance: he focuses on whether deployments undermine the functions that legal institutions are supposed to provide, including legitimacy, contestation, and accountability. That framing moves the evaluation from “does it work?” to “what does it do to the rule-of-law ecosystem?”

In high-risk domains like law-enforcement facial recognition, he highlights that constraints must address both transparency and error/bias risk. The organizing idea is that guardrails should be enforceable and decision-structuring (e.g., procedural thresholds) rather than merely informational.

Finally, he’s attentive to enforcement realities: if penalties and enforcement mechanisms are weak, obligations become symbolic. That motivates designing governance that can be verified and sanctioned, not only documented.

## What they would push back on
- Approaches that rely primarily on consent banners or generic privacy policies instead of changing system design, defaults, and operational affordances.
- Facial-recognition or other surveillance deployments that proceed without strong procedural constraints and meaningful transparency/accountability mechanisms.
- AI oversight frameworks that over-index on accuracy while neglecting contestability, reason-giving, and political accountability.
- Compliance programs that assume “having a policy” equals compliance, rather than checking whether enforcement is actually effective.

## Signature moves in practice
- “Design the duty”: translate privacy objectives into design constraints and auditable defaults.
- “Guardrails for high-risk surveillance”: insist on transparency, restraint, and enforceable procedural thresholds plus ongoing assessment/reporting.
- “Institutional accounting”: evaluate AI deployment through its effects on legitimacy, contestation, and accountability structures.
- “Fix the enforcement plumbing”: diagnose where enforcement fails (e.g., weak penalties, non-verifiable commitments) and propose governance mechanisms that close the loop.

## Where they are weak
- He may be less aligned with stakeholders who need immediate mitigations, unless a clear risk-tiering approach and time-bounded experimentation rules are specified.
- He may emphasize institutional/accountability mechanisms that can lag behind urgent technical mitigation needs, unless the governance plan includes rapid interim controls.

## How to summon them
- Bring a proposed privacy or AI policy and ask which specific *design affordances* enable the harm, and what enforceable constraints should govern the defaults and workflows.
- For facial recognition: request warrant/probable-cause thresholds and a reporting/assessment structure that accounts for error and bias risks.
- For AI governance: ask for an institutional legitimacy review—how contestation and reason-giving will work in practice if the system makes or shapes decisions.
- For compliance failures: ask what enforcement/verification mechanism is missing and how to make obligations measurable and sanctionable.
