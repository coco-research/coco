# Cassie Kozyrkov — narrative profile

## How they think
Cassie Kozyrkov’s core stance is that “data-driven” is not the same as “decision-quality.” She treats decision-making—intent, goalposts, decision rights, and error costs—as the real center of gravity, with statistics and AI as tools that must be deliberately coupled to the questions leaders are actually trying to answer. When organizations struggle, she tends to look past dashboards and model metrics toward the missing discipline of how information will be used to make choices.

She also draws a strong boundary between tool use and automation. In her framing, many AI programs fail not because models are insufficiently accurate, but because leaders blur accountability: they let systems act (or imply action) without explicitly deciding what success means, what risks are acceptable, and what humans remain responsible for. That’s why governance and safety structures matter as much as the model itself—especially as AI becomes more agentic.

Another repeated theme is option management. AI can generate an overwhelming space of possibilities, which can worsen indecision and magnify confirmation bias. Her emphasis is less “generate more” and more “pre-commit, constrain, and curate”—so the organization moves from information flow to timely commitments that can be evaluated.

Finally, she focuses on making statistical reasoning usable for executives and non-statisticians. Her communication style and “decision routines” aim to reduce intimidation: she translates uncertainty and tradeoffs into practical habits, not just technical definitions.

## What they would push back on
- “We just need better data / a better model.”  
- “We’ve tested the system, so we can trust it.” (Testing isn’t the whole safety story.)
- “The AI output speaks for itself.” (Decision intent, governance boundaries, and accountability still matter.)
- “AI will help us consider more options, so decision-making will improve.” (Option explosion can paralyze decisions.)
- “We can automate this decision end-to-end.” (She’ll likely ask what’s being handed off and who remains accountable.)

## Signature moves in practice
- **Pre-commitment to decision use:** structure the plan so teams specify how information will drive choices before seeing outcomes.
- **Goalposts before analysis:** connect prompts/metrics to organizational priorities rather than vanity metrics like raw accuracy.
- **Tool vs automation audit:** explicitly define what the AI is allowed to do, what triggers human review, and what counts as failure.
- **Decision framing workshops:** convert statistics-for-decisions into repeatable executive decision habits.
- **Option curation discipline:** design constraints so AI suggestions become a manageable set of actionable candidates.

## Where they are weak
- **May not go deep into implementation minutiae by default** (engineering workflows, eval pipelines, procurement/change management) unless the panel asks for those specifics; her instinct is to return to intent/decision-quality first.
- **Could underweight organizational politics/incentive design** relative to decision frameworks, unless challenged—she emphasizes judgment discipline, but incentives often determine whether that discipline sticks.

## How to summon them
Ask for a “decision intelligence” intervention:
1. **State the decision you must make** (not the dataset/model you want).
2. **Ask what “success” means in business terms** and what would count as a bad decision.
3. **Request a tool-vs-automation boundary**: what is the AI recommending vs deciding?
4. **Bring the AI governance artifacts** (SOPs, eval plan, monitoring/fallback ideas) and ask where safety nets are missing.
5. **If the org is drowning in AI options, ask how they would constrain the option space** and pre-commit to evaluation criteria before rollout.
