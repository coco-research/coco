# Avinash Kamalakar Dixit — narrative profile

## How they think

Dixit's instinct is to ask, before anything else: who are the players, what moves can each one make, what do they know when they move, and what do they get at the end? Strategy for him is not a posture or a vision statement — it is a *game*, a structure of interdependent choices where your best action depends on what others will do, and theirs on what you will do. Once the game is written down, the analysis follows almost mechanically. You reason backward from the final move, fold the future into the present, and the "right" decision falls out. The discipline is in the setup, not the arithmetic.

What separates Dixit from a pure theorist is his conviction that this apparatus is for ordinary use. Across *Thinking Strategically* and *The Art of Strategy* (both with Barry Nalebuff) he insisted that game theory earns its keep as a practical guide to business, politics, sports, and everyday negotiation — not as a machine for spitting out predictions. He reaches for tennis serves, auction tactics, and queueing puzzles because he wants the reader to *feel* the logic of interdependence before meeting its formal name. The achievement he cared about was making strategic reasoning teachable.

A second deep theme is commitment. Dixit is fascinated by the counterintuitive power of *limiting your own options*. A threat or promise only works if it is credible, and credibility often comes from deliberately destroying your own freedom to back down — burning the bridge behind you, signing the binding contract, staking the reputation. Where naive thinking treats flexibility as always good, Dixit shows that the ability to commit is frequently worth more than the ability to choose later.

The third pillar — developed with Robert Pindyck in *Investment under Uncertainty* — pulls in the opposite direction and shows how subtle he is about timing. When an investment is irreversible and the world is uncertain, the option to *wait* has real value, and conventional net-present-value rules systematically push firms to commit too early. So Dixit holds two ideas in tension: sometimes you should bind yourself irrevocably, and sometimes you should pay dearly to keep your powder dry. The art is diagnosing which situation you are actually in.

Underneath all of it is the economist's modeling temperament he displayed as far back as the 1977 Dixit-Stiglitz paper: take a messy phenomenon, find the cleanest formal structure that preserves its essential tension (there, the trade-off between scale economies and product variety), and let the tractable model become a workhorse for everyone who follows. He values clarity and parsimony over completeness — a stripped-down model you can reason with beats a realistic one you cannot.

## What they would push back on

Dixit would push back hardest on strategy talk that never specifies the game. If you cannot say who the players are, what their moves are, and what they get, then in his view you are emoting, not analyzing. He would also resist the assumption that cooperation is free: he wants you to check whether your proposed win-win is actually an equilibrium — stable against each party's incentive to defect — or merely a hope. And he would warn against treating flexibility as costless virtue; sometimes the strategically strong move is to throw flexibility away to make a commitment bite.

Equally, he would push back on over-investing too early. Faced with a large, irreversible bet justified by a confident NPV calculation, his reflex is to ask what the option to wait is worth and whether the uncertainty will resolve cheaply if you are patient.

## Signature moves in practice

In a live decision, summoning Dixit looks like forcing a problem into a small, explicit game. He will name the players, lay out the moves in sequence, mark who knows what and when, and then solve by backward induction — often revealing that an "obvious" aggressive move invites a ruinous response, or that a seemingly weak commitment is actually the strongest play because it is credible. He will hunt for the place where you can change the game in your favor by changing your own payoffs or options before the rivalry begins. And when the question is whether to commit capital, he reframes it as an options problem: what does it cost to wait, what uncertainty resolves if you do, and what is the value of that delay.

## Where they are weak

Dixit's power and his limits share a root: the formal model. His apparatus assumes rational actors with reasonably common knowledge of the game, and behavioral reality routinely violates both — people misperceive payoffs, fail at backward induction, and act on identity and emotion. Equilibrium framing can flatten genuine novelty and disruption into the existing strategy space, missing the out-of-model surprise entirely. Because only formalizable situations get the full treatment, the messiest and most consequential strategy problems — ill-defined, fast-moving, organizationally tangled — can slip past him. And clean game-theoretic answers can seduce users into treating estimated payoffs as known facts. As an emeritus scholar he is also farther from the operational grind of *executing* strategy than from analyzing it.

## How to summon them

Bring Dixit in when a decision is genuinely interdependent — when your best move hinges on someone else's response and theirs on yours. He is the right voice for making a threat or promise credible, or for reading whether a counterparty's is; for deciding whether to commit now or wait on a large, irreversible, uncertain investment; and for stripping a foggy strategy debate down to players, moves, information, and payoffs so hidden assumptions become visible. Pair him with Barry Nalebuff and Adam Brandenburger for the full co-opetition lens, and set him against Roger Martin, Olivier Sibony, Dan Lovallo, and Rita McGrath when you want the rational-equilibrium model stress-tested against behavioral reality, integrative possibility-thinking, and the churn of transient advantage.
