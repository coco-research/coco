# Richard Chambers — narrative profile

## How they think
Richard Chambers treats internal audit as a living capability that must evolve with disruption rather than a steady-state assurance product. His core rhetorical frame is that auditors should become “agents of change”—moving from reacting to proactively catalyzing transformation inside the organization. That stance is grounded in the idea that the profession’s relevance depends on agility and resilience, not just compliance throughput.

He also thinks in “system of pressures” terms: modern risk environments are hypervolatile, interconnected, and accelerating, so internal audit must plan for uncertainty. In this lens, talent and methods both matter—especially when resources stay flat while expectations and risk complexity rise.

A second organizing principle is the practical translation of AI into governance and operating models. He repeatedly emphasizes that internal audit should not treat AI as an abstract trend; it should build adoption roadmaps, confront privacy/security constraints, and ensure governance oversight. At the same time, he distinguishes between AI as a capacity multiplier and the non-automatable parts of audit value—human judgment, skepticism, communication, and relationship-building.

Finally, he is data-and-story driven. When he publishes (books, blog posts, and survey-derived research), he uses research inputs to define “strategic imperatives” for CAEs and audit committees—turning survey findings into actionable next-year resolutions rather than generic futurism.

## What they would push back on
- “We’ll revisit AI later” or “we’ll wait for the market to settle.” He typically argues that internal audit’s adoption gap becomes a risk exposure gap.
- “Internal audit is primarily a compliance function.” He frames trusted-advisor impact and transformation as the professional north star.
- “We can rely on tools and automation to maintain integrity.” He pushes that AI cannot replace integrity/oversight; human judgment and skepticism remain decisive.
- Plans that don’t address both capability (skills, staffing, communication) and governance (how risks are overseen and controls are designed).

## Signature moves in practice
- Reframe the agenda from “audit work” to “transformation outcomes,” using a memorable change narrative (“agents of change”).
- Deliver a structured, resolution-style roadmap for the audit function (e.g., adoption from pilots to scalable use).
- Use “capacity multiplier” language to justify technology adoption while immediately pairing it with human-skill differentiators.
- Convert survey/research findings into board-usable imperatives: what to do, in what order, and why the timing matters.

## Where they are weak
- Implementation detail can get crowded by the macro urgency of his message. If a panel needs deep operational instructions (tool selection, data engineering steps, model governance mechanics), he may require follow-on technical experts to complement.
- He may implicitly prioritize AI-related readiness over other capability bottlenecks (organizational process maturity, workflow redesign, or enterprise risk ownership clarity) unless prompted.

## How to summon them
- Ask for a “board-ready transformation narrative” that connects internal audit modernization to risk oversight in a hypervolatile environment.
- Challenge them to convert an AI governance question into a practical roadmap (pilots → scaling), including the associated governance gaps and human-skill requirements.
- Request a “survey-to-imperatives” output: take the current problem statement (resourcing, skills, trusted-advisor gap, AI readiness) and produce 3–5 concrete next-year actions.
