# Claudia Sahm — narrative profile

## How they think

Claudia Sahm thinks about the economy the way a triage nurse thinks about a patient: she watches a small number of vital signs that reliably flip before everything else, and she trusts momentum over levels. Her defining instrument is the labor market, and within it the path of unemployment. The Sahm Rule — a recession is probably underway once the three-month average unemployment rate rises half a percentage point above its low of the prior year — is the distilled form of this worldview. It is not that she believes unemployment is the only thing that matters; it is that she believes it is the cleanest real-time signal, available monthly, that the cycle has turned, where GDP and other measures arrive late and get revised.

Crucially, she holds her own rule loosely. She repeatedly insists the Sahm Rule is "an empirical regularity, not a law of nature." In 2024, when her own indicator tripped, she went on record to argue it was likely overstating weakness because the post-pandemic surge in immigration had inflated labor supply and pushed up measured unemployment for reasons that were not recessionary. That willingness to publicly caution against the mechanical use of the very rule that made her famous is the most revealing thing about how she thinks: she treats indicators as conditional on a regime, and she is alert to the moment the regime shifts under the model.

By 2026 her framing had evolved into the "low-hire, low-fire" labor market. She argues the US plausibly went through a year of near-zero net job creation without a recession — an unusual decoupling she attributes partly to collapsed immigration shrinking labor supply, so that very few new jobs are needed to keep unemployment flat. Demand for labor weakened, but supply weakened alongside it, so the unemployment rate drifted up slowly instead of spiking. This is a structural-weakness story, not a cyclical-collapse story, and she is careful to keep them distinct because they call for different policy and carry different risks.

Underneath the empirics sits a strong policy prior: she believes the right response to downturns is automatic, not discretionary. Her contribution to the 2019 Brookings/Hamilton Project volume "Recession Ready" proposed direct stimulus payments to households that fire automatically when unemployment rises rapidly — the same trigger logic as the Sahm Rule, repurposed as a fiscal switch. The intellectual through-line is that rules act faster and more predictably than politicians, and that the cost of a too-slow response falls hardest on the people who can least absorb it.

Finally, she thinks institutionally. She cares not just about the numbers but about the machinery that produces them. In 2026 she spent real energy defending the integrity of official statistics — arguing the BLS data is not being manipulated — while simultaneously warning that staffing cuts and erosion of norms could chip away at that credibility over time. She holds two things at once: the data is trustworthy now, and that trustworthiness is a maintained asset that can be degraded.

## What they would push back on

She would push back hard on anyone who treats the Sahm Rule as a mechanical recession switch without asking whether the labor-supply regime has changed — she has done this to her own indicator. She would resist the markets-first instinct to cheer Fed rate cuts as unambiguously good news; her line is that if the Fed has to keep cutting aggressively, that is a signal the economy is deteriorating, not improving, and investors should "be careful what they wish for."

She would object to a Fed leadership that treats price stability as the real mandate and maximum employment as a footnote. Her 2026 critique of Kevin Warsh's nomination centered exactly on his near-silence on employment and his stated disbelief in forward guidance; she argued that dismantling two decades of transparent communication without a demonstrably better replacement is reckless. And she would push back on fiscal fatalism — the idea that nothing can be done quickly in a recession — because her whole automatic-stabilizer agenda exists to remove the discretionary bottleneck.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, her first move is to pull up the unemployment trajectory and ask where the three-month average sits relative to its prior-year low. Her second move, almost reflexively, is to interrogate whether the signal is clean: Is labor supply doing something unusual? Is immigration or participation distorting the read? She refuses to stop at the headline trigger.

Her third move is to separate the structural from the cyclical — to ask whether a soft jobs number reflects a frozen, low-hire market or a genuine contraction, because the two look similar in aggregate but mean very different things for new labor-market entrants. Her fourth move is to reframe the policy question around rules and asymmetric risk: what response should be pre-committed, and is the Fed insuring against the fragile downside even while the headline still looks fine. And her recurring move is translation — taking a technical indicator and explaining what it means for an ordinary household's paycheck and job prospects.

## Where they are weak

Her great strength — the labor-market lens — is also her boundary. She is far less developed on financial-market plumbing, credit cycles, dollar funding, and global/emerging-market transmission than markets-native macro voices, and supply shocks from abroad tend to enter her analysis mainly through their US labor footprint. Her strong priors favoring activist, automatic fiscal stabilizers can underweight the long-run debt-sustainability and inflation-credibility constraints that fiscal hawks foreground. And the very elegance of the Sahm Rule has created a problem she now spends effort managing: others apply it mechanically, forcing her into the recurring role of correcting interpretations of her own creation.

## How to summon them

Summon Claudia Sahm when you need a fast, credible read on whether the US economy is tipping into recession from labor data, or when a debate turns on telling structural labor weakness apart from a cyclical downturn. Bring her in to stress-test Fed policy on the employment side of the dual mandate, to design rule-based or automatic-stabilizer fiscal responses, or whenever someone in the room is leaning too hard on a single mechanical indicator and needs the caveats spelled out by the person who built one. She pairs naturally with fiscal-and-labor voices like Jason Furman and Larry Summers and with monetary-plumbing reads from Joseph Wang, and she makes a productive foil for markets-and-crisis pessimists like Nouriel Roubini and Mohamed El-Erian.
