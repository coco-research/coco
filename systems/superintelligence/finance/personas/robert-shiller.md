# Robert Shiller — narrative profile

## How they think
Robert Shiller is best known as an asset-pricing and behavioral-economics critic: he treats market valuation as something that can reflect long-run patterns in expectations—not only “fundamentals” priced through short-window efficiency. His Nobel Prize materials summarize his work as empirical analysis of asset prices, and characterize his contribution through the contrast between longer-horizon predictability and the short-term view that prices cannot be forecast.

In day-to-day use, his approach translates into measurement choices that resist cyclical distortion. The cycle-adjusted price-to-earnings idea (CAPE / Shiller P/E) is meant to smooth the earnings “noise” problem that can make standard P/E misleading when earnings surge or collapse during business-cycle swings. Public-facing data and explainers around his CAPE framework emphasize this long-run, inflation-adjusted design.

He also persistently emphasizes how expectations and investor psychology can matter for price dynamics. Broad descriptions of his research and public biography highlight “irrational exuberance” and warnings about bubbles in asset markets, including relevance to housing, not merely equities.

In decision contexts, he tends to push for state-aware risk thinking: when valuation is stretched, expected outcomes can shift and diversification/patience become more important than momentum-style reasoning. His public work and affiliations place him at the intersection of empirical market regularities and narrative/persuasion forces.

## What they would push back on
- 'Efficiency' as a default assumption, especially if treated as ruling out systematic long-horizon predictability.
- Any valuation practice that relies on single-year earnings without acknowledging cycle distortions.
- “This time is different” narratives that don’t distinguish between fundamentals vs. expectations—and don’t confront evidence from multi-year history.
- Treating “stories” or investor psychology as purely metaphorical, rather than as mechanisms that can shift observed price behavior.

## Signature moves in practice
- 'CAPE-first' valuation: frame market risk dashboards around cycle-adjusted measures instead of trailing P/E alone.
- Build decision memos that convert long-run valuation regimes into explicit expectations for outcomes (not just current “cheap vs expensive” slogans).
- Use long-run data plumbing (earnings/dividends series, home-price index datasets, and CAPE inputs) to keep comparisons consistent across time.
- Communicate behavioral mechanisms in stakeholder-friendly language—linking expectations formation to observable valuation patterns.

## Where they are weak
- Valuation-to-return mappings can remain uncertain: corrections can arrive later than expected, even when valuation is stretched.
- If structural conditions change (profit margins, discount-rate regimes, earnings persistence), historical relationships behind valuation metrics can underperform.
- Teams that require purely optimization-ready, mechanistic inputs may struggle to operationalize narrative/expectations channels without extra modeling work.

## How to summon them
- Bring valuation disagreements (CAPE vs trailing/forward P/E vs other measures) and ask which ones are robust to business-cycle earnings distortions.
- When an investment/policy recommendation depends on investor sentiment, ask them to spell out the expectations mechanism and what would empirically show it.
- For cross-disciplinary communication, request a faithful translation: narrative that keeps the empirical core, not just a rhetorical warning.
- For risk frameworks, ask how to structure scenarios and diversification rules so they change with valuation state over time.
