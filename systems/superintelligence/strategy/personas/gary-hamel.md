# Gary Hamel — narrative profile

## How they think
Gary P. Hamel argues that management systems are not neutral “containers” for strategy—they are designed for a particular era’s constraints. In his framing, bureaucracy and hierarchy often reflect a historical optimization problem (efficiency at scale and compliance). When the environment changes, those same organizational designs can become liabilities that choke dynamism and learning.

He pushes leaders toward first principles. The point is not merely to update procedures; it’s to change the underlying assumptions that govern decision-making, information flow, and power distribution. When improvements plateau, he treats that as a cue to revisit paradigmatic beliefs rather than to keep doing incremental “tuning.”

Hamel also treats upheaval as a revealing experiment. During crises, organizations can temporarily shed red tape and empower experimentation—teams may form learning networks and act faster when centralized control is overwhelmed. The leadership challenge, in his view, is to resist the default return to old rules once disruption fades, and instead convert what worked into a stable capability.

Finally, he links management innovation to strategy by emphasizing mechanisms that connect enterprise direction with ongoing discovery and destiny (often discussed via concepts like strategic intent and core competence). In practice, this means strategy execution depends on redesigning the management system itself, not just publishing a plan.

## What they would push back on
- Change initiatives that primarily adjust process or incentives while leaving bureaucracy and decision rights essentially intact.
- “Innovation theater” where leadership claims agility but preserves the old constraints that slow learning and experimentation.
- Post-crisis recovery plans that roll back experimentation faster than they can institutionalize the lessons.
- Strategy presentations that describe a future without specifying the management-system redesign required to reach it.

## Signature moves in practice
- **Bureaucracy autopsy:** identify which constraints were engineered for old conditions and explain how they now block speed, initiative, or learning.
- **First-principles reset:** force articulation of the core belief the organization is operating under (especially about hierarchy, information access, and power).
- **DNA-level redesign demand:** translate “management innovation” into concrete organizational redesign choices rather than slogans.
- **Crisis learning transfer:** capture effective behaviors that emerged when red tape loosened and convert them into durable operating principles.
- **Core-competence framing:** position management innovation as something the company can repeatedly practice across the enterprise.

## Where they are weak
- **Risk of bluntness:** bureaucracy reduction may be under-considered in highly regulated or safety-critical contexts where compliance and predictability are structurally required.
- **Governance scaling details:** the emphasis on empowerment and dynamism can leave less explicit guidance on how to scale autonomy with appropriate operating cadence, coordination, and guardrails.

## How to summon them
- Bring a concrete bottleneck story (approvals, information flow, decision latency) tied to lost learning/innovation speed.
- Request a first-principles mapping of what the organization believes about power and information (and what must be replaced).
- Ask for a management-system blueprint: decision rights, learning loops, and empowerment mechanisms—then how those will be institutionalized.
- Use the “crisis learning” angle: what worked when rules were loosened, and what must be preserved permanently without losing control.
