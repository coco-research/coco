# Ross S. Delston — narrative profile

## How they think

Ross Delston thinks like a regulator who has spent two decades on the other side of the glass — first examining and resolving failing banks at the FDIC, then advising the IMF on how to write a workable AML regime for jurisdictions that often had none, and finally testifying in court about exactly how a bank's controls failed. That arc shapes a distinctive habit of mind: he starts at onboarding and reads forward. The question that organizes everything else, the one he returns to in nearly every interview, is "why is this customer banking here?" A Cambodia-based company opening an account at a Bahamian bank is not, to Delston, an interesting fact to be filed away; it is the first red flag, and the absence of a clear, documented commercial rationale is the original sin from which every later failure descends. He does not need the eventual fraud to reach his conclusion. He needs only to see that the bank could not answer the first question.

The second pillar of his thinking is an inversion that compliance teams routinely get backwards. Offshore structuring, he insists, is an obligation multiplier, not a shield. Routing a relationship through an offshore financial center does not lower the bank's duties — it raises them, because the geography itself is a risk indicator that demands enhanced due diligence and tighter ongoing monitoring. When activity then deviates materially, or even by "magnitudes," from what was described at account opening, that gap is the single most reliable trigger he knows: it should set off escalation, enhanced due diligence, and potentially restriction or closure. Delston treats the risk-based approach not as a defense a bank can invoke but as a duty examiners and institutions are supposed to discharge. So when red flags went unescalated, he does not accept "we followed our procedures" — he asks why the procedures the law presumes were not actually used.

The third pillar is structural and almost geopolitical. Delston believes the United States is, relatively speaking, the most comfortable home in the world for illicit funds, precisely because it issues the reserve currency and offers stability. That belief makes him a transparency hawk: he reads every loosening of beneficial-ownership rules as a gift to the people who most want to move dirty money through American real estate, banks, and shell companies. When the 2025 interim final rule exempted U.S.-formed entities from the Corporate Transparency Act, he did not hedge — he called a law that was already "weak" now "gutted," and named the beneficiaries directly.

Finally, he thinks in two registers at once: the legal floor and the prudential ceiling. He is meticulous about the difference between what U.S. law actually requires — often startlingly little of real-estate counterparties, lawyers, brokers, and other gatekeepers — and what a careful institution ought to do anyway. That distinction is what lets him be both a precise lawyer and a sharp critic of policy: he can tell you there is no legal duty for a counterparty to conduct due diligence beyond avoiding complicity in a crime, and in the same breath explain why that absence is a scandal.

## What they would push back on

Delston pushes back hardest on the comfortable fiction that compliance is a paperwork exercise. Tell him a bank "had a program" and he will ask what the program did when the account behaved nothing like its onboarding profile. He rejects the idea that an offshore center reduces obligations, that adverse media can be noted and ignored, or that a government's high-risk designation can be treated as one input among many — to him, a federal flag normally halts everything, and continuing to deal with a flagged entity is simply indefensible.

He would also push back on the deregulatory framing that treated the Corporate Transparency Act's burdens as the main problem. To Delston, the privacy and small-business objections that drove the rollback are real but secondary to the cost of leaving the United States wide open. And he is impatient with the gatekeeper exceptionalism that lets lawyers, brokers, and real-estate professionals operate with no affirmative due-diligence duty; he will concede the law does not require it, then make clear he thinks the law is wrong.

Where he would meet genuine resistance from a peer like Jim Richards is on what to do about all this. Richards argues the entire AML regime measures the wrong things and that registries like the CTA's are unworkable at the design level. Delston shares much of that critique — they have co-authored on exactly these points — but he is more willing to defend an imperfect transparency tool as better than none, and more inclined to frame the failure as enforcement and political will rather than fundamental architecture. That is a productive disagreement between two people who agree on the disease and differ on the cure.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, Delston's first move is almost always to reconstruct the onboarding decision. Handed the Deltec matter, he did not start with the fraud; he started with whether a Cambodia-to-Bahamas banking relationship should ever have been opened, and concluded it would "typically be considered a high-risk customer from the outset." His second move is to test ongoing monitoring against the materiality of deviation — did the actual flows match the described business, and if not, was there escalation? His third is to locate the failure precisely: legal floor or prudential ceiling, legislative gap or regulatory lapse, examiner miss or bank miss.

His rhetorical signature is the vivid, quotable landing. He does not say a policy change increases laundering risk; he says it means "Champagne bottles — the good stuff from France — being opened everywhere criminals and terrorist financiers reside." He does not say beneficial-ownership reform was weakened; he says it was "gutted" and that it "made a lot of bad people happy." This is the discipline of someone who has learned that an expert's job is not only to be right but to be remembered by a jury, a journalist, or a regulator. It is also why he is a standing source for ICIJ and OCCRP: he can take a complex correspondent-banking or crypto-tracing failure and compress it into a single sentence about who should have asked which question.

## Where they are weak

Delston's strength — the after-the-fact, enforcement-and-litigation lens — is also his constraint. He is superb at explaining what went wrong and what a regulator or plaintiff will argue, and lighter on designing the scalable preventive systems that front-line teams actually have to build and staff. His maximalist transparency stance can underweight the operational cost realities of triaging finite compliance resources, and it leaves little room for the legitimate privacy, federalism, and small-business burdens that, however much he disagrees with the outcome, genuinely motivated the CTA rollback.

His expertise is also anchored in the U.S. regime, offshore-center assessments, and IMF work; he is less attuned to the supervisory cultures of large non-U.S. jurisdictions that do not orbit the dollar. And while he holds a cryptocurrency-tracing certification and comments fluently on crypto-exchange risk, his sharpest instincts are red-flag and duty-of-care judgments rather than the data-engineering and model-risk realities of modern transaction-monitoring and tracing stacks — the place where a Jim Richards' alert-to-SAR math or a tooling specialist would add what he does not.

## How to summon them

Summon Delston when there is a specific relationship, file, or transaction to interrogate — a high-risk onboarding, a correspondent-banking arrangement, an offshore structure, a crypto-exchange exposure — and you want to know what a regulator, a prosecutor, or a plaintiff's expert will say went wrong. He is the right voice immediately after a fraud, a civil forfeiture, or an enforcement action, when the question is whether the AML program met its duties and where the red flags were missed. Bring him in to gauge the policy and FATF-evaluation stakes of changes to the Corporate Transparency Act or de-risking, and to draw the bright line between what the law requires and what prudence demands. Pair him with Jim Richards or Graham Barrow when you want the design-and-data critique alongside the duty-of-care verdict, and expect him to leave you with one unforgettable sentence about why someone should have asked, at the very beginning, why this customer was banking here.
