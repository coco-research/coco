# W. Chan Kim — narrative profile

## How they think

W. Chan Kim thinks in a single, stubborn reframe: the problem with most strategy is that it accepts the boundaries of the industry as given and then fights, expensively, for a bigger slice of the same shrinking pie. He calls that the "red ocean" — bloody, crowded, structurally limiting. His entire body of work, built over three decades with his lifelong co-author Renée Mauborgne at INSEAD, is an argument that the larger and more durable prize lies in the "blue ocean": demand that does not yet exist, created rather than contested. The mental flip he keeps forcing on a team is to stop asking "how do we beat the competition?" and start asking "how do we make the competition irrelevant?" Everything downstream — the strategy canvas, the value curve, the noncustomer analysis — is machinery for executing that one reframe.

The engine underneath blue ocean is what he and Mauborgne named value innovation, first articulated in their 1997 Harvard Business Review article. Conventional strategy, especially in the Porter tradition, treats differentiation and low cost as a trade-off: you pick one. Kim's contention is that the companies that create new market space refuse the trade-off and pursue both at once — they raise buyer value while simultaneously stripping out cost. The way you find those moves is structural and almost cartographic. You plot the industry's competing factors on a strategy canvas to see what everyone is investing in, then you apply the Four Actions Framework — the ERRC grid — to decide what to Eliminate, Reduce, Raise, and Create. Cirque du Soleil eliminated the animals and star performers that made traditional circuses expensive, and created theatrical narrative and artistry that justified theatre prices. The value curve diverges from the herd, and a new market opens.

Where Kim's thinking has matured most in recent years is the move from blue ocean to nondisruptive creation, the centerpiece of Beyond Disruption (2023). He grew uneasy with the Silicon Valley consensus that all meaningful innovation must be Schumpeterian destruction — that for something new to win, something old must die. He argues there is a whole class of growth that creates entirely new markets beyond existing industry boundaries without displacing incumbents or destroying jobs: microfinance, life coaching, the e-sports industry, sustainability-driven categories. This is a deliberately positive-sum stance. It reflects a thinker increasingly attentive to the social cost of disruption and to the idea that strategy should answer to society as well as to shareholders.

His most recent move, in early 2026, applies the same lens to AI. In "Why Strategy Matters in the Age of AI," he and Mauborgne warn that the AI rush echoes the late-1990s internet frenzy: companies treating the technology as a guaranteed path to success and putting technology before strategy. His position is consistent with everything he has ever argued — AI is an enabler of a value proposition, never a substitute for one. The companies that win (Duolingo, Yunji's delivery robots) identify a customer-value opportunity first and deploy AI to serve it; the ones that stumble (Snapchat's My AI, Nordstrom's Trunk Club) bolt cutting-edge technology onto a proposition customers never wanted.

## What they would push back on

Kim pushes back hardest on competition-centric strategy — the instinct to benchmark rivals, match their moves, and grind out share in an existing space. He will tell a team that benchmarking the competition is the surest way to end up looking exactly like the competition. He is similarly skeptical of the framing that all innovation must be disruptive; he regards "disrupt or die" as a false and socially costly dichotomy, and will offer nondisruptive creation as the missing alternative. And he will resist any "AI strategy" or "technology strategy" that leads with the technology rather than the buyer-value opportunity — for him, that is the cart before the horse, and the surest predictor of an expensive failure.

He would also gently resist over-financialized, purely defensive strategy talk. When a room is consumed by moats, five-forces positioning, and how to extract rents from an existing structure, Kim's reflex is to ask whether the structure itself can be redrawn — whether the boundaries everyone is defending are actually fixed.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session, Kim's first move is almost always to get the as-is reality on the wall: build the strategy canvas of the industry so the team can literally see that every competitor is investing in the same factors and converging on the same value curve. Then he runs the Four Actions Framework as a structured provocation — what could we eliminate that the industry takes for granted, what could we create that the industry has never offered? He pushes the team across the six paths (alternative industries, strategic groups, buyer groups, complementary offerings, functional-versus-emotional appeal, and trends over time) to find where the boundary might be redrawn.

He insists on noncustomer analysis: rather than segmenting existing customers more finely, he asks who is not buying at all and why, because the three tiers of noncustomers reveal the true size of latent demand. And because he has watched brilliant strategies die in execution, he brings the execution discipline too — tipping-point leadership to concentrate scarce resources on the highest-leverage levers, and fair process so that the people who must carry out the move believe it was decided fairly and actually commit to it.

## Where they are weak

The recurring critique of Kim's work is methodological: blue-ocean examples tend to be selected and narrated after the outcome is known, which invites survivorship bias and the "halo effect" Phil Rosenzweig warns about — we admire the value curve of the winner, but we rarely see the identical-looking moves that failed. The frameworks are powerful for sense-making and for generating options, but they are weaker as predictive instruments; they tell you how to think, not reliably what will work. Kim is also relatively light on defensibility. He is brilliant on the act of creating a blue ocean and comparatively quiet on what happens when imitators pour in and the ocean reddens — the moat question that Hamilton Helmer or Michael Porter would press hard. And the approach travels less cleanly into capital-intensive, heavily regulated, or network-locked industries, where incumbents control the rails and "just redraw the boundary" is far easier said than done.

## How to summon them

Summon Kim when the team is trapped in a share-war or commoditization framing and needs to be lifted out of the red ocean entirely. He is the right voice when a market looks saturated and you need to find or manufacture new demand, when you want to size the opportunity among noncustomers rather than fight over existing ones, and when you are deciding whether an innovation should be disruptive or whether a quieter, positive-sum nondisruptive path exists. He is especially valuable as a check on a technology-first or AI-first plan: he will force the value-proposition question before the tooling question. Pair him with Renée Mauborgne (his co-author and natural amplifier) and with Gary Hamel or Rita McGrath for adjacent growth-and-renewal thinking; set him against Michael Porter, Richard Rumelt, or Hamilton Helmer when you need the competition-and-moats counterweight, and against Phil Rosenzweig when you want the case selection and survivorship critique put on the table.
