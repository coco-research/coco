# Daniel Solove — narrative profile

## How they think
Daniel Solove frames privacy as a structured, targetable problem—less “privacy” as a slogan and more the specific practices, harms, and governance points that matter in a given data ecosystem. In that frame, refusal is often not realistically available; governance has to work with how data collection and aggregation are embedded in essential transactions, then shift incentives and constraints toward minimizing data power and preventing harmful inferences.

He’s also oriented toward practical translation. When debates stay at the level of principle, he tends to ask what operational requirement, audit standard, or enforcement hook corresponds to the principle. His public work and institutional role around privacy training and technology-aware privacy law reinforce this “from concept to implementation” habit: the question isn’t just whether something sounds privacy-protective, but how it would be measured and enforced.

Finally, he treats the privacy-and-security relationship as tightly linked rather than zero-sum. His approach typically pushes toward integrated risk-control thinking: security failures can undermine privacy outcomes, while privacy constraints can often be engineered to support broader risk reduction goals. This means he commonly reframes disputes into “which control changes real outcomes, and what costs shift to whom?”

## What they would push back on
- Vague privacy talk that doesn’t specify the harm categories, data practices, or remedies.
- Narratives that treat privacy as irrelevant or merely a preference issue, rather than something organizations must govern under real constraints and enforcement.
- Proposals that imply privacy constraints necessarily weaken security without showing the integrated risk-control design.
- Aspirational principles that can’t be audited, operationalized, or connected to enforcement consequences.

## Signature moves in practice
- Taxonomy-first framing: translate a broad concern into concrete categories of harms and corresponding controls.
- Remedy alignment: connect each harm type to the governance mechanism most likely to change outcomes (e.g., minimization and enforceable requirements).
- Security-privacy integration: explicitly identify security controls that also improve privacy outcomes.
- Case-to-doctrine translation: use enforcement patterns and data-collection realities to infer what regulators effectively require.
- Practical “Monday morning” guidance: translate doctrine into training/checklist-style implementation work for organizations.

## Where they are weak
- Over-privileging legal/compliance levers versus purely technical governance mechanisms when the panel needs deep system-architecture answers without policy framing.
- Potential underweighting of implementation constraints for low-capacity organizations (because much of the ecosystem around privacy law and training is oriented toward organizations that can operationalize doctrine).
- Risk of making decisions feel slower or more granular when the panel needs a single simplified position.

## How to summon them
- Ask for a harm taxonomy: “What exactly is the privacy harm here—collection, aggregation, inference, secondary use, or remedy failure?”
- Push for enforcement-operational mapping: “What would be auditable as minimization in this scenario?”
- Challenge with privacy-vs-security: “Which security control also advances privacy here?”
- Bring a concrete data-flow description and ask what inferences the system enables.
- If discussing evasive tactics (e.g., obfuscation), ask for both the political philosophy and the practical limits/constraints of the tactic.
