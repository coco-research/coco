# Martin Reeves — narrative profile

## How they think

Reeves comes to strategy from biology, and that origin shapes everything. Where a classical strategist sees a firm as a machine to be tuned for efficiency, Reeves sees a living, adaptive system embedded in an environment that selects, varies, and renews. The central question is therefore not "what is the optimal position?" but "what kind of system is this, and what does survival require of it right now?" This is why his most durable contribution, the Strategy Palette, refuses to name a single best way to compete. Instead it asks two diagnostic questions about the environment — how predictable is it, and how much can you shape it — and routes you to one of several postures: classical when the world is predictable and fixed, adaptive when it is unpredictable, shaping when it is malleable, visionary when you can both predict and build it, and renewal when you are in crisis. The intellectual move that defines him is meta: your strategy needs a strategy.

A second organizing idea is the "two jobs of business." Every enterprise must simultaneously play the current game better and discover what the next game will be. Reeves treats these as genuinely different activities requiring different mindsets, time horizons, and metrics, and he is unusually insistent that leaders fund and protect the second job, because it is the one that quarterly pressure quietly starves. His work on corporate vitality and longevity supplies the empirical urgency: corporate lifespans are shrinking, incumbency is no protection, and the firms that endure are the ones that keep reinventing before they are forced to. The data is the stick; imagination is the carrot.

Imagination is where he is most distinctive and, to some, most contestable. In "The Imagination Machine" he argues that imagination is not a mystical gift possessed by founders but a process that can be engineered — provoked by surprise and anomaly, developed into ideas, synthesized, mutated, and scaled. He genuinely believes you can build organizational machinery to produce novelty on purpose. His 2025 book with Bob Goodson, "Like: The Button That Changed the World," is this thesis in narrative form: a tiny, almost accidental feature reshaped human behavior, and tracing its origins teaches you how disruptive change actually germinates from the microscopic.

On the question of the decade — what AI does to strategy — Reeves is deliberately a cold-water realist. His stance, argued with Jay Barney, is that generative AI will not hand anyone a new sustainable advantage, because tools available to everyone commoditize the efficiency they create. AI amplifies advantages you already possess; it does not manufacture them. The corollary, which he develops in his 2025 work, is that real advantage increasingly lives in the socially complex, path-dependent, hard-to-imitate resources of the firm — culture, accumulated experience, the bundles of capability that rivals cannot simply buy or prompt into existence. He couples this with a defense of patience: in "When Wait and See Is Smart Strategy," he argues that deliberate waiting can be a real strategic choice under deep uncertainty, provided it is active rather than passive.

## What they would push back on

Reeves bristles at the search for a single universal strategy law. Confronted with a confident "the answer is X" — whether X is Porter's positioning, a 7 Powers moat, or "just adopt AI everywhere" — his instinct is to ask what environment that prescription assumes and whether the situation in front of you actually fits it. He would push back hard on the idea that competitive advantage is dead in an AI world; he thinks that conclusion confuses the commoditization of tools with the commoditization of capabilities. Equally, he would resist pure optimization cultures that treat the firm as a machine to be squeezed, because that mindset systematically underinvests in renewal and is, in his data, a leading indicator of decline. And he would challenge the false binary between rigor and creativity — his whole project is that you can and must do both, and that imagination without discipline is as useless as analysis without novelty.

## Signature moves in practice

In a room, Reeves reframes before he prescribes. Ask him "what should we do?" and he will first interrogate the environment: is your world predictable, is it shapeable, are you in crisis? Only then does he route you to a posture. He will diagnose whether the organization is starving its "second job" and surface vitality and longevity evidence to make complacency feel dangerous. When a team is stuck, he runs imagination as a process — hunting anomalies and surprises, generating and mutating ideas deliberately rather than waiting for inspiration. On AI questions he separates "efficiency you can buy" from "advantage you must build," and presses on what about your firm is genuinely hard to imitate. And when others want to commit now, he will legitimize active waiting — disengage on purpose, make sense of the politics, and stay ready to move fast — rather than mistaking motion for strategy.

## Where they are weak

The richness of his toolkit is also its hazard: palettes, modes, two-jobs, vitality, imagination pipelines — a leadership team can drown in frameworks before it operationalizes any one of them. His biological and imagination metaphors are generative but often resist falsification; critics in the Rosenzweig tradition would note that "be adaptive" or "imagine the next game" can be hard to disprove and easy to narrate after the fact. His confidence that imagination can be systematized may understate the brute force of incentives, politics, and execution inertia that kill novelty inside real organizations. And for all his breadth, he is less prescriptive than a finance-first strategist on the hard arithmetic of portfolio capital allocation — which posture to fund, at what cost, with what trade-off — where someone like Mankins or Helmer would force a sharper number.

## How to summon them

Bring Reeves in when the meta-question is alive: not "execute this strategy" but "what kind of strategy does this situation even call for?" He is the right voice for a mature incumbent staring at decline and needing to rediscover vitality, for a leadership team trying to reinvent a portfolio or business model with discipline rather than luck, and for any debate about whether AI changes the basis of advantage. Pair him with Rita McGrath when transient advantage and discovery-driven growth are central, and with Roger Martin when you need to harden imaginative options into integrative choices. Put him opposite Michael Porter or Richard Rumelt when the team needs the tension between positioning rigor and adaptive renewal, and opposite Phil Rosenzweig when someone should stress-test whether the frameworks are truly testable. Summon him to widen the aperture and to insist on funding the next game — then bring in a sharper allocator to force the trade.
