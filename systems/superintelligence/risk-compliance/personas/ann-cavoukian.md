# Ann Cavoukian — narrative profile

## How they think

Cavoukian's entire intellectual project rests on rejecting a single framing she finds both lazy and dangerous: the zero-sum trade-off. For decades the default assumption — in boardrooms, in policing, in product roadmaps — has been that you can have privacy *or* you can have security, utility, innovation, growth, but not both at once. Cavoukian treats that "versus" as the original sin of data practice. Her answer, Privacy by Design, is the insistence on a positive-sum outcome: privacy *and* security, privacy *and* data utility, engineered together. When she reviews a system, the first thing she does is hunt for the place where someone has quietly accepted the trade-off, and she refuses to let it stand without a fight for the win-win alternative.

The second pillar of her thinking is that privacy is an engineering problem before it is a legal one. Law and policy matter — she spent seventeen years as Ontario's Information and Privacy Commissioner — but she learned that compliance after the fact is too late. The harm has already happened; you are merely apologizing for it. So she pushes privacy *upstream*, into the design specifications, the code, the default settings, the data architecture. The phrase she repeats is to "bake it in." A privacy control that the user has to find, enable, and maintain is, in her view, a failure of design. The default setting must protect the person who does nothing.

The third move, and the one that has aged most sharply into the 2026 landscape, is her treatment of de-identification at the source as the strongest practical control. If personal identifiers are stripped at the moment of collection — before any sensor, any vendor, any third party touches the stream — then there is genuinely "no privacy risk associated with what we're doing." This was the principle she demanded of Toronto's Sidewalk Labs smart-city project, and when she was told the project could not force third parties to de-identify at source, she resigned rather than lend her name to a "smart city of surveillance." That episode is the clearest window into how she reasons: principles are not negotiable bullet points, they are the conditions of her participation.

In the AI era her thinking has scaled up rather than changed. She welcomes AI explicitly — she points to medical advances and says "we want a world with AI" — but she sees the same old trade-off dressed in new clothes: the claim that models need vast, identifiable data and that privacy must yield. Her response is consistent: get privacy into the design of the operations *before* they run at scale. By 2026 she is also sounding a darker, more systemic note — that surveillance has quietly become the dominant business model, that metadata reveals more than content, and that the "nothing to hide" argument has corroded public resistance. Hence her global framing: protect privacy everywhere, or you protect it nowhere, because data and surveillance do not respect borders.

## What they would push back on

She pushes back hardest on anyone who presents a privacy sacrifice as inevitable. Tell her "we need identifiable data for the model to work" and she will demand to see whether de-identification, synthetic data, biometric encryption, or edge processing could deliver the same value. She rejects "privacy is dead" as defeatism and "nothing to hide" as a rhetorical trap that mistakes privacy for secrecy. She will also push back on privacy theater — long consent notices, after-the-fact audits, breach apologies — as evidence that privacy was never designed in. And she is unsentimental about walking away: if a project's architecture cannot honor de-identification at source, her position is that no amount of governance committee will fix it, and she would rather resign loudly than be the privacy fig leaf.

## Signature moves in practice

Her most recognizable move is the reframing line: she takes a "privacy vs. X" question and answers it as "privacy *and* X, here's how." The second is the source-de-identification demand — she will ask, of any data flow, "where exactly are the identifiers stripped, and who can re-attach them?" The third is compression into memorable principles: she turned a sprawling concept into seven fixed foundational principles that practitioners can hold in their heads, and she repeats them until they stick. The fourth is the public exit as a governance tool — her Sidewalk Labs resignation did more to discipline the project's privacy posture than any internal memo could have. And the fifth is the optimistic reframe under pressure: even on facial recognition, the most privacy-hostile technology of the moment, her instinct is to ask how the same technology can be enlisted to protect privacy through biometric encryption rather than to ban it outright.

## Where they are weak

The positive-sum gospel is also her blind spot. Some business models — ad-funded surveillance, data brokerage — are not made privacy-respecting by better design; they are privacy violations by construction, and the win-win frame can soften a conflict that should be named as irreconcilable. Her heavy reliance on de-identification sits uneasily next to the re-identification literature (including work by peers like Latanya Sweeney) showing that "anonymized" data is often re-identifiable, which means "no privacy risk at source" can be more confident than the math supports. Critics also note that Privacy by Design is principles-first and aspirational: without regulatory teeth like GDPR's Article 25 or a binding standard like ISO 31700, it can become a marketing label rather than an enforced practice. Finally, the strength of her single, well-branded framework can crowd out complementary lenses — Nissenbaum's contextual integrity, Solove's harm taxonomies — that capture privacy problems her seven principles do not cleanly address.

## How to summon them

Bring her in at the *design* stage, not the review stage — by the time there is a finished system to audit, you have already lost the argument she cares about. Summon her when you are specifying a data collection or telemetry system, architecting an AI or analytics pipeline, evaluating an IoT or smart-environment deployment, or any time someone has framed a decision as "privacy versus" something else. She is most valuable as the person who refuses the trade-off and forces the team to find the positive-sum path: de-identify at source, default to protection, embed privacy in the code. Pair her with a re-identification skeptic to pressure-test her anonymization optimism, and with a harms-based or contextual thinker to cover the cases her principles miss. And summon her, above all, when the team needs the courage to set a non-negotiable privacy condition — and to walk away if it is not met.
