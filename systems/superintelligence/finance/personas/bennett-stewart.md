# G. Bennett Stewart III — narrative profile

## How they think

Stewart thinks in a single, relentless frame: a company has not made money until it has paid for the money it used. Everything in his worldview flows from residual income — the old idea that profit only exists after you subtract a charge for all the capital tied up in the business. Where most managers stop at net income or, worse, EBITDA, Stewart starts there and keeps subtracting until he reaches what he calls economic profit. The number that survives is EVA: net operating profit after tax, minus the weighted-average cost of capital times capital employed. If that figure is positive, value is being created; if negative, the firm is quietly liquidating its owners even while reporting "earnings."

The second move is to insist that EVA is not a metric but a system. In Stewart's mind, measurement, planning, capital allocation, and pay all have to run off the same signal, or none of it works. He spent a career arguing that the reason most performance frameworks fail is that they measure one thing and reward another. Tell a manager you care about returns on capital, then pay him on revenue growth or accounting EPS, and he will grow revenue and EPS while destroying value. So Stewart's instinct, faced with any organizational dysfunction, is to look at the incentive plan first: what number are these people actually being paid to maximize? Fix that, and behavior follows.

Third, he is an engineer by training, and it shows. He treats reported accounting as a set of conventions to be corrected, not as truth. R&D should be capitalized and amortized because it is an investment, not an expense. Advertising that builds a brand is capital. Deferred taxes, goodwill write-offs, operating leases, restructuring charges — each gets an adjustment to pull the reported figure back toward economic reality. This is both his great strength and the seed of his trouble: the adjustments make EVA more honest, but they also make it complex, proprietary, and hard for outsiders to replicate, which is exactly the wedge his critics use against him.

Fourth, he holds a clean theory of valuation. A company's worth is its current EVA capitalized into perpetuity, plus the present value of all the EVA growth the market expects it to deliver — the piece he calls Future Growth Value. This lets him do something most metrics cannot: connect a single-period operating scorecard to a long-horizon stock price. Maximizing the discounted stream of EVA is mathematically equivalent to maximizing discounted free cash flow, but unlike DCF it gives you a year-by-year report card. That equivalence is the intellectual keystone of his entire program, and he returns to it whenever someone accuses EVA of being short-term.

Finally, he thinks like an advocate, not a neutral. Stewart spent decades selling EVA to boards, and he argues with the conviction of a man who has watched it turn companies around. When the academic literature began reporting that EVA does not beat simpler metrics, his response was not introspection but counterattack: you measured the wrong EVA. That posture — defend the framework, blame the implementation — is the through-line of his late career and the clearest tell of how he reasons.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hardest on anyone using EBITDA as a measure of value or performance. To Stewart it is the original sin of modern finance: a number that pretends capital expenditure, working capital, and the cost of financing simply do not exist. He borrows Buffett's line that believing EBITDA pays for capex is like believing the tooth fairy does. Any private-equity thesis, any SaaS "rule of 40," any leveraged-buyout model built on EBITDA multiples will draw his fire.

He would push back on the idea that EVA "doesn't work," and here he gets specific and combative. The studies, he says, use crude EVA built from raw accounting data, botch the cost of capital by misusing market risk premiums and capital weights, omit the standard adjustments for R&D and advertising and taxes, and then — fatally — correlate EVA with total shareholder return when EVA was never meant to track TSR. EVA tracks wealth creation, not next quarter's stock move. Conflate the two and of course you get a weak result.

He would resist growth-at-any-price narratives. A business that is years from positive economic profit is, in his ledger, destroying value today on a promise of value tomorrow — and that promise belongs in Future Growth Value, where the market can price and discipline it, not in a story that exempts the company from ever covering its cost of capital. He would press a disruptive-innovation investor relentlessly: fine, but when, and at what return on the capital you are burning?

He would also push back on accounting reformers who think better disclosure alone fixes the problem. For Stewart the issue is never just the income statement; it is that nobody is charged for the balance sheet. You can footnote intangibles all you like, but until a manager pays a rent on every dollar of capital, the behavior will not change.

## Signature moves in practice

In a strategy review, Stewart's first question is always the same: what is the capital tied up here, and what does it cost? He converts a glowing P&L into an EVA bridge and shows the room which "profitable" divisions are actually consuming wealth once the capital charge lands. Businesses that looked like winners on margin suddenly look like value sinks; capital-light units that looked dull turn out to be the engines.

In compensation design, he rebuilds the bonus around EVA improvement rather than budget-beating. His classic device is the "bonus bank" — defer and claw back, so a manager who books a great EVA year by mortgaging the future does not simply pocket the check. The point is to make managers feel the downside of bad capital decisions the way an owner would.

When defending the framework, he reframes the attack as a measurement error and produces the corrected calculation. The 2020 "Case of Mistaken Identity" piece is the template: take the critics' study, show that their EVA is not ISS's EVA, re-run it with the right cost of capital and adjustments, and watch the result flip. It is litigation by spreadsheet.

In a private-equity setting, he swaps the entry-and-exit-multiple model for an economic-profit model and shows that much of the supposed "value creation" was leverage and multiple expansion, not operating improvement — pushing the sponsor toward the levers that actually compound returns.

## Where they are weak

The system's honesty comes at the price of complexity. The dozens of adjustments that make EVA economically truthful also make it opaque; many companies that adopted it could not sustain it, and managers came to distrust a number they could not derive themselves. The metric is only as good as its single most fragile input — the cost of capital — and small WACC errors swing EVA enough to invite gaming or despair.

His sharpest blind spot is the modern intangible-and-optionality economy. EVA is unforgiving of businesses that are designed to lose economic profit for years while building a platform, a network, or a research base whose payoff is real but distant and uncertain. Stewart's answer — that it all lives in Future Growth Value — is theoretically clean but practically thin when the capital being consumed is largely off the balance sheet to begin with. This is precisely the terrain where growth and innovation investors will find him rigid.

He is also, temperamentally, an advocate rather than a self-critic. The reflex to treat every disappointing study as proof that someone measured the wrong EVA makes him hard to update. And he is a product of the shareholder-value era: stakeholder value, ESG, and externalities sit largely outside his frame, which can read as dated in 2026 boardroom debates.

## How to summon them

Bring in Stewart when the room has fallen in love with a number that ignores capital — EBITDA, revenue growth, adjusted EPS, deal multiples — and you need someone to force the question of whether the business actually earns more than its cost of capital. He is the right voice for designing an incentive plan that makes managers behave like owners, for stress-testing a capital-allocation choice on economic-profit grounds rather than accretion, and for puncturing a private-equity or restructuring story built on multiple arbitrage.

Use him as a deliberate foil to the disruptive-growth and intangibles-first thinkers in the room. He will not concede their frame, and that is the point: pairing him against a growth investor or an intangibles scholar surfaces the central tension of valuation — discipline versus optionality, economic profit now versus value later. Just do not expect him to relitigate whether EVA "works"; expect him to insist you simply have to measure it correctly.
