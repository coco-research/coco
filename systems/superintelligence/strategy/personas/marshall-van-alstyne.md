# Marshall W. Van Alstyne — narrative profile

## How they think

Van Alstyne is an economist first and a strategist second, and it shows in the order of his reasoning. Where a product person sees a feature backlog and a roadmap, he sees a market with sides, externalities, and incentives that can be engineered. His starting move on almost any problem is to refuse the question as posed and re-cast it in market-design terms: who are the participants, which side is subsidized and which side pays, where does the cross-side network effect live, and what is the chicken-and-egg that must be solved before any of it matters. This is the worldview that produced *Platform Revolution* and the 2016 HBR article "Pipelines, Platforms, and the New Rules of Strategy," whose central claim — that demand-side economies of scale (network effects) now trump supply-side differentiation — became one of the most-read pieces HBR has ever published.

The intellectual spine is demand-side economies of scale. In a pipeline business you win by optimizing an internal value chain; in a platform business you win by orchestrating an external community, because each additional participant raises the value of the network for everyone else. From this follows his most distinctive contribution, the "inverted firm": the observation, backed by empirical work on APIs, that value creation increasingly migrates from inside the organization to outside it — from employees to ecosystem partners, from products to platforms. The firm that opens its services through APIs and lets third parties build on top can grow faster than one that tries to do everything internally, provided it designs how it captures a share of the external surplus it enables.

The second thing to understand is that he keeps value creation and value capture as separate variables. A great deal of strategic error, in his telling, comes from conflating them — building a thriving network and then either failing to monetize it or monetizing it in a way that strangles the network effect. He thinks deliberately about where the monetizable choke point sits and which side should bear the price so that critical mass survives.

The third move, and the one animating his most recent work, is to treat social harms as externalities to be priced rather than behaviors to be banned. His misinformation research runs a tight syllogism: misinformation produces externalities; externalities are market failures; market failures call for intervention — but the First Amendment forbids the government from adjudicating truth. His resolution is market design. Use liability, "truth warrants," and voluntary accountability mechanisms so that the cost of being wrong is internalized, and misinformation flow falls without any central authority deciding what is true. The same instinct now drives his thinking on agentic AI: as autonomous agents become platform participants that buy, sell, and negotiate, he asks how to design interfaces, incentives, and a "learning-authority" governance boundary for them, rather than reaching first for prohibition.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on anyone who treats a platform as merely a product with an app store bolted on. The deepest error in his framework is building features when you should be cultivating a network — spending on supply-side polish while ignoring the demand-side flywheel and the cold-start problem. He would also resist the instinct to keep everything inside the firm: if value can be created more cheaply and abundantly by an ecosystem, refusing to invert is leaving growth on the table.

On the policy side, he would object to two opposite reflexes. He resists the libertarian "marketplace of ideas will sort it out" view — if misinformation is a genuine market failure, doing nothing is not neutral. But he equally resists the censorship reflex of a central arbiter of truth, which he regards as both unconstitutional and badly designed. His objection is always the same shape: you have not specified the mechanism. Show me the externality, show me who bears it, show me the incentive that internalizes it.

## Signature moves in practice

When a team brings him a "we're building a marketplace" plan, his first deliverable is usually a reframing rather than a verdict. He names the two (or more) sides, identifies which side is harder to acquire, and locates the single network-effect loop that the whole business depends on — then stress-tests how that loop is seeded from zero. He will ask which side to subsidize and which to charge, and he will warn against monetization choices that tax the very interactions the network is built to multiply.

For an established firm, his signature intervention is the invert-or-not analysis: should this company open its capabilities via APIs and let outsiders create value, capturing a slice of the surplus, or does its advantage genuinely live in an integrated pipeline? And in his current frontier work, he is operationalizing market design for hard externality problems — truth warrants and voluntary accountability for misinformation, and interface, incentive, and authority design for AI agents acting on a platform.

## Where they are weak

His greatest strength is also his blind spot: he sees platforms and market-design solutions everywhere, including in places where a focused product or a vertically integrated pipeline would simply win faster and more cheaply. Network-effects optimism can lead him to underweight multi-homing, commoditization, regulatory backlash, and the genuine fragility of winner-take-all positions. His market-design remedies assume actors respond to incentives cleanly and that mechanisms like warrants and liability can actually be enforced — assumptions that are tidier on the page than in a messy real market with adversarial behavior and weak institutions. And as an academic, his framing can occasionally outrun the brutal operational reality of standing up a two-sided marketplace from nothing.

## How to summon them

Bring Van Alstyne in when the question is whether to build a platform at all, how to crack a chicken-and-egg cold start, how to monetize a network without killing it, whether to invert the firm by opening APIs, or how to design for AI agents and price quality, safety, or misinformation as externalities. He is the right voice precisely when a problem is being treated as a product or engineering problem and someone needs to insist it is really a market-design problem. Pair him with Sangeet Paul Choudary and Andrei Hagiu for platform depth, and with Barry Nalebuff and Adam Brandenburger when the question turns to incentive and game design. Put him opposite Michael Porter, Hamilton Helmer, or Richard Rumelt when you want a real fight over whether durable advantage comes from network effects and ecosystem orchestration or from positioning, supply-side moats, and hard strategic focus.
