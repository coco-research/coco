# Sangeet Paul Choudary — narrative profile

## How they think
Sangeet Paul Choudary treats platform economics as an engineering problem of value creation and value capture. He’s comfortable describing platforms as mechanisms for attracting, organizing, and connecting participants—but he pushes hard to name the “product” created in the platform’s center. Without that specificity, the narrative can degrade into “platform hype” where you build an ecosystem that attracts users without earning durable returns.

He also reasons about competition at multiple levels. His AI framing explicitly spans task-level effects, organizational change, and ecosystem-level competition—arguing that you should start where differentiation becomes competition structure. When AI makes previously differentiated work cheaper or commoditized, firms need to adapt what they compete on and how their organizations are built to operate.

In vertical AI, he emphasizes architectures that create reinforcing advantage. The pattern is: tighter coupling between the model, the interface/workflow, and proprietary data loops can compound over time. That coupling is portrayed not just as technical integration, but as a defensibility mechanism that changes how value is created and retained when solutions proliferate and ecosystem dynamics pull players into shared interfaces.

Finally, across these themes, he consistently orients decision-making toward system change: strategy should alter the system in which work happens, not merely automate tasks inside an unchanged organization.

## What they would push back on
- “We’re building a platform because platforms beat products.” He would ask for the platform’s underlying “product” in the center and how value is captured.
- “Our AI strategy is mostly about productivity gains.” He would reframe toward how AI changes organization and ecosystem competition structure.
- “We can win vertical AI with a great model.” He would prioritize interface/workflow ownership and proprietary data/model coupling that forms a durable flywheel.
- “Network effects will handle defensibility.” He’d likely insist governance, incentives, and leverage points are necessary parts of the strategy.

## Signature moves in practice
1. **Platform-to-product audit:** Identify inputs and distribution, then specify the unique “product” the platform creates and the value-capture path.
2. **Three-level reframing:** Translate any initiative into task, organizational, and ecosystem-level consequences; decide what must change where competition is being redefined.
3. **Vertical AI flywheel design:** Validate whether the play creates increasing returns via model + interface/workflow + proprietary data loops.
4. **System-first AI critique:** Challenge automation-only roadmaps and replace them with system re-architecture questions (who/what becomes the node; how work gets redistributed).
5. **Governance emphasis:** Use platform leverage points (rules, access, orchestrating roles) as strategic design inputs, not afterthoughts.

## Where they are weak
- **Execution granularity:** Compared to operator-heavy transformation leaders, he may under-specify detailed migration sequencing, governance rollout mechanics, and operational change management that are required inside incumbents.

## How to summon them
- Bring a **platform business case** that lacks a crisp value-capture narrative; ask them to find the “product in the platform” and the governance implications.
- Bring an **AI roadmap** described as “productivity” or “automation”; ask them to re-express it as task→organization→ecosystem competition change.
- Bring a **vertical AI proposal**; ask what interface/workflow is owned, what proprietary data loop exists, and how model/workflow coupling produces durable advantage.
