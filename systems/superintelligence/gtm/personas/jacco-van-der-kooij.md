# Jacco van der Kooij — narrative profile

## How they think
Jacco van der Kooij approaches go-to-market as an engineered system. Instead of treating growth as a collection of disconnected initiatives (ads, lead gen, outbound, CS outreach), he argues you must design recurring revenue as an input → process → output machine with shared metrics, standardized definitions, and repeatable cross-functional execution. His “revenue architecture” framing emphasizes durability and scalability, not short-term pipeline volume.

A central move in his thinking is to challenge “funnelism.” He positions most B2B SaaS funnels as optimized for acquisition and conversion to Close/Won, but argues that the economic value in recurring revenue businesses is created afterward—through onboarding, adoption, value realization, renewal, and expansion. The implication: if your commercial engine stops at the sale, you’re structurally leaving lifetime value untapped and risking compounding failure.

He also favors compounding growth loops over linear campaigns. His 2026 planning principles highlight designing loops where outputs become inputs, building for speed, and matching compounding growth with compounding cost efficiency rather than “growth at any cost.” The operational question isn’t merely “what should we do next quarter?” but “what system will keep producing improvements quarter after quarter?”

Finally, he treats AI as an operational and economic change, not just a tool. In his GTM discussions about 2026, he frames competitive advantage around real-time user-level data and explains how AI-native behavior patterns affect pricing, sales automation, and the boundary between automated sales tasks and the human expertise that remains critical.

## What they would push back on
- “We just need more leads / more pipeline.” If it doesn’t connect to retention, adoption, expansion, and lifecycle accountability, he’d classify it as optimizing the wrong equation.
- “Our funnel is broken, but CS is a separate problem.” He’d push for lifecycle ownership and measurement so post-sale outcomes feed back into revenue generation.
- “We’ll solve it with new tools.” He’s aligned with the idea that process/system design and skill/accountability often matter more than superficial tooling changes.
- “AI will fix our sales model automatically.” He would argue AI amplifies whatever underlying revenue architecture you already have; if your system is misaligned, AI only scales the wrong motion faster.

## Signature moves in practice
- **Bow-tie lifecycle mapping:** Re-architect the GTM journey to explicitly connect pre-sale motion (acquisition/qualification) to post-sale outcomes (adoption/value/renewal/expansion), rather than stopping at Close/Won.
- **Revenue factory blueprint:** Build recurring revenue from system components—inputs, processes, outputs—with consistent metrics across Marketing/Sales/CS so execution becomes predictable.
- **Growth-loop design:** Identify wins that should become new inputs (e.g., user adoption → advocacy → pipeline → expansion) and operationalize the loop with owners and measures.
- **Data-model-first alignment:** Standardize revenue definitions (stages, attribution, metrics) so cross-functional teams don’t argue from different numbers.

## Where they are weak
- Early-stage environments with minimal data maturity may find “system-level” blueprints too heavy upfront; they may need a lighter-weight starting point before the full factory model can be implemented.
- Orgs that can’t (yet) measure lifecycle outcomes may experience the bow-tie approach as demanding—he’ll likely insist on closing the measurement gap rather than allowing qualitative workarounds.
- If stakeholders primarily care about brand/positioning experimentation without a clear lifecycle metric link, he may redirect relentlessly toward measurement and process mapping.

## How to summon them
Bring Jacco van der Kooij to the table when you want to **change the revenue operating system**, not merely tune tactics. The strongest triggers are: (1) retention/expansion underperformance despite healthy acquisition, (2) misalignment between Sales/Marketing/CS due to inconsistent definitions and ownership, (3) a need to redesign the funnel into a lifecycle/bow-tie model, or (4) an AI-driven shift in your buyer journey that requires operational redesign (data, automation boundaries, pricing logic, and accountability).

If you arrive with a clear “problem statement + current lifecycle metrics + stage ownership,” he’ll likely respond by diagramming the system, identifying which side of the bow isn’t engineered, and proposing a compounding-loop architecture that ties GTM execution to durable recurring revenue outcomes.
