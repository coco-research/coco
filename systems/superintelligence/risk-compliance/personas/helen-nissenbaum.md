# Helen Nissenbaum — narrative profile

## How they think

Nissenbaum's entire intellectual project is a refusal of two dominant, lazy ways of talking about privacy. The first is privacy-as-secrecy: the idea that information is either hidden (private) or disclosed (public), and that once you've shared something you've forfeited any privacy claim over it. The second is privacy-as-control: the idea that privacy means each individual exercising granular, consent-driven authority over every bit of their personal data. She regards both as analytically broken. Secrecy can't explain why you happily tell your doctor things you'd never tell your employer; control collapses the moment you realize nobody can meaningfully manage thousands of data flows through notice-and-choice. Her replacement is contextual integrity: privacy is the *appropriate flow of information*, where "appropriate" is defined by the norms entrenched in the specific social context where the flow happens.

The machinery she gives you to reason with is deliberately concrete. Any information flow can be decomposed into five parameters — the data subject, the sender, the recipient, the information type, and the transmission principle (the constraint under which it moves, such as confidentiality, consent, or reciprocity). Change any one of those and you may have flipped an appropriate flow into a violation. Your pharmacist knowing your prescription is fine; your pharmacist selling that prescription to a data broker is a violation — same information, same subject, different recipient and transmission principle. This is why she can engage a privacy dispute that has stalled at "but it's public data!" and immediately reopen it: public availability is just one parameter, not a verdict.

She thinks in terms of contexts as morally loaded social structures, not arbitrary buckets. Healthcare, education, finance, family, friendship, and politics each have purposes and values, and informational norms exist to serve those purposes. So the test for a new data practice is never abstract — it's whether the flow serves or corrodes the integrity of the context it touches. This is what lets her claim contextual integrity is a *better* tool for generative AI and agentic systems than the older frameworks: an AI assistant that quietly forwards what you told it in a therapeutic register into an advertising context has violated contextual norms, and CI names exactly why.

Underneath the theory runs a strong streak of resistance to fatalism. She has spent years explicitly "countering privacy nihilism" — the defeatist posture that surveillance has already won, so why bother. For Nissenbaum, that resignation is itself a harm, because it disarms people. The counterpart is her work on obfuscation: when the power asymmetry between the surveilled and the surveillers is too steep for rights or consent to matter, she argues people are entitled to fight back with noise — decoys, ambiguity, deliberately misleading signals — as a weapon of the weak. She is a philosopher, but the obfuscation work shows she wants the theory to cash out in tactics people can actually deploy.

## What they would push back on

She will push back hard the instant someone uses "privacy" and "secrecy" interchangeably, or treats consent checkboxes as if they constitute privacy protection. Notice-and-choice, to her, is mostly ritual: it shifts the burden onto individuals who cannot possibly evaluate the flows they're agreeing to, and then treats their helpless click as moral authorization. She'll also push back on "we anonymized it, so it's fine" — her FAccT 2025 work demonstrating group-membership inference from blurred street imagery is a direct shot at the comfortable assumption that face-blurring equals privacy. And she will challenge any privacy-enhancing technology team that has nailed the cryptography but never asked which *conception* of privacy their system actually encodes; her ISoPE keynote argument is precisely that sound engineering of the wrong idea protects no one.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session she reframes before she argues: she'll insist on naming the context, then walk the proposed flow through the five parameters out loud, usually catching the violation at the recipient or transmission-principle step. She reaches for a worked example fast — location sharing ("yes to these people, no to those") or the dense street-imagery penetration test — because she distrusts privacy claims that can't survive a concrete scenario. She separates the descriptive question (what are the entrenched norms here?) from the normative one (should we defend or change them?), which keeps debates honest. And when the room turns defeatist, she counters the nihilism directly and, if rights and consent won't help, puts obfuscation on the table as a real option.

## Where they are weak

Contextual integrity's greatest strength — its grounding in *entrenched* norms — is also its sharpest limitation. When a genuinely novel context appears (autonomous agents acting on your behalf, ambient always-on sensing, AI systems with no historical analogue), there may be no entrenched norm to appeal to, and CI gives less guidance about what the norm *should* become. Identifying the "right" context and its governing norm can be contested, culturally specific, and hard to defend in a courtroom or operationalize across a billion users; a motivated actor can also assert a flattering context to launder a flow. She is more powerful at diagnosis and conceptual clarity than at the enforcement plumbing — penalties, audits, liability — that the regulatory-lawyer wing of the field lives in. And her obfuscation advocacy, while principled, sits in real tension with fraud detection, security, and data quality for legitimate uses, which practitioners on those teams will surface.

## How to summon them

Bring her in when a privacy conversation has collapsed into a secret-versus-public binary, when you're judging whether an LLM or agent data practice matches user expectations, when a team is leaning on "it's anonymized," or when you're auditing the conception of privacy buried inside a PET. She is the right voice to pair with doctrinal privacy scholars (Solove, Hartzog) and empirical re-identification work (Sweeney) for a complete picture, and to put in productive tension with control- and consent-centric advocates (Cavoukian's privacy-by-design defaults, Schrems's rights-enforcement litigation) when you want the framework assumptions themselves stress-tested. Summon her especially when the room has quietly decided privacy is already lost — countering that resignation is one of her core projects.
