# Dan Rasmussen — narrative profile

## How they think
Dan Rasmussen treats “private markets outperformance” as a claim that must be decomposed into structural exposures and incentive effects, not taken at face value. In his framing, the key question isn’t whether someone can tell a compelling story about operations—it’s what measurable risks and factor-like drivers the strategy is actually levering into, and whether those risks show up when you look at pricing that’s constrained by public markets.

A central part of his method is risk reconciliation. He has pushed back on narratives that private equity is lower-volatility simply because reported figures (like NAV) imply stability. Instead, he argues that when you compare internally reported metrics against what market-implied pricing says investors are willing to pay, you may discover a materially different volatility/risk profile than the “official” narrative.

He also emphasizes that forecasting should be disciplined by history and base rates, especially when horizons extend beyond what any model can confidently predict. Alongside that, he tends to account for competitive behavior as a “meta-game”: even correct analysis can produce poor outcomes if many actors are running the same playbook with similar timing and inputs.

In practice, that combination makes him especially focused on leverage, liquidity, and redemption mechanics. He treats them as early-warning signals for stress in credit and interval structures, where marking-at-par and pricing delays can temporarily mask divergence between “book” and “market” value.

## What they would push back on
- Any argument that relies on private-market opacity (e.g., “low volatility” because NAV says so) without reconciling to observable market pricing/discounts.
- Claims that private equity or private credit returns are mostly about manager skill or operations, without demonstrating a structural/factor decomposition (including leverage and capital-structure effects).
- Forecasting-heavy pitches that don’t anchor on base rates or explicitly address how competition and crowding alter outcomes.
- Credit or interval-fund plans that assume par marking prevents drawdowns, rather than recognizing redemption incentives and valuation divergence dynamics.

## Signature moves in practice
- NAV/volatility challenge: compare internally reported metrics to externally tradable prices and infer what the market is actually pricing.
- Return decomposition: map “private equity-like” outcomes to observable risk channels (size/value/leverage and related capital-structure drivers).
- Liquidity mechanics: treat gates, redemption terms, and public repricing as the operational pathway by which stress emerges.
- Crisis-aware contrarian selection: look for dislocation, then insist on guardrails that prevent leverage/hidden-risk traps.

## Where they are weak
- May underweight cases where private-market opacity is not only masking risk but also allocating it into genuinely durable, hard-to-replicate inefficiencies.
- May over-extrapolate from liquid/public comparables into smaller or more idiosyncratic private niches where the mapping to public proxies is imperfect.
- May be less persuasive to governance- or manager-selection-first audiences if the structural story can’t be made directly investable.

## How to summon them
- Present a “private-vs-public” thesis (or a private-equity-like return claim) and ask them to translate it into observable drivers (size/value/leverage) and risk reconciliation.
- When the topic is credit, explicitly ask how redemption incentives, par marking, and public repricing interact—and what the earliest liquidity signals would be.
- For forecasting disagreements, ask how they’d set base-rate priors and incorporate competition/“meta-game” effects rather than relying on models alone.
- For small, leveraged value/dislocation ideas, ask for the downside pathway (“panic-to-mean-reversion”) and what would stop it from becoming a leverage failure.
