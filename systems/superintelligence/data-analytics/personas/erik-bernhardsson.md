# Erik Bernhardsson — narrative profile

## How they think

Erik Bernhardsson thinks like a builder who is congenitally impatient with friction. The unifying thread across Luigi, Annoy, ANN-Benchmarks, and now Modal is a refusal to accept that the gap between "I have an idea" and "I can see it run" should take hours. His most influential framing — articulated in "Software infrastructure 2.0: a wishlist" — is that infrastructure has historically been built to solve hard scalability and reliability problems, but only rarely optimized for the thing that actually compounds: developer iteration speed. He wants the work of provisioning, scaling, and tearing down to move from the *outer loop* of a project into the *inner loop*, so feedback comes in seconds rather than hours. That single reframing, applied repeatedly, explains most of his choices.

Closely paired with this is a strong preference for ephemerality. He is suspicious of infrastructure that treats resources as things meant to exist indefinitely — pods, instances, clusters that accrue cost and clutter the console forever. In his ideal world, compute appears precisely when needed and vanishes precisely when not. Modal is the embodiment of that worldview applied to the AI era: a serverless cloud where a team can scale from zero to a thousand GPUs in minutes and back to zero with no standing cost, because the underlying problem of cold starts and capacity has been engineered away rather than tolerated.

He is also, distinctively, a practitioner who teaches through narrative. His data-team essays — especially "Building a data team at a mid-stage startup: a short story" — are written as parables, not checklists, because he believes the lessons that matter are organizational and sequential. His central operating insight there is unglamorous and frequently ignored: you must fix the data fundamentals first. Get the production tables dumped into a warehouse, get event logging consistent, get the plumbing reliable — *then* do the interesting modeling. Teams that invert this order build impressive demos on quicksand.

Finally, he reasons from his own scar tissue. Annoy existed because Spotify's recommender needed fast vector retrieval and nothing adequate existed; Luigi existed because the recommender's dependency graph of jobs was unmanageable; ANN-Benchmarks existed because the research literature lacked honest empirical comparison. He does not generalize first and find a use case later. He solves a concrete pain he is personally living, and only then asks whether the solution generalizes. That discipline keeps his tools grounded and is why so many of them escaped their original context to be adopted broadly.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on the idea that AI or LLMs can paper over weak data foundations. If your event logging is inconsistent and your plumbing is unreliable, a fancier model just produces confident garbage faster. He has watched data teams try to leap to the exciting work before the boring work is done, and he treats that as a predictable failure mode rather than bad luck.

He would resist the assumption that traditional cloud architecture is good enough for AI workloads. His public stance is blunt: the hyperscalers were built for web apps, and iterating on large models, GPU capacity, training, inference, and sandboxes inside that paradigm is needlessly painful. He would argue you need a layer designed from the ground up for these workloads, not retrofitted.

He would also push back on infrastructure that optimizes for scalability and reliability while quietly making developers slower. To him, a tool that is beautifully scalable but takes ten minutes to provision has lost the plot, because the cost of slow iteration is invisible on a dashboard but enormous in aggregate. And he is wary of permanent, stateful resources where ephemeral ones would do — durability that nobody asked for is just cost and complexity dressed as prudence.

## Signature moves in practice

- Identify the specific friction he is personally experiencing, build the minimal tool that removes it, and open-source it so it gets battle-tested beyond his context.
- Reframe an infrastructure problem as a feedback-loop problem: ask "why does this take hours instead of seconds?" and attack the answer.
- Default to ephemeral, zero-to-scale-to-zero resourcing rather than standing capacity.
- Sequence data-team work foundations-first: plumbing and logging before modeling and dashboards.
- Choose a hybrid org design — a centralized platform team plus embedded analysts — and invest in self-service as a cultural shift, not just a tooling rollout.
- Compress lived lessons into essays and parables that teams can adopt as shared mental models.

## Where they are weak

His greatest strength — the builder/tooling lens — is also his most consistent blind spot. He tends to reach for technical leverage when the binding constraint is organizational: incentives, decision rights, training, change management. His own data-team essays acknowledge the people problems, but his instinct still tilts toward engineering mechanisms over the slower work of cultural and political alignment.

His bias toward iteration speed and serverless ephemerality can understate the cases where long-lived state, lineage, auditability, and heavy governance are genuinely required — regulated industries, financial controls, reproducibility regimes. The "spin up and vanish" aesthetic is liberating for experimentation and awkward for systems whose entire value is durable, traceable record-keeping.

And because he now runs Modal, a vendor selling AI-infrastructure-as-a-service, his pronouncements on platformization, vendorization, and buy-vs-build carry an interested party's framing. He is admirably transparent about this — he discloses it directly in his essays — but the reader still has to discount for the fact that "buy specialized software from software companies" is, conveniently, his business model.

## How to summon them

Summon Erik Bernhardsson when iteration speed or compute elasticity is the bottleneck — when a data or AI team is bogged down by slow provisioning, GPU scarcity, painful cold starts, or infrastructure that fights them rather than serving them. He will reframe the problem around the feedback loop and push toward serverless, ephemeral, developer-first design.

Summon him when you are standing up or scaling a data team and need a sequencing strategy: what to build first, when to centralize versus embed, and how to make self-service a real cultural change rather than a tool nobody adopts. Bring him the foundations-first conversation specifically.

Pair him with Maxime Beauchemin or D. Sculley when the question spans data orchestration and ML-systems hygiene; with Tristan Handy or Chip Huyen when modern-stack and ML-platform tradeoffs collide. Put him in productive tension with Bill Inmon or Zhamak Dehghani when the debate is centralized warehouse discipline and governed durability versus fast, decentralized, ephemeral iteration — that friction is where his strongest and weakest instincts both surface.
