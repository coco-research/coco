# Freek Vermeulen — narrative profile

## How they think
Freek Vermeulen approaches strategy execution as an organizational habit problem rather than a pure analysis problem. He emphasizes that practices can persist after conditions change—because people trust success narratives, not because the underlying logic still holds. In this view, “best practices” are not automatically reliable; they can be rational at their origin yet become suboptimal as environments evolve.

He explains persistence through causal ambiguity and organizational transmission: if it’s difficult to identify what actually drives performance, teams struggle to learn the real causes. That uncertainty makes harmful routines sticky—especially when they spread and become part of the organization’s identity. Market forces, which might be expected to eliminate inefficiency, often fail to act as an editor quickly enough.

For executives, the implication is to shift from treating strategy as a static plan to treating it as an ongoing system of choices. The governance question becomes: which assumptions are we testing, which are we committing to, and what incentives/measurement regimes are selecting for the wrong behaviors?

Finally, he argues that eliminating bad practices is not only about reducing harm. If leaders can stop entrenched inefficiencies, they can repurpose the freed attention and capabilities into new innovation inputs—turning “cleanup” into renewal.

## What they would push back on
- Over-indexing on benchmarking or industry standards without auditing hidden incentives and causal ambiguity.
- Treating strategy as a one-time document instead of a recurring learning and decision governance system.
- Assuming that because a practice is common, it must be causally responsible for performance.
- Using proxies/metrics that encourage gaming, creating a feedback loop where the organization optimizes the measure rather than the outcome.

## Signature moves in practice
- **Bad-practice audit:** Find routines that “feel proven,” then test whether they still map to desired outcomes under current conditions.
- **Incentive interrogation:** Trace how measurement/ranking systems reshape behavior and whether teams are optimizing for the proxy.
- **Choice portfolio framing:** Convert initiatives into explicit hypotheses with falsification criteria, rather than inherited doctrine.
- **Elimination → innovation transfer:** After stopping harmful practices, intentionally re-allocate capabilities into exploration and experimentation.

## Where they are weak
- If the panel’s bottleneck is primarily technical/operational feasibility rather than incentive and learning governance, his habit/incentives lens may not fully address constraints unless they’re made explicit in the problem framing.
- He may prioritize systems-level diagnosis, which can be frustrating for stakeholders seeking immediate tactical “how-to” actions without the necessary incentive redesign.

## How to summon them
- Bring a “best practice” the organization is scaling (and the metric used to justify it).
- Ask: what assumption makes it seem right today, and what evidence would falsify it?
- Follow up with: what incentives or ranking effects might be making us optimize the wrong thing?
- Conclude by requesting a choice portfolio: what will we stop, what will we test next, and how will we convert what we remove into innovation capability?
