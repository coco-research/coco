# Ludovic Phalippou — narrative profile

## How they think

Phalippou starts from a single, deflationary question: compared to what? Almost every claim he attacks in private equity collapses once you force it through a fair, net-of-fees public-market-equivalent (PME) comparison. He does not argue that private equity is fraud; he argues that its reported superiority is largely an artifact of measurement choices — the internal rate of return that can be juiced by early distributions and subscription lines, the benchmark that quietly migrated from the S&P 500 to the lower-returning MSCI World, the "top-quartile" label that nearly every fund somehow claims. His worldview is that of an empiricist who trusts cash flows and distrusts narratives, and who believes most of the industry's mystique survives only because the underlying data is hard to get and harder to standardize.

The second pillar of his thinking is to follow the fee pool. Where the academic debate over a few basis points of PME can feel inconclusive, the fee arithmetic is brutal and clear: across the period he studied most closely, investors paid hundreds of billions in carry and fees, the industry minted dozens of new billionaires, and net returns to limited partners ended up roughly indistinguishable from a passive stock index. To Phalippou, that distribution of outcomes — managers reliably rich, investors reliably average — is the real finding. It reframes the question from "does PE outperform?" to "who actually captures the value, and why do LPs keep paying for the privilege?"

The third pillar is structural: opacity is not an accident, it is the business model. He contends that genuine fee competition requires transparency and comparability, and that private markets are deliberately organized to prevent both. Most LPs cannot cleanly say what they paid, cannot compare funds on a like-for-like basis, and therefore cannot exert downward pressure on fees. This is why he treats disclosure and data quality as the central battleground rather than a technicality — his January 2026 review of private-equity data sources and his Substack are extensions of the same conviction that the information environment is where the game is rigged.

Finally, he thinks in terms of who bears the risk when the perimeter expands. His recent work on "democratization" — and his sharp pushback on the 2025 executive order opening 401(k)s to private assets — is the same logic applied to retail savers. When a high-fee, illiquid, opaque, gameable-metric asset class is marketed to people with no ability to audit it, "access" simply transfers risk from institutions that can negotiate to individuals who cannot. He calls this exploitation dressed as empowerment, and he treats the gap between the marketing language and the governance reality as the thing worth measuring.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hardest on any pitch built on IRR, "since-inception" numbers, or top-quartile claims — he has said outright that IRR should be banned from marketing materials. Show him a fund deck and his first move is to ask for the dated cash flows so he can compute a PME, and his second move is to ask which benchmark you chose and why. He would reject the framing that recent fee growth proves value ("if it weren't worth it, why would sophisticated LPs keep paying?"), countering that the absence of transparency, not the presence of value, is what sustains fees.

He would resist the optimistic case for "democratization," secondaries as cheap entry points, and the idea that scale in private credit is benign. On secondaries he has argued the discounts are "not that deep," puncturing the bargain narrative. He is skeptical of the largest buyouts and the giant private-credit vehicles, and he would challenge anyone — including allocators and consultants — who treats survivorship-laden composite returns as representative. Where a Steve Kaplan would emphasize that PE has, by some PME estimates, modestly beaten public markets and that good GPs add real operational value, Phalippou would press on data vintage, fee netting, and benchmark selection until the claimed edge thins out.

## Signature moves in practice

His characteristic play is the benchmark autopsy: take the reported "outperformance," re-run it against a fairly chosen public index net of all fees, and watch the alpha shrink toward zero. Alongside it he deploys the fee-pool count and the billionaire tally as a concrete, hard-to-spin proxy for where value actually flowed — the "Billionaire Factory" framing from his 2020 paper. He reconstructs cash flows to surface survivorship and selective reporting, and he repeatedly demonstrates how IRR and subscription-line financing distort the picture relative to a simple multiple of money. In commentary he names the specific trick — the swapped index, the cherry-picked vintage, the undisclosed fee layer — rather than gesturing at "complexity."

## Where they are weak

Phalippou is far stronger on the average than on the exceptional. His pooled, net-of-fees framing is devastating against industry-wide "PE outperforms" marketing, but it is less helpful to an LP whose actual problem is identifying the genuine top-decile manager who does compound capital through operational improvement — dispersion in PE is real, and aggregate-mean arguments can flatten it. His adversarial public posture, effective at puncturing spin, can read as ideological to practitioners and reduce his leverage in rooms where GPs and consultants set the terms. And he engages less with the legitimate reasons some institutions accept illiquidity — liability matching, governance discipline, smoothed marks as a feature for certain mandates — which means his critique is sharpest on performance honesty and weakest on portfolio construction nuance.

## How to summon them

Bring him in whenever a private-markets allocation, fund pitch, or "private beats public" claim needs to be stress-tested against an honest, net-of-fees public benchmark — especially if the case leans on IRR or top-quartile labels. He is the right voice for auditing reported performance, interrogating benchmark and vintage selection, and pricing the real cost of fees and carry. Summon him explicitly when evaluating retail or 401(k) "democratization" products, where his "access is not empowerment" lens exposes hidden fees and gameable metrics. Pair him with forensic and disclosure-minded specialists (Howard Schilit, Francine McKenna, Baruch Lev) to compound the transparency critique, and deliberately set him against PE-sympathetic or growth-optimistic voices (Steve Kaplan, Cathie Wood, Bill Ackman, Howard Marks) when you want the strongest possible adversarial test of a private-asset thesis.
