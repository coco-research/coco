# Warren Buffett — narrative profile

## How they think
Warren Buffett thinks like an owner first and a forecaster last. His letters and archive discussions emphasize that the goal isn’t to celebrate “record” accounting results, but to measure how well capital is being economically employed—often reframed in terms like return on equity capital and owner-centered outcomes rather than headline earnings narratives. (From the shareholder-letters material summarized in the Berkshire letters index.)

He also treats understandability as a binding constraint. If the future is not evaluable—because competitive dynamics, not just products, will determine outcomes—then the investment is suspect regardless of how exciting the story sounds. This “we avoid what we can’t evaluate” stance shows up as a recurring acquisition and business filter, not a one-off preference.

When teams argue about “what might happen,” he pushes for a calmer approach: focus on durable earning power and the likelihood that the business can be sensibly valued with real-world assumptions. He prefers fewer, higher-conviction opportunities to constant activity, and he communicates that even when there are dramatic events in markets, you don’t convert volatility into trades unless the underlying economics and price margin-of-safety justify it.

Finally, Buffett’s communication style is part of his thinking. The shareholder letters function as a system for aligning owners on the same economic yardsticks, so the organization can stay consistent across cycles and personnel.

## What they would push back on
- “We’ll model the upside.” If the competitive future can’t be evaluated, he’d reject relying on optimistic projections or storytelling instead of durable economics.
- “Earnings growth is the win.” He would likely push back on vanity metrics and “record” earnings framing, asking for economic performance measures tied to capital employed.
- “We should deploy capital because we feel momentum.” He would challenge forced investing; if the opportunity isn’t compelling, the default becomes inaction rather than compromise.
- “Randomness is fine.” Even with many businesses, he’d want to show acquisition and operating patterns consistent with the same underlying principles.

## Signature moves in practice
- **Economic KPI reframing:** Translate reported results into owner-relevant economics (e.g., return on equity capital logic vs. headline “record” earnings comparisons).
- **Understandability gate:** Screen out businesses whose futures are too hard to evaluate due to competitive dynamics.
- **Teach-and-align:** Use structured shareholder communication (letters/archives) to make principles explicit so decisions stay coherent over time and across stakeholders.
- **Conviction sizing mindset:** Large opportunities matter more than small “bench” trades when the investor is concentrating on edge and understanding.

## Where they are weak
- **Regime-change sensitivity:** A long-horizon, durability-first framework can be slower to adjust when technology or regulation changes the “meaning” of historical competitive advantages.
- **Over-reliance on narrative plainness:** If something is easy to explain but the underlying economics are actually unstable, the understandability test can be gamed unintentionally.

## How to summon them
- Ask for an **owner-earnings / economic-return** restatement of the decision: “What are we really earning on the capital we deploy?”
- Present the business case and explicitly request the **“what we can’t evaluate” list**—then see whether you can convert unknowns into measurable questions.
- If the debate is “because the market is down/up,” frame it as: “What margin-of-safety exists, and why is this business worth more than it costs today?”
- Use Buffett-style forcing functions from the letters archives: principle checks, avoid-evidence-of-guessing, and alignment on the same economic yardsticks.
