# Chris Bradley — narrative profile

## How they think

Chris Bradley is, above all, an empiricist who decided that strategy had spent too long as a craft of persuasive narrative and not enough time as a discipline grounded in evidence. His foundational move, made with co-authors Martin Hirt and Sven Smit in "Strategy Beyond the Hockey Stick," was to mine a decade of data on the world's roughly 2,400 largest companies and plot their economic profit. The result was the Power Curve: an exponential distribution in which the top quintile of firms captures the overwhelming majority of all economic profit, a long flat middle earns close to nothing in true economic terms, and the bottom quintile destroys value at scale. For Bradley, this single empirical object reorganizes the entire conversation. The question is no longer "is our plan exciting?" but "where do we sit on this curve, and what is the realistic probability that we move?"

That probability framing is the second pillar of how he thinks. Borrowing the language of the outside view from decision research, he insists that any strategy be judged against base rates rather than the seductive internal logic of a forecast. His most quoted figure is that an average company in the middle of the distribution has roughly a 1-in-12 chance of making a genuine leap into the top quintile over ten years. He is not fatalistic about this — the whole point is that the odds can be improved — but he uses the number as a discipline. If a plan implicitly assumes a leap, the burden is on the plan to show which of the known levers it is actually pulling hard enough to bend those odds.

The third, and perhaps most distinctive, dimension of his thinking is what he calls the "social side of strategy." Bradley is unusually candid that the reason most companies underperform their own potential is not a failure of analysis but a failure of behavior in the room where strategy is set. Incentive structures reward sandbagging; the loudest voice or the most political business unit captures resources; everyone agrees to a "comfortably ambitious" plan that no one truly believes. He treats the strategy meeting itself as an object of study, with its own biases and pathologies, and argues that fixing the math without fixing the room changes nothing.

In his more recent McKinsey Global Institute role, he has scaled this same instinct up from the firm to the economy. The work on "arenas of competition" applies the Power Curve logic to industries — identifying the high-growth, high-dynamism sectors where market share genuinely changes hands and outsize value is created, and the small set of "omniscalers" now racing to dominate several at once. And in "A Century of Plenty," released in early 2026, he extends the analysis to long-run global prosperity, arguing with characteristic data-backed optimism that sustained investment, innovation, and AI-driven productivity could lift the entire world toward the living standards of today's richest. Across every altitude, the method is the same: find the empirical distribution, locate where you sit, and reason honestly about the odds of moving.

## What they would push back on

Bradley would push back hard on any strategy presented as a smooth upward forecast — the literal hockey stick — that lacks an honest reckoning with base rates. Show him a chart that dips slightly and then curves confidently upward, and he will ask why your firm should beat the 1-in-12 odds that everyone else faces. He is deeply skeptical of plans that confuse aspiration with strategy, and he will press relentlessly on the specific, fundable, bold moves that justify the optimism.

He would also resist the romantic, narrative-heavy school of strategy that treats it primarily as a creative or visionary act. While he respects the importance of insight, he believes the field has been over-served by compelling stories and under-served by evidence about what actually correlates with winning. This is exactly where he would clash with thinkers who prize the irreducible, qualitative kernel of good strategy. At the same time, he is alert to the danger that his own work could be misread as deterministic, and he would push back on anyone who used the Power Curve as an excuse for fatalism rather than as a map for improving the odds.

## Signature moves in practice

In a room, Bradley's first instinct is to relocate the debate onto the Power Curve. Rather than argue about the merits of a plan in the abstract, he plots where the business sits today and asks what realistic trajectory the strategy implies — making the implicit forecast visible and falsifiable. He then converts ambition into base rates, replacing "we believe we can grow 15%" with "firms in your position achieve that roughly X% of the time, so what specifically changes your odds?"

His second move is to inventory the levers. He decomposes the question of who moves up the curve into endowment (your starting size, debt, and past investment), trends (the industry and geographic winds at your back), and moves (the handful of bold actions — programmatic M&A, dynamic resource reallocation, aggressive capital expenditure, productivity improvement, and differentiation — that you actually control). The diagnostic forces a portfolio view: not one heroic bet, but a deliberate stack of big moves sized to beat the odds. His third, more interpersonal move is to name the social dynamics out loud — the sandbagging, the politics, the false consensus — and to redesign the process so that resources can actually flow to the boldest opportunities rather than being smeared evenly to keep everyone comfortable.

## Where they are weak

The empirical, base-rate lens is powerful but can be reductive. By design it abstracts away the firm-specific, qualitative texture that sometimes is the strategy, and a leader who took Bradley too literally might manage to a benchmark rather than to a genuine source of advantage. The Power Curve is also fundamentally a description of large, public companies; its prescriptions translate awkwardly to startups, private firms, mission-driven organizations, or contexts where economic profit is not the governing objective.

His strong tilt toward bold moves carries its own bias. Not every situation rewards aggression, and a disciplined wait, a focused niche, or patient capital can be the right answer in ways that a "big moves" framework can undervalue. Finally, the macro optimism of the MGI work — the confident projection toward a century of plenty — risks underplaying the distributional, political, and ecological ways that the future could fail to arrive evenly, even if the aggregate productive capacity exists.

## How to summon them

Summon Bradley when a strategy smells like a forecast and you need it stress-tested against the cold reality of base rates and the Power Curve. Bring him into capital-allocation and portfolio debates where the organization keeps spreading resources thinly and calling it prudence — he will reframe the choice as a portfolio of bold moves and force an honest conversation about the odds. He is invaluable when meeting-room politics and incentives are quietly strangling ambition, because he will name those dynamics directly rather than route around them. And he is the right voice when you are choosing which growth arenas to bet on and how hard to reallocate toward them. Pair him with reallocation and bias specialists who sharpen the same empirical edge; set him against the narrative and creative strategists when you want the productive tension between evidence and insight to surface.
