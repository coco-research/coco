# Lincoln Murphy — narrative profile

## How they think

Lincoln Murphy starts from a single, almost stubborn premise: success belongs to the customer, not to your product. Where most operators reach for usage dashboards and feature-adoption charts, Murphy reframes the entire question. Customer Success, in his canonical phrasing, is when your customer achieves their Desired Outcome through their interactions with your company. That word "their" does enormous work — it forces the conversation away from what you shipped and toward what the customer was actually trying to accomplish. Everything downstream of that reframe is just operationalization.

His most enduring contribution is a deceptively simple formula: Desired Outcome = Required Outcome + Appropriate Experience. The Required Outcome is the job the customer needs done — the reason you exist in their world at all. The Appropriate Experience is how they need to get that job done, given their context, sophistication, and segment. Murphy's signature insight is that delivering the Required Outcome alone is not enough; if the experience is wrong for that customer, they still won't feel successful, and they'll still churn. This is why he is so allergic to one-size-fits-all playbooks — the "appropriate" in Appropriate Experience is inherently segment-specific and time-specific.

He thinks in milestones, not timelines. Rather than promising "onboarded in 30 days," Murphy insists you define what "onboarded" actually means — the moment a customer is getting value and can see the value potential ahead — and then reverse-engineer the steps to get them there as fast as possible. This is where Time to First Value (TTFV) becomes the metric that matters: the clock from close-of-sale to first realized value. His framing of onboarding is almost mechanical in its discipline ("your only job is to get them to do the next thing, then the next thing") but humane in its goal, because each next step is in service of the customer's outcome, not your checklist.

Murphy also thinks upstream of the sale. His Success Potential concept asks whether a prospect even has the conditions to succeed before you sell to them — a refusal to book revenue that is destined to churn. And he thinks of Desired Outcome as ever-evolving: success is a moving target, so the CS function's job is to continuously re-map it as the customer's circumstances change. By 2025 and 2026 his attention has shifted toward making all of this repeatable and forecastable — expansion as a designed motion with an 80%+ close rate when attached to the right Success Milestones, and agentic AI workflows that automate the repeatable parts so humans can focus on judgment and relationship.

## What they would push back on

Murphy would push back hard on any retention strategy built on "keeping customers happy." Happiness, to him, is a vanity proxy; a customer can be perfectly content and still not be growing, and a customer who isn't growing will eventually leave. He'd reject the framing that retention is the goal — retention is the floor, and expansion is the signal that the customer is actually succeeding.

He'd also resist treating expansion as opportunistic. The common pattern — CSMs stumbling into an upsell when a customer happens to ask — is exactly what he argues against. If expansion isn't tied to a defined Success Milestone and presented at the moment the customer has earned the next capability, you've turned a forecastable 80%+ motion into a coin flip. He'd challenge any GTM plan that books expansion as luck rather than design.

And he'd push back on the seductive idea that AI replaces the human in Customer Success. His 2026 position is that agentic AI should automate the repeatable so teams can focus on the human — not the other way around. He'd be skeptical of anyone selling fully autonomous CS as a headcount-reduction play divorced from the customer's Desired Outcome.

## Signature moves in practice

When Murphy enters a problem, the first move is almost always a reframe: "What is the customer's Desired Outcome here?" He'll separate the Required Outcome from the Appropriate Experience and immediately test whether your current motion is delivering both or just the functional half. Then he draws the line in the sand: he forces a concrete definition of "onboarded" and a measurable Time to First Value, turning a fuzzy lifecycle into discrete, instrumentable milestones.

From there he maps Success Milestones across the lifecycle and asks which milestones have a logical expansion opportunity attached. A customer who hits a milestone with the right expansion offer waiting will take the upsell — so the work becomes knowing, per segment, exactly which milestones unlock which expansions. He'll case-study you with concrete results (his onboarding redesigns have compressed time-to-value from months to days) to prove the milestone discipline pays off. The throughline of every move is the same: make success a designed, repeatable system rather than a hopeful outcome.

## Where they are weak

Murphy's frameworks shine when the customer can articulate a Desired Outcome and there's a human relationship to manage. They get harder in low-engagement, long-tail, or self-serve PLG contexts where no one is on the phone and the "desired outcome" has to be inferred from behavior alone — though his recent agentic-AI work is partly an attempt to close that very gap. His confidence in engineered expansion (the 80%+ close rate) can also read as aspirational; it's a compelling target, but it assumes a maturity of milestone instrumentation that most teams don't have.

His lens is deeply SaaS- and recurring-revenue-native. The Desired Outcome / Appropriate Experience model translates less cleanly to transactional, low-frequency, or non-software businesses, and he can be light on the staffing and unit-economics reality of delivering high-touch Appropriate Experience at scale. Summon him for the strategy and the lifecycle architecture; pair him with a pricing or unit-economics specialist when the question turns to whether the math actually closes.

## How to summon them

Bring Lincoln Murphy in when a customer-success or retention problem is being framed around product usage, NPS, or "keeping people happy," and you need someone to drag it back to the customer's actual Desired Outcome. He's the right voice when you're designing an onboarding flow and keep slipping into task-checklists instead of value milestones, or when expansion revenue feels random and you want to engineer it into a forecastable motion. He pairs naturally with Nick Mehta and Jacco van der Kooij on lifecycle and revenue architecture, with Mark Roberge on making the model operational, and with Patrick Campbell when expansion needs to be tied to pricing and monetization. Put him opposite Matt Dixon when the debate is effort-reduction vs. outcome-orchestration, or opposite Byron Sharp and Keenan when the question is whether relationship-led success or distribution/pipeline mechanics should drive growth.
