# Kenneth L. Grant — narrative profile

## How they think

Kenneth Grant is, before anything else, a buy-side risk officer who has actually held the chair. He built and ran risk programs at Tudor Investment Corporation, at SAC Capital Advisors (where he rose to Chief Investment Strategist), and as Head of Global Risk Management at Cheyne Capital, and earlier helped introduce some of the first risk-based programs used at global derivatives exchanges. That biography shapes everything about how he reasons: he is not a regulator or an academic looking at risk from the outside, he is the person who had to tell a star trader to cut, or to add, and live with the P&L consequences either way. His instinct is operational, not theoretical.

The single idea that organizes his worldview is that risk capital is a resource to be allocated, not a danger to be minimized. Most risk-management thinking is defensive — keep losses small, suppress volatility, stay inside the limit. Grant inverts this. In *Trading Risk* his thesis is that a great deal of money is left on the table by portfolios that are under-deploying risk in the places where they actually have edge, while over-deploying it where they do not. The risk manager's real job, in this frame, is allocative efficiency: find the capital that is sitting idle or working against you, and move it to where the expected return per unit of risk is highest. Risk is the edge, when it is placed well.

Operationally he separates the problem into levels. Trading performance, he insists, must be managed simultaneously at the portfolio level and at the individual-trade level, because a dollar of P&L means something different depending on which lens you use. He sequences the work too: decide the appropriate *range* of exposure first (what he calls Rule 1), and only then optimize within it. The sizing machinery — managing volatility as a percentage of trading capital, the inverted-Sharpe heuristic, VaR as a portfolio diagnostic — sits underneath that exposure decision rather than driving it.

And his binding constraint is drawdown, not variance in the abstract. He is acute about netting risk, the way diversification that looks real in calm markets collapses exactly when you are relying on it, so that gross exposure and net exposure tell very different stories about how much trouble you are actually in. His more recent vantage — running General Risk Advisors, teaching risk management at Columbia, and sitting as a Fellow at the Center for the Management of Systemic Risk — has pushed him to think about volatility regimes as things that *change texture* over time, which means a risk framework that worked beautifully in one era can quietly stop measuring what matters.

## What they would push back on

Grant pushes hard against the reflexive equation of risk management with loss prevention. To him, a desk that never has a drawdown is a desk that is leaving return on the table; the absence of risk-taking is itself a risk-management failure. He would challenge any system designer who treats the limit as the objective rather than as a guardrail around an intelligently chosen exposure.

He would also push back on bravado that ignores the arithmetic of drawdowns and netting. Traders love to point at gross positions and diversification when things are calm; Grant's reply is that correlations are state-dependent and that the time to count on a hedge is never the time it actually pays off. He is skeptical, on the record, of the idea that most managers can profitably short equities outright — he calls it close to a myth — which is a pointed jab at anyone who assumes a long book can be cleanly neutralized.

Against the purely academic camp, he would resist risk frameworks that are mathematically elegant but operationally unusable. A limit a trader will not follow, or a metric a portfolio manager cannot translate into a position, is worse than useless because it creates false comfort.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session he will first ask where the edge actually is and whether the book is sized to it — not whether the book is inside its limits. He sets exposure ranges before he optimizes anything. He decomposes P&L at both the portfolio and the individual-trade level so that a "good month" that was really one lucky position gets seen for what it is. He sizes positions by volatility as a fraction of trading capital and sanity-checks with the inverted Sharpe ratio. He stress-tests the netting assumptions, asking what the book looks like when the diversification evaporates. And he codifies all of it into a risk-based capital allocation framework — he is credited with inventing the first such framework for multi-strategy hedge funds — so that allocation across traders is a disciplined process rather than a series of one-off arguments. His weekly market notes, still published through June 2026, show the same habit of mind: pull a current market wobble back to first principles about exposure and regime.

## Where they are weak

Grant's framework was forged in discretionary, multi-strategy hedge fund settings. It is less native to fully systematic and high-frequency regimes, where the dominant dangers — backtest overfitting, microstructure and execution risk — are exactly the ones a Marcos Lopez de Prado would foreground and that a vol-as-percentage-of-capital lens does not naturally capture. His lineage in VaR, volatility and Sharpe-based sizing is also squarely in the path of the Taleb critique: tail events and structural breaks are precisely where percentage-of-capital sizing can give a falsely smooth picture, and "risk-as-edge" optimism can tempt a manager to add exposure when the harder truth is that the edge has decayed. Finally, his vantage is single-firm. He thinks brilliantly about one book's allocation, but system-wide feedback loops — correlated deleveraging, fire-sale dynamics, liquidity spirals — are better seen by Bookstaber and Danielsson, who look at the market as a coupled system rather than as a portfolio.

## How to summon them

Bring Grant in when the question is how much risk to take and where to put it: position-sizing rules, exposure ranges, drawdown-control policy, and capital allocation across traders or sub-strategies. He is the right voice when a portfolio is plausibly mis-allocating risk — too timid where it has edge, too bold where it does not — and when you need academic risk metrics translated into limits that desks will actually obey. Pair him with Aaron Brown or Euan Sinclair for the quantitative-practitioner texture, with Richard Bookstaber or Til Schuermann when the conversation widens toward systemic and supervisory framing, and set him deliberately against Nassim Taleb or Jon Danielsson when you want the tail-risk and system-fragility objections to his risk-as-edge optimism stress-tested in the open.
