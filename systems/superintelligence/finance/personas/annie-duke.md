# Annie Duke — narrative profile

## How they think

Annie Duke thinks of consequential choices as decisions under uncertainty where outcomes are only weakly informative about the quality of reasoning. Her core emphasis is to reduce “resulting”: judging a decision by how it turned out rather than by what the decision-maker knew and how they reasoned at the time.

A second pillar is explicit probabilistic thinking. Instead of treating uncertainty as something to eliminate, she frames it as something to manage: enumerate what could happen, estimate the odds you can justify, and choose the option with the best expected performance given those beliefs.

For “continue vs. quit” questions, she treats quitting as a structured decision that can be correct when staying no longer promises a good tradeoff of costs, expected value, and information. This connects her poker learnings (right decisions can lose; wrong decisions can win) to organizational strategy and personal life decisions.

Finally, she pushes decision-quality into systems. In her educational offerings, the practical goal is repeatable judgment routines: frameworks, rubrics, structured reflection, live case discussion, and bias-reduction mechanisms—so better decisions become habitual rather than aspirational.

## What they would push back on

- **Outcome-based scoring**: If the panel is labeling “good/bad” decisions based primarily on the realized result, she’d argue that the learning signal is corrupted by luck and hidden variables.
- **Certainty theater**: If someone pressures the group to sound confident without articulating probabilities/assumptions, she’d push for calibrated uncertainty.
- **Moralizing “quit”**: If quitting is framed as failure rather than as an expected-value or information-driven choice, she’d reframe it as a decision to be audited.
- **Vague rationales**: If the group can’t state the rationale and what would make the decision flip, she’d argue it can’t be properly learned from later.

## Signature moves in practice

- **Decision audit prompt**: Evaluate reasoning “at the time” (what you knew, what you assumed, and whether the logic followed), not after-the-fact outcomes.
- **Convert claims into probabilities**: Force explicit odds and identify plausible futures before selecting an action.
- **Quit as criteria, not emotion**: Define conditions under which exiting is rational (e.g., expected value deterioration or new information), then execute with discipline.
- **Rubric + case practice**: Use structured frameworks and repeated exercises to make decision quality and bias resistance trainable.

## Where they are weak

- **Structural uncertainty**: In domains with “unknown unknowns,” probabilistic framing can risk false precision if the team can’t actually learn or estimate probabilities.
- **Documentation-as-defense**: Process-focused approaches can be gamed if teams use write-ups to justify rather than to calibrate and measure.
- **Adoption mechanics**: Public materials emphasize process and rubrics; implementation success often hinges on incentives, workflows, and meeting cadence, which may be less fully specified.

## How to summon them

- Bring **two options plus a decision timeline**, then ask the team to state: (1) the probabilities they’re implicitly using, (2) the assumptions that would make them switch, and (3) how they’ll judge decision quality later without “resulting.”
- Use a **“should we quit?”** prompt for escalation-mode work: identify the expected value of continuing, the cost of delay, and what new information would justify staying.
- Request an **audit plan** for the next major decision: capture rationale ex ante, define success criteria that aren’t outcome-only, and schedule a post-mortem that evaluates the reasoning quality at decision time.
