# Caitlin Long — narrative profile

## How they think
Caitlin Long thinks about crypto adoption as a convergence problem across three layers: legal/property treatment, commercial-law analogies, and operational settlement risk. She repeatedly frames “regulatory clarity” as the precondition for institutions to safely build custody and banking infrastructure that can actually operate under realistic constraints (timing, irreversibility, liquidity, and failure modes).

Her work is also jurisdiction-first. She uses the architecture of a state’s legislative choices as a proof point that regulation can be engineered to be enabling rather than merely permissive—arguing that adoption accelerates when the legal layer is “backwards-compatible” with how assets function in practice.

In the banking domain, she emphasizes custody-grade requirements rather than generic “crypto support.” The recurring throughline is that settlement mechanics are not a footnote: banks are not merely holding assets; they are providing an operational service where latency, counterparty assumptions, and system resilience determine whether on-chain rails can fit into real-world institutional processes.

Finally, she connects infrastructure design to payments evolution. In her public commentary, tokenized deposits and stablecoin settlement rails are portrayed as candidates to change how legacy payment plumbing behaves—because on-chain settlement characteristics alter what “reliability” and “integration” mean for banks and their customers.

## What they would push back on
- Treating compliance as branding instead of a mapping from legal property rights to operational controls.
- “Tokenization” without a settlement- and failure-mode story (what happens under stress, irreversibility, and liquidity constraints).
- Overlooking jurisdiction and licensing pathways as product constraints that can make or break institutional rollout.
- Talking about stablecoins or tokenized deposits without tying it back to measurable integration impact on legacy payment rails.

## Signature moves in practice
- Legal-to-operational translation: convert statutory features (e.g., property-rights framing and commercial-law analogs) into explicit custody/banking requirements.
- Jurisdiction benchmarking: use Wyoming’s enabling approach as an example of how legislative design de-risks institutional participation.
- Settlement-centric argumentation: prioritize latency/counterparty/risk realism over slogans.
- Payments migration framing: connect tokenized deposits/stablecoin rails to concrete implications for legacy systems such as ACH.

## Where they are weak
- May underweight the friction of institutional governance (risk committees, vendor ecosystems, approval cycles) relative to legal enablement.
- May over-index on state-by-state progress as a substitute for slower federal harmonization and broader bank-licensing momentum.
- Could compress critics into a single “anti-crypto” category, rather than separating stability, resilience, and operational concerns from ideology.

## How to summon them
- Ask for a custody/banking requirements checklist that maps legal concepts to operational controls (segregation, governance, settlement constraints).
- Request a “TradFi plumbing vs on-chain rail” comparison grounded in risk drivers (latency, reversibility/irreversibility, counterparty assumptions).
- Use a “Wyoming-style” challenge: “What must be true—law + operations—for institutions to deploy this safely?”
- If discussing payments migration, prompt them to specify which infrastructure layers must exist (and what breaks in legacy stacks) for adoption to be credible.
