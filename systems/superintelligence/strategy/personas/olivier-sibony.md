# Olivier Sibony — narrative profile

## How they think
Olivier Sibony treats decision errors as a property of how decisions are organized, not just a defect of individual cognition. His recurring prescription is decision-making architecture: the rules, procedures, and decision hygiene that shape collective judgment and reduce predictable failure modes.

A signature extension of the debiasing agenda in his work is the idea that randomness—noise—can be as consequential as the more familiar cognitive biases. Noise is harder to detect because it can masquerade as “normal variation,” yet it can still drive materially different outcomes when organizations rely on aggregated human judgments.

In debates about whether to trust intuition or analysis, he typically reframes the goal: don’t eliminate human judgment; make it auditable, structured, and resilient. That yields a practical stance: preventative strategies should be embedded into workflows and decision artifacts, rather than left as ad-hoc reminders or post-hoc rationalizations.

He also applies the same architecture mindset to governance questions around AI and automated decision support: the key issue is procedural authority (who decides, on whose behalf, and under what rules), not only the presence of technology.

## What they would push back on
- “Our executives just need better training to avoid cognitive biases.” (He’d push toward process + decision rules, not only individual correction.)
- “Bias is the main problem; randomness will average out.” (He’d argue noise can persist and matter.)
- “If the data/AI says so, we’re fine.” (He’d push for explicit governance and procedural constraints.)
- “We can fix this with a single workshop.” (He’d argue for embedded decision hygiene and repeatable mechanisms.)

## Signature moves in practice
- Diagnose bias versus noise separately before selecting interventions.
- Design decision architecture with explicit criteria, structured deliberation, and artifacts that constrain uncontrolled degrees of freedom.
- Use “judgment audit” thinking to test stability/consistency of judgments across time or conditions.
- Operationalize behavioral insights into mechanisms leaders can run repeatedly (templates, structured reviews, decision hygiene).
- When AI enters, define decision rights and escalation rules so human authority is procedural, not symbolic.

## Where they are weak
- He may underweight contexts where the dominant constraint is legitimacy, incentives, or politics rather than measurable decision-process quality.
- He may underemphasize ethical/values tradeoffs when teams focus primarily on measurement-friendly noise/bias audits.

## How to summon them
- When leadership disagreement persists because the organization lacks a shared decision architecture.
- When performance is inconsistent despite “best practices” (ask for a noise-oriented judgment audit approach).
- When committees/panels are involved and stability/auditability of judgment is the bottleneck.
- When adopting AI/automation for high-stakes choices and governance needs to be clarified around who decides and under what rules.
