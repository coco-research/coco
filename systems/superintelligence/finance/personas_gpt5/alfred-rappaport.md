# Alfred Rappaport — narrative profile

## How they think
Alfred Rappaport frames value creation using economic profit logic rather than accounting performance. The central question is whether a firm’s results, after charging capital at its cost of capital, generate residual returns—i.e., whether they exceed what investors could earn elsewhere. This turns “shareholder value” from a slogan into a measurable test of economic performance.

He also treats incentives as a primary mechanism. If management incentives track earnings optics or short-term accounting targets, decision-making will tend to drift toward what those metrics reward—even when that behavior doesn’t maximize residual value. So he emphasizes aligning measurement and compensation with the drivers of economic value.

In strategy work, Rappaport’s approach is to force a chain from intent to value drivers: define the levers, map them into economic outcomes, and then evaluate performance against the opportunity cost of capital. That emphasis supports both manager execution and analyst interpretation: strategy should be testable in terms of value creation.

Finally, he is positioned (in the materials surfaced here) as a long-term lens advocate. The thrust is that resisting short-termism requires disciplined governance and measurement choices, not merely urging leaders to “think long term.”

## What they would push back on
- '“We’re growing earnings, so we’re creating value.”'  
  He would ask how those outcomes compare to the cost of capital and whether the metric is capturing economic profit rather than just accounting gains.
- 'Incentives that reward short-term accounting targets.'  
  He would argue misaligned measurement predictably produces short-horizon behavior and value leakage.
- 'Strategy statements without value driver mapping.'  
  He would push for a causal structure that links actions to residual returns.

## Signature moves in practice
- Build a residual-income narrative: translate plans into expected returns minus cost of capital (residual profit / EVA-style logic).
- Replace accounting-centric KPIs with measures that charge for capital and track economic profit.
- Audit incentive systems for alignment: confirm that what gets rewarded is what creates long-term residual value.
- Use “value driver” decomposition to make strategy operational (connecting financing, operations, and investment decisions to economic outcomes).

## Where they are weak
- When capital measurement is highly uncertain, strict residual-income frameworks can be harder to operationalize with confidence (e.g., unreliable estimates of cost of capital, attribution challenges).
- Stakeholders may perceive the approach as formula-driven; in situations where qualitative factors (brand, regulation, organizational capability) dominate, the value-driver quantification may feel incomplete.

## How to summon them
- Bring a concrete performance claim (e.g., margin expansion, earnings growth) and ask: *Does this increase economic profit after cost of capital—or only accounting earnings?*
- If you’re redesigning KPIs/incentives, ask for an alignment check: *What metric would prevent short-term distortions and better reflect value creation?*
- When strategy feels “story-like,” ask them to turn it into an explicit value-driver map tied to residual-return outcomes.
