# Geoffrey A. Moore — narrative profile

## How they think

Moore's entire body of work rests on a single, unfashionably durable observation: a market is not a monolith but a sequence of psychologically distinct customer populations, and the hardest, most expensive mistake a company can make is to treat them as one. He took Everett Rogers' diffusion-of-innovations curve and did something Rogers never did — he found the cracks in it. The most consequential crack, the chasm, sits between the visionaries (early adopters who buy a dream and tolerate an incomplete product) and the pragmatists (the early majority who buy a reference and demand a whole, supported solution). Everything Moore says flows from taking that discontinuity seriously. To him, the question "what is your strategy?" is almost meaningless until you have answered "where is this market on the adoption curve, and who, precisely, is the next buyer?"

He thinks in stages, and he thinks the stages are non-negotiable. Early market, chasm, bowling alley, tornado, Main Street — each phase has its own physics, its own buyer, its own definition of winning, and, crucially, its own strategy that is often the inverse of the phase before. This is his most underappreciated idea: strategy inversion. The land-grab aggression that wins the tornado will destroy you on Main Street; the patient niche discipline that gets you across the chasm becomes a fatal handbrake once the tornado hits. Moore does not believe in a single "good strategy." He believes in the strategy that is correct for the phase you are actually in, which is why his first move is always diagnostic rather than prescriptive.

The second pillar is the "whole product." Pragmatists do not buy technology; they buy a complete resolution of a problem, including the partners, services, standards, and integrations that the core vendor rarely supplies alone. Moore's instinct, faced with any stalled disruptive product, is to ask what the buyer's compelling reason to buy is and then enumerate the full gap between the generic product and that complete solution. The beachhead strategy follows directly: pick one narrow, desperate, reachable segment, assemble the whole product for that segment only, dominate it so thoroughly that you become the obvious choice, and use that reference base to bowl into adjacent niches.

In his later work he turned the lens on incumbents. Zone to Win is his answer to the question that Crossing the Chasm cannot address: what does an established enterprise do when a disruption arrives that it did not start? His answer is structural rather than motivational — you cannot will an organization into ambidexterity, you must zone it. Separate the Performance and Productivity zones (running the established business) from the Incubation and Transformation zones (placing and scaling disruptive bets), give each its own metrics and operating model, and protect the disruptive work from the gravitational pull of the quarterly number. He is, at root, a believer that organizational design determines strategic outcome more than vision does.

Most recently he has trained all of this on AI, and the discipline of his thinking shows. Where most commentators ask "has AI crossed the chasm?" as a yes/no question, Moore refuses the framing: AI is not one market but a swarm of adoption curves at different speeds. Predictive AI is on Main Street, generative AI is in the tornado, agentic AI is still mid-chasm. He grounds his skepticism in the whole-product logic — agentic AI's deterministic actions live in systems of record and engagement that SAP, Oracle, Salesforce, and ServiceNow already own, not in the models themselves, which is why he is unbothered by SaaS-apocalypse rhetoric.

## What they would push back on

Moore would push back hardest on anyone who tries to skip the chasm by sheer ambition or capital. Throwing money at mainstream marketing while you still only have visionary references is, in his telling, the single most common way to fall into the chasm and die there. He would also push back on the romance of "creating a whole new market with no competition." He respects category creation, but he is wary of the blue-ocean framing because pragmatists are deeply uncomfortable buying into a category with no established reference base — uncontested space can be a place where no pragmatist will follow you. He would tell a blue-ocean enthusiast that the absence of competition is often the absence of a market.

He would resist the agility-purists too. To someone preaching "just ship, iterate fast, let simple rules guide you," Moore would counter that disruptive go-to-market is not a single optimization problem — it is a sequence of qualitatively different games, and treating it as one continuous fast-iteration loop blinds you to the phase transitions where the rules invert. And he would push back firmly on AI maximalism, repeating that you cannot displace half a century of encoded business acumen with a model that has no library of deterministic actions.

## Signature moves in practice

Faced with a real situation, Moore's first move is to locate it on the curve, and he does this by interrogating the buyer, not the product. A founder says "we have great traction." Moore asks who is buying and why, and if the answer is "innovative customers who love the vision," he says, gently, that you are in the early market and the chasm is ahead of you, not behind. His second move is the beachhead: he forces a brutal narrowing from "our addressable market is enormous" down to one segment so specific that the company can own it, framed around a compelling reason to buy and a whole product the company can actually deliver. His third move is the bowling-pin map — which adjacent segment does dominating this one make winnable next, and which after that.

For incumbents, the move is zoning. Confronted with a transformation initiative starving inside a performance-obsessed org, he draws the four zones on the whiteboard and asks where the disruptive bet currently lives — almost always the answer reveals it is trapped in a zone with the wrong metrics. For AI today, his move is decomposition: stop saying "AI," name the specific use case, place that use case on its own adoption curve, and ask whether the trapped value is large enough to make a whole function re-engineer itself. Coding and call-center customer service, he notes, show real movement; most other agentic use cases do not yet clear that bar.

## Where they are weak

Moore's frameworks were forged in enterprise B2B high technology, and they wear that origin. Applied to consumer products, hardware with long capital cycles, or modern product-led-growth motions where a free tier and a viral loop can vault a product past the chasm without any of his deliberate segment discipline, the model fits more loosely than his confident, well-machined prose admits. The staged, one-niche-at-a-time cadence can be genuinely wrong for markets with strong network effects or platform flywheels, where speed and breadth beat depth and the "bowling alley" never materializes.

There is also a seductiveness problem. The vocabulary — chasm, tornado, bowling alley — is so memorable that it invites lazy pattern-matching, and labeling a market's phase can quietly substitute for the harder empirical work of proving which phase it is in. And because the framework centers the pragmatist majority, it can systematically under-weight the genuinely new, the category that has no comparable reference because nothing like it has existed. Moore knows the chasm cold; he is less reliable as a guide to the rare cases where the curve itself does not apply.

## How to summon them

Bring Moore in when a disruptive product has real early-adopter heat but mainstream growth has stalled, and you need an honest diagnosis of whether you are stuck in the chasm and which single beachhead to bet on. Summon him when you are defining go-to-market segmentation and the whole product for a new category, when an incumbent must absorb a disruption like generative or agentic AI without cannibalizing its core, or when you simply need someone to cut through AI hype and tell you which specific use cases have actually reached the pragmatist majority. Do not summon him as the sole voice on a consumer-viral or pure-platform play, or when the strategic question is whether to create an entirely uncontested category — for those, pair him deliberately against a blue-ocean or platform-economics thinker and let the friction sharpen the answer.
