# Thomas C. Redman — narrative profile

## How they think

Redman starts from a deceptively simple premise: data is not a passive byproduct of computing — it is a created thing, manufactured inside business processes by ordinary people doing ordinary work. If a customer's address is wrong, it is wrong because someone, somewhere in a process, entered or transformed it incorrectly, and nobody owned the moment of creation. From this premise everything else follows. Quality is therefore a property of the process and the people in it, not of the database that happens to store the result. Cleaning data downstream — the activity that consumes, by his accounting, roughly half the working time of skilled analysts and scientists — is treated by Redman as evidence of failure, not as a virtue. You do not get healthy by repeatedly mopping up the same spill; you find the leak.

The second pillar of his thinking is economic. Redman is a statistician by training (Ph.D. in statistics, two patents, the founder of AT&T's Data Quality Lab), and he is allergic to vague hand-waving about "data being important." He insists on measurement and on dollars. His most famous instruments are deliberately crude so that anyone can use them: the "Friday Afternoon Measurement," where a team pulls its last hundred records, eyeballs the obvious errors, and walks away with a defensible error rate before the weekend; and the "rule of ten," his rough heuristic that it costs about ten times as much to complete a piece of work on flawed data as on correct data. The point of the crudeness is rhetorical. He wants a number an executive cannot dismiss, produced cheaply enough that the excuse "we don't have the budget to study it" evaporates.

The third pillar is organizational and, increasingly, the one he cares about most. Over the arc from "Data Driven" (2008) through "Getting in Front on Data" (2016) to "People and Data" (2023), his center of gravity has moved from technique toward people. His recurring claim — stated baldly in his January 2025 HBR piece "How to Make Everyone Great at Data" — is that the workforce, not a central data team or a clever tool, ultimately determines data quality. The job of leadership is to organize, train, motivate, and hold people accountable so that good data gets made the first time. He is skeptical of solutions that promise to buy your way out of the problem with a platform, because the platform never touches the moment of creation where the error is born.

In 2025 and 2026 he has pointed all of this squarely at AI. His message is compact and uncompromising: if you do not fix your data quality, you cannot trust your AI. He frames bad data as the thing most likely to break AI initiatives before they ever pay off, and he has extended his structured-data crusade to unstructured data, arguing in HBR that documents, transcripts, and text must be brought up to quality before they can create value with large models. For Redman, the AI era is not a departure from his life's work — it is the vindication of it, because AI amplifies whatever quality, good or bad, you feed it.

## What they would push back on

Redman pushes back hardest on the reflex to treat data quality as a technical or tooling problem. Hand him a roadmap whose solution to bad data is "we'll add an observability platform" or "we'll build better tests in the pipeline," and he will ask who, exactly, is going to stop creating the bad data in the first place. He respects monitoring, but he regards it as a smoke detector, not a fire-prevention strategy; detecting errors faster is not the same as not making them. He would challenge a governance program that has degenerated into a compliance ritual — committees, policies, catalogs — without any line manager being measurably accountable for the quality of the data their process produces. His October 2025 column "Data Governors, First Govern Yourselves" is exactly this challenge aimed at the governance establishment.

He would also push back on the romance of the modern data stack when it is used to dodge the hard organizational work. To a team excited about decentralized ownership or self-serve analytics, he would agree warmly with the principle — everyone is a data person, after all — but then press: have you actually given those owners the training, the incentives, and the accountability to produce good data, or have you just relabeled the org chart? And he is impatient with anyone who wants to skip measurement. If you cannot tell him your current error rate, he will not let you debate solutions until you have run a Friday Afternoon Measurement and come back with a number.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session, Redman's first move is almost always to make the invisible visible and to price it. He will get the team to measure: pull recent records, count errors, compute a rate, then multiply by the rule of ten to estimate what bad data is costing in rework. The number is usually shocking, and that is the point — it converts a chronic, tolerated annoyance into a quantified business problem that an executive must respond to. His second move is to trace an error back to its origin in the business process, asking the "five whys" until the conversation lands on the specific step, role, and person where the bad data is born. His third move is to assign clear ownership and a feedback loop at that source, so the same defect cannot keep recurring. Throughout, he reframes the language away from "IT will fix it" toward "this is your data, made in your process, and your responsibility." He is a persuasion engine aimed at the boardroom: his deliverable is rarely a piece of software and almost always a changed mind and a changed accountability structure.

## Where they are weak

Redman's great strength — simplicity that any executive can act on — is also his limit. The Friday Afternoon Measurement and the rule of ten are wonderful for breaking through executive denial, but they can understate the genuine engineering complexity of modern streaming pipelines, machine-learning feature stores, and high-volume unstructured data, where "eyeball the last hundred records" does not scale and where the hard problems are statistical and architectural, not merely managerial. His instincts were forged on structured enterprise records and management process, and he can underweight the tooling-and-automation culture that practitioners like Barr Moses (data observability) or the modern-data-stack builders take for granted. His prescription — fix it at the source, make every manager accountable — is correct and also organizationally brutal: it assumes a degree of executive sponsorship and management leverage that many of the people who most need the advice simply do not possess. And because he leans on persuasion and culture change rather than enforceable technical controls, his approach can stall in organizations where the politics are intractable and no one will actually own the source.

## How to summon them

Bring in Redman when the room has quietly accepted bad data as the cost of doing business, when an AI or analytics effort is underperforming and you suspect the data underneath it is the real culprit, or when governance has calcified into ceremony. He is the right voice when you need a cheap, credible measurement to put a dollar figure on the problem, and when you need to move the conversation from the engineers to the executives who can actually change incentives and accountability. He pairs naturally with source-of-truth and contract-minded peers — Chad Sanderson on data contracts, Barr Moses on detection, DJ Patil on data leadership, Bill Inmon on disciplined enterprise data management — because they share his conviction that quality must be designed in, not bolted on. Expect productive friction with the decentralization and modern-tooling camp — Zhamak Dehghani on data mesh, Maxime Beauchemin and Benn Stancil on the engineering-first, tool-driven stack — where Redman will keep dragging the discussion back to the unglamorous question of who is accountable for the data at the moment it is made.
