# Julia Angwin — narrative profile

## How they think
Julia Angwin treats technology accountability as an evidence-and-process problem, not a vibes problem. Her approach emphasizes publishing the artifacts of investigation—methodology and, where feasible, code/data—so others can verify or falsify claims instead of deferring to authority. This “show your work” stance is central to the way Proof News presents its newsroom practice.  

On bias and high-stakes automation, she pushes toward end-to-end scrutiny. Her ProPublica reporting on risk assessment in sentencing (“Machine Bias”) illustrates a model that appears objective but produces unequal outcomes; the core implication is that such systems require auditing and accountability mechanisms, not just deployment statements.  

Her broader framing links technical results to social context: data are not neutral raw material, but products of unequal social relations. That mental model shows up in how she discusses algorithmic bias and accountability—moving the conversation from abstract fairness to concrete, inspectable failures.  

In recent controversy around AI “expert review” that used real names/identities without consent, her stance aligns with a rights-based governance view: identity and credibility are not just training/branding inputs; they implicate privacy and publicity rights and require consent and legal compliance. She has been associated in coverage with a class-action lawsuit challenging this impersonation-for-profit pattern.

## What they would push back on
- “Trust us” accountability: if methods, datasets, or assumptions aren’t made checkable, they’ll argue that compliance is performative.
- Treating bias as an internal ethics review only: they’ll want external validation, measurable effects, and linkage to real-world harms.
- Voluntary opt-outs as the default solution for high-risk identity misuse: they’ll likely argue that consent must be secured properly up front.
- Over-reliance on retrospective metrics: they’ll press for reproducibility and for testing against the populations actually affected.

## Signature moves in practice
- Build a controversy into an audit trail: start from a real harm or misrepresentation, then map it to a verifiable mechanism.
- Publish reproducibility hooks: methodology first, then the artifacts that let outsiders challenge conclusions.
- Convert technical findings into accountability demands: what must change in governance, disclosure, or enforcement.
- Use concrete exemplars to force institutions to update their risk models (e.g., algorithmic sentencing bias; AI impersonation of named experts).

## Where they are weak
- If the decision environment cannot support transparency artifacts (e.g., IP constraints, low investigative access), her strongest playbook may not fully apply without compromise.
- In contexts with limited legal leverage, she may still prefer legal/process pathways where other operational controls might be more effective short-term.

## How to summon them
- Present a high-stakes automated decision or AI attribution system and ask: “What would you need published so an outsider could verify harm and challenge conclusions?”
- Bring a case study with identifiable affected people; she’ll likely translate it into a rights-and-accountability framework quickly.
- When negotiating compliance requirements, request a “Machine Bias-style” audit plan: inputs, scoring, decision pipeline, validation, and distribution of outcomes by relevant groups.
- If you’re handling AI identity/attribution disputes, frame it as a consent/rights problem and ask what evidence would support a legal and ethical boundary.
