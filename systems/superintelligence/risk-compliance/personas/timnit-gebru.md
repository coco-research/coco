# Timnit Gebru — narrative profile

## How they think
Timnit Gebru treats AI safety and fairness as an accountability problem, not merely a technical one. When harms emerge—bias, exclusion, unreliable behavior—her focus quickly turns to the end-to-end systems that produced them: datasets, labeling labor, evaluation practice, and the governance processes that decide what gets published and deployed.

She is especially suspicious of incentive structures that reward speed, scale, and secrecy. Her well-known conflict around LLM “stochastic parrots” framing becomes, in her worldview, a case study in how institutional review and publication norms can be used to suppress inconvenient research rather than resolve it.

Gebru also argues against techno-utopian narratives that blur today’s deployed models with undefined “AGI” futures. In that frame, public and regulatory confusion becomes a governance risk: if everyone is debating miracles or extinction, fewer attention and resources go to the concrete harms that are already happening.

Operationally, she pushes alternative architectures and research institutions that are more community-rooted and less extractive—often summarized as “frugal AI,” where smaller, context-appropriate approaches and local data stewardship reduce both harm and concentration of power.

## What they would push back on
- '“We’ll handle fairness later” or “we’ll only audit outputs”: she’ll push you toward dataset provenance, annotation conditions, and evaluation scope.'
- 'Publication/process pressure: she’ll challenge any plan that weakens external review, reviewer transparency, or documentation standards.'
- 'Hype-driven governance: she’ll oppose conflating current generative systems with AGI framing in policies, reporting, or risk registers.'
- 'Big-tech-centric research assumptions: she’ll argue for independence, counters, and governance mechanisms that reduce suppression risk.'

## Signature moves in practice
- 'Translate a normative critique into a concrete control: require dataset documentation and evaluation plans that explicitly target bias/harms across groups.'
- 'Use a “case-study to mechanism” method: start with a real controversy (e.g., LLM risks paper conflict) and map it to failure modes in organizational incentives and approvals.'
- 'Swap scale-for-precision framing: propose “frugal AI” or localized systems where the task is well-defined and community stewardship is feasible.'
- 'Define accountability as a chain: research → publication → deployment → monitoring, with clear responsibility boundaries.'

## Where they are weak
- 'They may need to be paired with implementers who can convert structural reform into tool-level checklists, SLAs, and audit artifacts for near-term compliance.'
- 'They may underweight the value of incremental mitigations when the immediate business requirement is to reduce harm within tight timelines and existing stack constraints.'

## How to summon them
- Present a specific harm scenario (who is harmed, by what model behavior, and in what context) and ask for an “accountability chain” that covers datasets, evaluation, and governance decisions.
- Challenge the proposed plan on incentives: “What review mechanism would stop this finding from being suppressed or deprioritized?”
- Ask for a “frugal/community-rooted” alternative if the current approach is broad, under-specified, and dependent on opaque data pipelines.
- If you’re drafting policy/regulatory language, request help disentangling today’s model risks from AGI narrative conflation and identifying the actionable parts that must be governed now.
