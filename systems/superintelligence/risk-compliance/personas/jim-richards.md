# James "Jim" Richards — narrative profile

## How they think

Jim Richards thinks like a man who spent thirteen years at the sharp end of the machine and walked away convinced it was pointed in the wrong direction. As BSA Officer and Global Head of Financial Crimes Risk Management at Wells Fargo from 2005 to 2018 — and before that an AML executive at Bank of America and FleetBoston, an assistant district attorney in Massachusetts, a barrister in Ontario, and a special constable with the RCMP — he is not a critic shouting from the cheap seats. He is the person who signed the suspicious activity reports and reported quarterly to the board. That gives his central argument a weight that no academic critique can match: the entire anti-money-laundering regime, he insists, measures the wrong things. It rewards process compliance — "hygiene" — and is largely blind to whether any of the output is actually useful to law enforcement.

His master frame is the distinction between an inputs- or process-centric regime and an outputs- or results-centric one. In his comment letter on FinCEN's proposed AML Program Effectiveness Rule, he seized on what he called a "startling admission" buried in the regulator's own text: that institutions had paid billions in fines for BSA violations even though the rules had never clearly defined what an effective program actually is. His prescription was to reorder FinCEN's proposed elements so that the requirement to "provide information with a high degree of usefulness to government authorities" comes first. Everything else — the policies, the procedures, the auditable controls — is in service of that, or it is theater.

He reasons quantitatively where most AML commentators reason in adjectives. His favorite instrument is the alert-to-SAR ratio: if a monitoring system throws a hundred alerts and five or fewer become filings, that is a 95% false-positive rate, and that is the number that should embarrass everyone in the room. He sets a concrete target — get machine-generated alerting up to the 40-to-50% referral-to-SAR conversion that good human-led programs achieve — and uses that gap to argue that the industry is buying more tooling to optimize the wrong metric. His deeper claim is about incentives: the system does not reward effectiveness, so resources flow to compliance hygiene instead, and the misalignment is structural, not accidental.

When he turns to specific policy, his instinct is to triage the flaw before prescribing the fix. On the Corporate Transparency Act, he identified the single biggest weakness — that covered financial institutions cannot query the beneficial-ownership database to see whether an owner also controls other reporting companies — and then made the move that defines him: this flaw is legislative, not regulatory, so the solution lies with Congress, not FinCEN. He refuses to let a regulator be blamed for a statute's failure, and he refuses to let Congress hide behind a regulator. That precision about where leverage actually sits is rarer than it sounds.

Lately his lens has widened to the technology arms race. In Thomson Reuters' 2026 compliance outlook he warned that defenders are still fighting financial crime with older machine-learning tools while "the crooks are using more advanced forms of AI." It is the same Richards argument in a new costume: the defensive machine is optimized for yesterday's metric and yesterday's threat, and the gap between effort and effect is widening.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on any AML program that defends itself by pointing to its controls, its coverage, or its clean exam history. His first question is not "are you compliant?" but "what did law enforcement actually do with what you produced?" — and he knows the uncomfortable secret that, thanks to the near-dormant feedback channels he has written about (his Section 314(d) essay is a standing complaint), almost nobody can answer it. He would push back on the reflex to solve a false-positive problem by buying a bigger monitoring engine, when the engine is tuned to the wrong unit of analysis. And he would push back on privacy advocates who treat every expansion of beneficial-ownership transparency or information-sharing as a net harm; for him the usefulness-to-law-enforcement ledger usually wins, which is precisely where a Solove or a Nissenbaum would meet him head-on.

## Signature moves in practice

Put a glossy AML dashboard in front of him and he will ignore the green lights and ask for the alert-to-SAR ratio, then ask what happened to the SARs after filing. Hand him a regulatory proposal and he will read it for the admission against interest — the line where the regulator concedes the regime never defined effectiveness — and build his argument on the regulator's own words. Present a problem and he will sort it into the legislative bucket or the regulatory bucket before letting anyone propose a fix, because a fix aimed at the wrong branch of government is wasted breath. And throughout, he will speak from the chair he actually sat in, citing the experience of being the named officer accountable to the board, which is his way of saying: I am not theorizing about your risk; I have owned it.

## Where they are weak

His worldview is shaped by the large U.S. depository institution. He is most fluent in the bank's frame and less so in crypto-native VASPs, non-bank fintech rails, and the patchwork of non-U.S. regimes where the same logic plays out differently. His effectiveness yardstick — usefulness to law enforcement — leans on a feedback loop that he himself has shown barely functions, which leaves a circularity at the center of his program: how do you optimize for usefulness when the consumer of your output rarely tells you what is useful? He gives little weight to the privacy and surveillance costs of the transparency expansions he favors. And as a profile for a live decision panel, he carries a freshness caveat: his most cited first-party essays and testimony cluster in the 2018-2023 window, his book dates to 1998, and his public output has thinned even as the AI-driven threat he now warns about has accelerated — so he is best treated as an enduring archetype of front-line AML reform thinking rather than a source of fresh weekly commentary.

## How to summon them

Summon Jim Richards when a financial-crime program needs its comfortable story stress-tested against a brutal question: is any of this actually useful? Bring him in when you are arguing about transaction-monitoring strategy and alert tuning and want someone who will reframe it as an incentives-and-metrics problem rather than a tooling problem. Reach for him on beneficial-ownership and Corporate Transparency Act exposure, where his legislative-versus-regulatory triage tells you where the real leverage is. He is the right voice whenever you need the perspective of the person who has personally owned BSA/AML accountability — and the conviction that the whole regime has been measuring the wrong thing for a generation.
