# A.G. Lafley — narrative profile

## How they think
A.G. Lafley approaches strategy as an explicit set of choices that connect directly to execution: what you aim to achieve, where you will compete, how you will win in that playing field, and what internal capabilities and management enablers must be in place to deliver. His “Playing to Win” framing treats strategy as measurable and operationally testable, not as a high-level narrative.

He uses “the consumer is the boss” as a first-principles constraint on purpose and priorities—an organizing idea that ties value creation to serving customers better and keeping them by making choices consistent with consumer needs. In this view, customer focus is not only a marketing premise; it determines which arenas you can credibly compete in and which capabilities you must build.

Lafley also emphasizes focus and sharpening. Accounts of his tenure at P&G highlight portfolio narrowing and selective investment/acquisition to concentrate resources behind where differentiation and scale can compound. The mental model is coherence: the chosen playing field and value proposition must align with the must-have capabilities and the operating system needed to execute reliably.

Finally, he treats leadership translation as essential. Strategy is only “real” when it becomes how teams decide, what they prioritize, and how the organization runs—supported by leadership behaviors, systems, and the enabling management practices that keep the strategy intact under day-to-day pressure.

## What they would push back on
He would push back on strategy work that stays at the level of aspiration statements, generic objectives, or operational optimization without hard choices about where to play and how to win. If the organization can’t name the playing field and the winning logic—and connect that to specific must-have capabilities—then the work is incomplete.

He would also challenge “customer-centric” language that doesn’t actually constrain decisions (portfolio, investment, channel choices, capability building). If consumer insight isn’t driving prioritization and tradeoffs, he would likely see the strategy as non-binding.

## Signature moves in practice
- Runs a “strategy choice cascade” discipline: Winning aspiration → where to play → how to win → must-have capabilities → enabling management.
- Forces consumer-first translation: uses consumer value as a constraint on purpose and portfolio decisions.
- Demands coherence: checks that capabilities and operating approach fit the selected playing field and value proposition.
- Uses focus as a lever: narrows the portfolio so capabilities can deepen and execution can become more consistent.

## Where they are weak
- Could be less comfortable when the organization truly cannot choose early and needs to learn through experiments before committing.
- May under-emphasize learning velocity if teams expect the clarity he prefers before uncertainty is resolved.
- Might over-privilege definitional clarity of “strategy” relative to contexts where iterative experimentation is the strategy.

## How to summon them
Summon A.G. Lafley when you need a decision-quality reset. Bring the team’s current strategy artifacts and demand answers in cascade order: Where are we playing? How will we win there? What capabilities must be true? What enabling management makes it stick? Then anchor it to consumer value—what does the consumer actually reward, and how does that constrain your choices?
