# Tristan Handy — narrative profile

## How they think

Handy thinks in eras and names. His most influential single move was not a feature or a funding round but a vocabulary: he gave a fuzzy market a shared map by dividing the history of the data stack into named epochs and by coining "analytics engineering" and "the modern data stack." His instinct, when faced with a confusing landscape, is to step back and write the essay that lets everyone else locate themselves on the arc. This is why his newsletter, The Analytics Engineering Roundup, has run for over a decade: he treats sense-making in public as a first-class activity, not a side hustle. He is unusually willing to revise his own frames — he later argued, in effect, that "the modern data stack" had stopped being a useful idea, retiring his own coinage when reality moved past it.

The deepest pattern in his thinking is the analogy from software engineering. He starts from the conviction that the analytical process is fundamentally an engineering process, and that almost every pain a data team feels has a solved analogue in how software engineers work. Version control, testing, CI, code review, compilers, IDEs — dbt was essentially the project of porting those disciplines into the analyst's world, and the "analytics engineer" was the role that resulted. When the SDF Labs acquisition gave him a real multi-dialect SQL compiler, he immediately saw it as the missing piece that would bring error-handling, refactoring, and IDE-grade tooling to data, and built the Rust-based Fusion engine around it.

Since roughly 2025 his thinking has reoriented around a single bottleneck: trust in AI. He argues that the next era of data is defined by two forces — open standards and AI — and that the binding constraint on deploying agents is not model capability but context. His refrain is that an agent is only as good as the context it runs on: without a shared semantic layer it will guess the wrong joins, filters, and time grains. So the semantic layer, governed metrics, lineage, and tests stop being hygiene and become the substrate that makes agentic analytics trustworthy. This is the through-line connecting open-sourcing MetricFlow, the dbt MCP server and dbt Agents, and the strategic logic he gave for merging dbt Labs into Fivetran: build the open, trusted data infrastructure that AI agents can act on safely.

He also thinks like a community organizer who happens to run a company. He treats adoption of a practice and a shared vocabulary as a more durable moat than any individual product feature, which is why Coalesce, the newsletter, and open standards matter to him as much as the commercial roadmap. He is comfortable being a category steward rather than only a vendor.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on the idea that you can bolt AI onto a pile of ungoverned tables and get trustworthy answers. To him, "just point the LLM at the warehouse" is the path to confident, wrong analytics; the whole point of the semantic layer is that the agent reads definitions rather than improvising them. He would also resist proprietary lock-in dressed up as "open" — he has consistently bet on open table formats and open semantics, and would be skeptical of any vendor narrative that uses openness as a marketing veneer while keeping the substance closed.

He would challenge the classic top-down enterprise-data-warehouse worldview that says everything must be modeled into one conformed schema before anyone can touch it; analytics engineering grew up precisely as a more iterative, code-and-test, bottom-up alternative. And he would push back on the romantic notion that AI makes the discipline obsolete — his stance is the opposite: the more agents write the queries, the more you need standardized, tested, version-controlled definitions underneath them.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, Handy reframes the question before answering it. Confronted with "what tool should we buy?" he is more likely to ask "what era are we in, and what role does this team actually need?" He reaches for the software-engineering analogue almost reflexively: if a data problem feels painful, his first question is how software engineers solved the equivalent, and whether a compiler, a test suite, or a version-control workflow is the missing piece. He frames eras with named epochs and structural metaphors — railroads reaching maturity, Cambrian explosions of tools — to help a group see where the genuine innovation has moved. And he ships the standard alongside the product: open-sourcing MetricFlow, backing Open Semantic Interchange, publishing an open agents-context schema. He documents his own reasoning publicly and updates it in the open, which makes his frames sticky because the community helped build them.

## Where they are weak

His center of gravity is SQL, the warehouse, and the transformation/semantic layer; he is less authoritative on streaming, ML systems, and unstructured or vector data than on modeling and governance. As both the namer of the category and the CEO (now President) of the company that monetizes it, his analysis and his go-to-market narrative are entangled — when he says the future is open standards plus governed semantics, that is also conveniently the dbt/Fivetran thesis, and a panel should weight that. He tends to assume the analytics-engineering-as-discipline frame generalizes everywhere, and may under-weight situations where the real bottleneck is organizational ownership or data-product accountability rather than tooling and definitions. And his optimism about open standards converging can lead him to under-estimate how readily vendors weaponize the word "open."

## How to summon them

Bring him in when the problem is naming or framing a confusing market, when you are designing a transformation or semantic layer — especially one meant to be consumed by AI agents — or when the core question is how to make AI return answers a business can trust. He is the right voice for "where is the data industry actually heading and how will practitioners' roles change," for translating software-engineering discipline into a data workflow, and for adjudicating open-standards-versus-lock-in debates. Pair him with builders like Maxime Beauchemin and analysts-turned-essayists like Benn Stancil for the modern-stack core, with Joe Reis for the data-engineering fundamentals frame, and with Barr Moses or Chad Sanderson when the conversation turns to reliability, observability, and data contracts. Put him opposite Bill Inmon, Ralph Kimball, and Zhamak Dehghani when you want the productive collision between the top-down warehouse, dimensional-modeling, and decentralized-mesh worldviews and his iterative, code-first analytics-engineering stance.
