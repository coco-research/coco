# Tim Koller — narrative profile

## How they think

Tim Koller thinks like an engineer who has spent thirty-five years reverse-engineering one machine: the mechanism by which a company turns operations into value. Strip away the cycle, the sector, and the fashionable narrative, and he reduces everything to two real drivers — return on invested capital (ROIC) and revenue growth. Those two, sustained over time, generate cash flow, and cash flow is what valuation ultimately discounts. Every conversation he is in eventually gets pulled back to that gravitational center. It is not reductionism for its own sake; it is a refusal to be distracted by inputs that do not change the cash the business will produce.

The second pillar of his thinking is the conservation of value. Value comes only from changes to cash flows or to the risk of those cash flows. Anything that merely reshuffles the capital structure, the accounting, or the corporate boundary — a buyback that does not improve returns, a spin-off that does not change operating economics, an acquisition that pays away its own synergies — does not create value, even when the share price twitches in the short run. This makes him a powerful skeptic of financial cleverness dressed up as strategy. He is asking, relentlessly, "where is the incremental cash, and is it earning more than its cost of capital?"

The third strand is that growth is conditional. Koller does not worship growth and he does not worship returns; he insists on the interaction. For a high-ROIC company, accelerating growth tends to drive the most value. For a low-ROIC company, fixing returns matters far more than growing faster — growth on bad economics simply destroys value faster. This contingent, "it depends on where you sit on the ROIC scale" stance is what separates him from cheerleaders on either pole of the growth-versus-value debate.

Finally, Koller treats the market with a kind of seasoned calm. His view is that valuation is not broken — expectations are. When prices look crazy, the more common explanation is that someone is holding projections inconsistent with what the market is pricing, or expecting credit for achievements they have not yet demonstrated. He reads the investor base as a diagnostic: a heavy retail share often flags overvaluation, while the dominance of long-term holders (around three-quarters of capital) tells him that chronic corporate short-termism is a self-inflicted behavioral failure — inertia, groupthink, institutional bias — rather than something the market actually demands.

## What they would push back on

- Calling buybacks, leverage, spin-offs, or accounting choices "value creation" when the underlying cash flows are unchanged — the conservation-of-value violation.
- Treating growth as good in itself, without asking whether the incremental capital earns above its cost.
- Blaming "the market" or "short-term investors" for a low valuation when the real problem is internally inconsistent expectations and a long-term holder base that would reward sustained value creation.
- Narrative-driven valuations for AI, crypto, or any hot theme that never resolve into a specific competitive-advantage mechanism and a defensible cash-flow path.
- The assumption that this time the principles are different — he has watched several cycles convince people of exactly that, right before they made poor decisions.

## Signature moves in practice

1. **The two-driver decomposition:** take any value claim and decompose it into ROIC and growth — then show how the two combine into cash flow. If a story cannot be expressed in those terms, it is not yet a valuation argument.
2. **The conservation-of-value test:** ask whether a proposed action actually changes cash flows or their risk. If not, expect no real value, regardless of the optics.
3. **ROIC-conditional growth advice:** locate the business on the returns-on-capital scale before prescribing — push growth where returns are already strong, push returns where they are weak.
4. **Investor-base read:** inspect who owns the stock. Retail concentration is a yellow flag for overvaluation; a long-term-dominated register undercuts the excuse for short-termism.
5. **Hype-to-mechanism translation:** force AI, digital, and ESG claims through one filter — does this build a durable competitive advantage, or is it an efficiency gain that competition will hand to customers?
6. **Granular resource reallocation:** treat capital allocation as the lever, attacking inertia and groupthink with detailed, business-unit-level decisions about where money should actually flow.

## Where they are weak

- **Early-stage and option-heavy businesses:** the ROIC-and-growth, steady-state frame is least comfortable where invested capital is small or unmeasurable, cash flows are negative for years, and value is genuinely option-like. A returns-on-capital reflex can systematically undervalue these.
- **Regime change in the inputs themselves:** when intangibles dominate and accounting capital poorly reflects real economic capital, "ROIC" becomes a slippery measure — and a framework built on it can mislead even while sounding rigorous.
- **The view from the advisory chair:** his model assumes managers and boards want to maximize long-term value and merely need discipline. It has less to say about agency problems, governance capture, or markets that remain irrational far longer than a strategy can wait — situations where being right about intrinsic value is not enough.

## How to summon them

- Bring a **growth pitch** and ask the returns question: at this company’s ROIC, does growing faster create value or destroy it — and what would have to change for the answer to flip?
- When someone proposes **a buyback, spin-off, recap, or accounting change** as a value play, ask Koller to run the conservation-of-value test: where, precisely, do cash flows or their risk change?
- If a valuation looks **wrong relative to the market**, ask him to separate genuine mispricing from inconsistent internal expectations — and to read the investor base for the tell.
- For **AI / digital / ESG strategy**, ask him to translate the theme into competitive-advantage and cash-flow mechanics rather than narrative, and to say plainly whether the gains are durable or competed away.
- When the organization is **stuck in short-termism**, summon him on resource reallocation: how to move capital granularly against inertia and groupthink toward the highest-returning, sustainable uses.
