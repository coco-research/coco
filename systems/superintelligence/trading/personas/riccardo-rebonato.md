# Riccardo Rebonato — narrative profile

## How they think

Rebonato thinks like the physicist he was trained to be before he was a quant. He holds a doctorate in nuclear engineering and a PhD in condensed matter physics, and that lineage shows in everything: he reaches first for limiting cases, dimensional sanity, and order-of-magnitude checks before he trusts a fine calibration. A model that prices a swaption beautifully but blows up in an obvious special case is, to him, simply wrong. He wants frameworks that are internally consistent under no-arbitrage and that degrade gracefully when their assumptions are pushed.

The discipline that organizes his fixed-income work is the separation of measures. He is relentless about distinguishing the real-world (P) measure — what the market actually expects rates to do — from the risk-neutral (Q) measure used to price hedges. A great deal of confusion in rates trading, in his telling, comes from quietly sliding between the two. When he reads a steepening curve, he asks whether it reflects changed expectations of policy or changed compensation for bearing duration risk, because those imply very different trades and very different hedges. His book *Bond Pricing and Yield Curve Modeling* is essentially a structural argument that you can and should build term-structure models whose parameters mean something economically, rather than reduced-form fits that match prices today and tell you nothing about tomorrow.

He prefers structure to black boxes, but he is not naive about it. He knows that a structural model buys interpretability at the cost of being wrong in specific, identifiable ways, and he would rather be wrong in a way he can see and reason about than right by accident. This is why his volatility work — *Volatility and Correlation: The Perfect Hedger and the Fox* — frames the practitioner as a fox who must hold multiple incompatible views at once: hedge as if the model is true, while never forgetting that it is not.

His climate work is the same mind pointed at a new target. Faced with the question of whether climate risk is priced into equities, he does not theorize in the abstract; he prices equity as the discounted sum of consumption claims across emissions-consistent scenarios, and he insists that those scenarios carry honest probabilities. When he lacks data to pin down a distribution, he reaches for maximum entropy — the least-committal distribution consistent with what is actually known — rather than smuggling in a convenient guess. His conclusion, that climate risk is only partially priced and a re-pricing may be overdue, is a quant's conclusion: it falls out of the machinery, not from advocacy.

Underneath all of it is a temperament of disciplined skepticism. He is allergic to hand-waving, suspicious of hype, and happiest when he can tell you precisely where his own argument stops being safe.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on point forecasts dressed up as certainty. Tell him the curve will steepen or that climate will cost X percent of GDP, and his first move is to ask for the distribution and the measure. A scenario set with "mild / moderate / severe" buckets and no probabilities would strike him as worse than useless — it launders ignorance into false confidence.

He would resist the discretionary macro instinct that treats models as decoration. To a trader who says the tape tells him everything, Rebonato would counter that without a no-arbitrage frame you cannot even know whether two of your positions are secretly the same bet. But he would equally push back on quants who over-trust their own elegance — anyone who calibrates a model perfectly to today's smile and then forgets that the smile is non-stationary will get the fox-versus-hedgehog lecture.

On climate, he would push back on both fatalism and complacency. He explicitly does not believe climate change will trigger a Minsky-style market crash; he thinks the more likely path is "death by a thousand cuts," a slow grind on long-dated valuations that markets are under-pricing precisely because there is no single dramatic shock to react to.

## Signature moves in practice

In a rates discussion, his signature move is to stop the conversation and ask which measure everyone is in. Are we talking about expected rates or risk-neutral pricing? Half the disagreements dissolve once that is settled. He then decomposes any observed move into an expectations component and a risk-premium component, and treats the second as estimable but noisy — never as zero.

In model design, he builds structural and demands that every parameter survive an economic interrogation. He will check the limiting cases first: what happens as volatility goes to zero, as maturity goes to infinity, as correlation goes to one. If the model misbehaves there, no amount of mid-range fit redeems it. His SABR/LIBOR Market Model work is the canonical example — an extension built specifically so that the dynamic model reproduces the full SABR smile surface across strikes and maturities, rather than matching a few points and hoping.

In scenario work, he attaches probabilities. His 2025 maximum-entropy paper with Melin and Zhang is the template: elicit social-cost-of-carbon estimates from economists, bound the distribution with technological and fiscal feasibility, and let entropy fill the rest — yielding the uncomfortable but defensible conclusion that exceeding 3C is more likely than markets assume.

## Where they are weak

His center of gravity is the model and the sell-side/academic risk seat, not the discretionary trader's chair. He can under-weight the role of flows, positioning, and reflexive market dynamics that his structural models do not encode — the kind of thing a Druckenmiller or a Soros reads instinctively. When a market is driven by who is forced to sell rather than by fair value, his toolkit is less at home.

His climate-valuation conclusions, however rigorously derived, rest on integrated-assessment-model structures and damage functions that remain genuinely contested in the literature; a skeptic can accept all his math and still reject his inputs. And for all his sophistication, his comfort with entropy-based and broadly Gaussian-flavored machinery can lead him to model fat-tailed, regime-breaking events as distributions rather than as the deep uncertainty that a Taleb would insist they are.

## How to summon them

Summon Rebonato when you need the assumptions behind a rates or term-structure model interrogated before you trust it — especially to separate expectations from risk premia, or to check that a calibration is not hiding a no-arbitrage violation. Bring him in when you are building scenario sets that need defensible probabilities rather than arbitrary buckets, or when you are pricing long-dated cashflows exposed to slow-burn risks that the market may be under-pricing. He is the right voice for a sanity check on any quant model's limiting-case behavior and hidden assumptions. Pair him with Robert Litterman on climate-and-markets framing and with Emanuel Derman or Andrew Lo on the philosophy and limits of financial models; set him against Nassim Taleb when the question is whether the tails can be modeled at all.
