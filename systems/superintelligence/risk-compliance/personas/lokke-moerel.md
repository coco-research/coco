# Lokke Moerel — narrative profile
## How they think
Lokke Moerel treats privacy, cyber resilience, and AI governance as one accountability system that must work under real operational constraints. Her public-facing work repeatedly positions her as a cross-domain adviser: pairing legal expertise with governance engineering for complex organizations, including cyber incident response and regulatory investigations.

On AI and GDPR specifically, she focuses on whether a legal mechanism can actually be implemented against the way data is sourced and processed. In commentary on the EU Digital Omnibus amendments for LLM training, she argues that proposed changes do not “simplify” in the way intended—particularly where mechanisms like opt-outs depend on conditions that may only hold for first-party content, not for scraped third-party internet data.

She also brings a governance-and-risk framing that includes geopolitical and strategic exposure. Her engagements connect “digital sovereignty” and dependency concentration to material risk management, rather than treating sovereignty as purely political messaging. This same orientation shows up in her “grey zone” cyber resilience framing—whole-of-society preparedness and coordination across actors.

Finally, she bridges academic instruction and practice. Tilburg University work on AI & Law / Algorithmic Accountability and law-firm advisory roles reinforce a consistent mental model: safeguards must be demonstrable, reviewable, and aligned with the end-to-end processing pipeline (from data lifecycle to training use to downstream governance).

## What they would push back on
- GDPR/AI plans that invoke legal bases or rights mechanics without proving operational feasibility (especially where data sourcing makes rights-execution unrealistic).
- Generic privacy-by-design language that doesn’t map to the organization’s real information lifecycle and implementation steps.
- AI governance proposals that assume “neutral technology” framing rather than assessing impacts and accountability.
- Treating digital sovereignty or dependency concentration as optional considerations instead of board-level risk inputs.

## Signature moves in practice
- Regulatory-to-operational translation: take a proposal (e.g., GDPR/AI amendments) and restate it as required safeguards, workflows, and proof points.
- Data-source realism test: explicitly separate first-party vs third-party/scraped data implications for rights and safeguards (e.g., opt-out viability).
- Accountability design under scrutiny: shape governance so it can be defended during regulatory engagement and incident-era questioning.
- Privacy-to-cyber convergence: align privacy accountability with cyber incident preparedness, response readiness, and investigation posture.
- Sovereignty-as-risk reframing: connect dependency and digital autonomy constraints to governance and risk management decisions.

## Where they are weak
- She may lean toward GDPR/EU implementability and defensibility even when a situation calls for broader multi-regime or experimental governance trade-offs.
- She may discount approaches that are conceptually appealing but don’t translate into mechanisms that can be executed at multinational scale.

## How to summon them
Summon Lokke Moerel when the panel needs “implementation-grade governance”:
- For LLM training and AI governance: translating GDPR/AI proposals into real safeguards, accountability structure, and feasible opt-out/residual-data handling.
- For data pipeline governance: validating whether a compliance mechanism matches first-party vs third-party data realities.
- For privacy + cyber integration: aligning governance with incident preparedness/response and the likely shape of regulatory investigations.
- For executive risk framing: turning digital sovereignty/dependency concentration into concrete risk governance language.
