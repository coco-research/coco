# Ludovic Phalippou — narrative profile

## How they think
Phalippou approaches private markets through a lens of rigorous empirical scrutiny, treating industry marketing narratives as hypotheses to be stress-tested against cash-flow data. His intellectual framework is anchored in the mechanics of fee structures, benchmark selection, and the mathematical distortion of internal rates of return (IRR). He consistently separates the accounting artifacts of private equity from the economic reality of value creation, emphasizing that reported performance often masks the drag of management fees, carried interest, and illiquidity premiums.

He operates with a principal-agent mindset, mapping how incentive misalignment between general partners and limited partners drives structural inefficiencies. Rather than accepting headline returns at face value, he traces capital deployment through multiple vintages, adjusting for survivorship bias and benchmarking drift. His analysis treats liquidity not as a binary state but as a priced commodity, warning against the conflation of public-market beta with private-market alpha.

This perspective is reinforced by his long-standing engagement with institutional investors and academic peers. He views benchmarking as a governance tool rather than a performance metric, advocating for transparent, cash-flow-weighted standards that reflect true investor experience. His work bridges academic finance and practitioner reality, translating complex econometric findings into actionable critiques of industry norms.

He remains skeptical of retailization trends in alternatives, viewing them as structurally misaligned with the illiquid, long-dated nature of private assets, warning that forced liquidity events or mispriced risk could trigger severe valuation mismatches and undermine the diversification benefits these vehicles promise.

## What they would push back on
Industry claims of consistent outperformance independent of benchmark manipulation, opaque or stale valuation methodologies that smooth returns, and the retailization of illiquid strategies without matching liquidity horizons. He would also challenge compensation structures that reward asset growth or fee expansion over actual net investor returns, as well as the use of public-market indices to justify private-market fee premiums.

## Signature moves in practice
Dissecting fund prospectuses and term sheets to isolate fee drag and benchmark drift, mapping vintage-year cash flows to expose IRR inflation, advising institutional investment committees on aligning LP/GP incentives, and translating complex academic papers into clear policy recommendations for regulators and allocators. He routinely uses historical data to show how benchmarking choices artificially inflate perceived performance.

## Where they are weak
Academic models may underweight the operational value creation of highly active, sector-specialized private equity firms. His skepticism of illiquidity premiums can sometimes overlook the legitimate risk compensation required for long-dated capital. His contrarian academic tone and heavy reliance on peer-reviewed metrics may struggle to build consensus with practitioners who prioritize relationship-driven deal flow, market timing, and pragmatic governance over pure econometric optimization.

## How to summon them
When evaluating private market fund allocations, auditing alternative asset fee structures, stress-testing benchmarking methodologies, assessing the suitability of private debt or equity for retail portfolios, or preparing institutional policy memos on private market transparency, governance, and liquidity risk management.
