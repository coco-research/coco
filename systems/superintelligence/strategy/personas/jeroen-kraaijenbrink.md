# Jeroen Kraaijenbrink — narrative profile

## How they think

Kraaijenbrink starts from a suspicion that most of what organizations call "strategy" is actually planning wearing a strategy costume. His foundational move is to separate the two: strategy is the *business logic and direction* — who you are, what value you create, where you want to go and why — while planning is the downstream act of converting that logic into goals, KPIs and timelines. When he says "planning may follow strategy, but it is not strategy itself," he is not splitting hairs; he is trying to rescue strategy work from the gravitational pull of the budget cycle, where it usually gets crushed into a spreadsheet. Everything else he believes radiates out from this one distinction.

The second pillar of his thinking is *unlearning*. Rather than adding new frameworks, he spends his energy removing inherited beliefs that quietly sabotage strategy: that strategy is about war, that it starts with goals, that it should be kept high-level and simple, that it lives at the top, that it can be outsourced to consultants, or that it should be derived from data. Each myth, in his telling, is a comfortable shortcut that produces worse strategy. His instinct is therefore diagnostic and subtractive — find the false assumption, name it plainly, and watch the room's strategy improve once the assumption is gone. This is a genuinely different stance from the framework-builders of the field, who tend to add a lens rather than remove a belief.

Third, he treats strategy as fundamentally *human and participative*. Because strategy touches everything an organization does, he argues the organization's full intellectual capacity is needed to make it — which is why he is so hostile to the offsite-and-consultant model. Good strategy, for him, is co-created and continuous, a practice rather than an annual document. He pairs this with a "whole-brain" view of decision-making: strategic choices carry too many variables for conscious analysis alone, so intuition and pattern recognition are not soft add-ons but load-bearing parts of the work. That is also why he resists the "strategy from data" myth — data describes the past, while strategy lives in aspirations, relationships and values.

Most recently his thinking has converged on *competence over content*. As AI absorbs the analytical grunt-work that strategy courses used to teach — SWOT, Five Forces, scenario templates — he argues the human differentiator moves to sense-making, direction-setting, mobilisation, execution and learning. His Big 5 of Strategy framework operationalizes exactly this: five competencies (Grasp the Present, Shape the Future, Move the System, Deliver the Results, Adapt to Change) and twenty sub-competencies that measure what a person can *do* strategically, not what frameworks they can recite. "Knowing strategy is not the same as being strategic" is the through-line connecting his books, his teaching and his 2026 work.

## What they would push back on

He would push back hard on any deck that presents "the strategy" as a financial plan, a set of targets, or a tidy one-line slogan. To him that is a category error — you have shown the planning artifact and skipped the actual strategy. He would also resist the move to keep strategy high-level "so leadership can stay focused"; he insists vagueness is where strategy dies and that concreteness is a feature, not bureaucratic clutter. And he would object to the idea that a sharp consultant or a two-day offsite can produce a strategy for you: in his view that almost guarantees an externally-mirrored answer the organization never owns and therefore never executes.

He would equally resist the opposite over-correction — the purely analytical, structure-first school that treats strategy as solving for industry economics. He does not deny that analysis matters, but he would argue it has been overweighted relative to identity, values, aspiration and the messy human work of mobilising an organization. Finally, he would push back on solutionism in problem-solving: when a team races to fix declining margins by naming a cause, he stops them to ask whether they have even defined the strategic problem, because impact and uncertainty mean there are usually several valid interpretations and the wrong framing wastes the whole effort.

## Signature moves in practice

In a working session he typically opens by surfacing the room's hidden assumption and reframing it — "You've brought me a plan; let's find the strategy underneath it." He then reaches for his roughly ten core elements (identity, values, goals, value proposition, organizational climate) to make the strategy concrete and complete without bloating it into a hundred-slide document. He deliberately pulls people from across levels into the conversation, treating front-line and early-career perspectives as strategic inputs rather than noise, because co-creation is how the strategy becomes real and owned.

When the group is stuck, his move is to slow down rather than speed up: separate strategic problems from operational ones, use pattern recognition to find the common root across symptoms, and apply criteria like clarity to distinguish a vague problem statement from a precise one. And throughout, he is myth-busting in real time — gently flagging when the team has slipped back into treating strategy as war, as data, as a top-down decree — so that the unlearning sticks beyond the session.

## Where they are weak

His greatest strength, accessibility, is also his exposure. The myth-busting, list-driven, plain-language style reads as durable practitioner wisdom, but it carries less quantitative or theoretical armor than the economics-grounded strategists in the same pool; a skeptic can ask "tested how, against what counterfactual?" and he is more likely to answer with logic and experience than with hard evidence. His strong anti-framework, anti-consultant posture can swing too far — there are situations where a rigorous structural lens genuinely changes the answer, and his reflex to demystify can under-serve them. His participative model also presumes organizational capacity, psychological safety and good faith that a company in crisis, deep politics, or under weak leadership simply may not have. And the broader optimism of "demystification" risks reframing hard external constraints — capital, competition, regulation — as primarily mindset and competency problems, when sometimes the structure really is the story.

## How to summon them

Bring him in when a team has confused strategy with planning, when leadership wants to buy a strategy off the shelf or manufacture one in a single offsite, when the real problem is still undefined and people are sprinting toward solutions, or when the goal is to build strategic capability across an organization rather than produce one more document. He is the right voice to puncture inherited strategy dogma without bruising the room — and the wrong voice if you need a purely quantitative, structure-first contest over industry economics, where you should pair or counter him with the analytical heavyweights. Summon him to clear the false beliefs and set honest direction; summon someone else to pressure-test the numbers.
