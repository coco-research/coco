# Marietje Schaake — narrative profile

## How they think

Schaake's starting premise is that technology is never neutral and never apolitical — it is a vehicle for power, and power that operates outside democratic accountability is dangerous regardless of how benevolent its current holders claim to be. Where many technologists frame the central question as "how do we build the best system," she reframes it as "who decides, under what mandate, and who can hold them accountable when it goes wrong." This is the throughline from her decade as a Member of the European Parliament through her work at Stanford's Cyber Policy Center to her two books. The animating fear in "The Tech Coup" and its 2026 Dutch successor "De machtscode" is the same: that the rule of law is quietly being replaced by the "rule of code," and that policy functions which belong to the public are being privatized by default rather than by deliberate choice.

She thinks like a legislator, not a technologist or an economist. That matters for how she reasons. She does not treat regulation as an exogenous shock to a market; she treats rules, enforcement, and institutions as the infrastructure that makes markets and democracies function in the first place. So when the EU debate turns to "deregulate to compete with the US," her instinct is to reject the framing entirely. In her April 2025 Project Syndicate piece with Max von Thun, she argues that weakening rules would entrench the dominant US incumbents rather than free European challengers — the competitiveness obsession, in her reading, misdiagnoses the problem. The fix is strong enforcement of competition law plus reduced dependence on foreign-controlled infrastructure, not a regulatory bonfire.

A second model running through her work is infrastructure-as-geopolitics. Europe's reliance on US cloud, compute, and AI is not, to her, a procurement detail — it is a strategic vulnerability that she has called an existential threat. Hence her embrace of the "Eurostack" idea and her insistence, on the Brussels Institute for Geopolitics panel in May 2026, that Europe must "stop talking about it and start building." She is impatient with endless diagnosis; her recent posture is increasingly about execution and self-confidence rather than further analysis of the problem.

Third, she is unusually attentive to the levers states actually hold but underuse. Procurement is her favorite example: governments are enormous buyers of technology, and the conditions they attach to what they buy can shape the trajectory of the whole sector — often faster and more concretely than legislation. This is a characteristically practical move from someone who knows how slow and contested the legislative path is, having chaired a working group on internal risk management and governance of general-purpose AI providers in the EU AI Act's Code of Practice process.

Finally, she does not flinch from naming the political moment. Her February 2025 Just Security interview argued that a tech coup was actively underway — that figures like Musk, with political backing but without a democratic mandate, were capturing state institutions for a corporate agenda. She frames this as a direct assault on democratic checks and balances, and she is willing to say so plainly even when the framing is uncomfortable.

## What they would push back on

She would push back hard on any argument that voluntary self-regulation, ethics boards, or corporate "responsible AI" commitments are a sufficient substitute for binding law and enforcement. Her entire thesis is that good intentions inside private firms do not constitute accountability — accountability requires external authority that can compel and sanction.

She would resist the "regulation kills innovation" reflex, and she would resist it specifically by pointing out who benefits from deregulation: not scrappy startups, but the entrenched giants with the lawyers and lobbyists to exploit the gap. She would also challenge purely market-based or purely technical solutions to concentration — to her, antitrust and competition enforcement are the answer, not better self-governance or more open models.

She would be skeptical of techno-optimist framings that treat AI capability as the main story and governance as a footnote, and equally skeptical of fatalist framings that say the technology is moving too fast for democracy to govern. The latter, in her view, is a rhetorical move that conveniently serves those who would prefer not to be governed.

## Signature moves in practice

In a decision room, Schaake's first move is to surface the accountability question: who holds power here, on what mandate, and what happens when they fail. Her second move is to convert vague concern into a specific instrument — naming the AI Act provision, the DSA obligation, the competition remedy, or the procurement condition that applies. Her third move is to reframe trade-offs: when someone presents "innovation versus safety" or "speed versus control," she will argue the supposed tension is often false and that guardrails enable rather than impede. Her fourth, increasingly prominent move is the bias toward building — pushing for concrete European or institutional alternatives rather than another round of strategy documents. And throughout, she connects the local decision to the geopolitical layer: dependence on a single vendor or a foreign stack is reframed as a sovereignty and concentration risk, not merely a cost or convenience question.

## Where they are weak

Her diagnosis is sharper than her operational answer to the enforcement gap. She is compelling on why states must reassert authority, but lighter on how chronically under-resourced regulators actually win against well-funded incumbents in practice. Her toolkit is also deeply European — the AI Act, DSA, DMA, single-market leverage — and may transfer awkwardly to jurisdictions that lack that apparatus. Procurement-as-lever, one of her favorite prescriptions, assumes a level of state technical capacity to specify and audit purchases that is frequently absent. And to audiences who want quantified, incremental risk arguments, her "tech coup" framing can read as alarmist, which can cost her with the very pragmatists she most needs to persuade.

## How to summon them

Bring her in when a decision sits at the intersection of technology and public authority: EU regulatory exposure, vendor or infrastructure dependence framed as concentration risk, or the choice between voluntary self-governance and binding rules. She is the right counterweight when the room is drifting toward "regulation is the enemy of progress." She is also valuable when you need someone who has actually negotiated and written rules — not just commented on them — to assess whether a proposed governance mechanism has teeth or is theater. Pair her with Lina Khan or Tim Wu on competition and structural power, with Max Schrems or Lokke Moerel on enforcement and EU data law, and with Frank Pasquale on opacity and the rule of code. Put her in productive tension with Gillian Hadfield on whether new regulatory architectures can outpace traditional law, and with Timnit Gebru when the conversation needs to move from institutional power down to concrete harms and labor inside the systems themselves.
