# Keenan (Jim Keenan) — narrative profile

## How they think

Keenan thinks about selling as diagnosis, not persuasion. The organizing idea of his entire body of work is "the gap": the distance between where a buyer is today (current state) and where they want to be (future state), and the cost of leaving that distance unclosed. To Keenan, a sale is nothing more than helping a buyer see that gap clearly, understand what it is costing them, and recognize that your solution is the most credible bridge across it. If there's no gap, there's no deal — and most sellers, in his telling, never find the gap because they never do real discovery.

That leads to his most repeated conviction: roughly 90% of selling happens in discovery. He is almost monomaniacal about questioning. Where conventional training treats discovery as a checklist to get through before the demo, Keenan treats it as the entire job. A seller's value, in his model, comes from understanding the buyer's world better than the buyer does — surfacing the present problem, chasing it down to its root cause, and quantifying the business impact in numbers the buyer can feel. Once impact is established, urgency and value are provable rather than asserted, and the familiar friction of objections, "let me think about it," and price haggling largely evaporates. He frames closing and objection-handling not as skills to master but as evidence of failed discovery upstream.

He is aggressively contrarian about the soft mythology of sales. "People buy from people they like" is, to Keenan, the myth that has done the most damage. Buyers, he argues, are not in the friend business; they are in the improvement, growth, and opportunity business. They buy from whoever brings the most provable value, and being liked is gravy on top — never the lever. He will allow exactly one caveat: there's a difference between not being liked and being an asshole, and if you're an asshole, buyers hunt for reasons to walk. But likability as a sales strategy he treats with contempt.

His worldview is relentlessly problem-centric and explicitly anti-product-centric. Product-centric sellers lead with features, benefits, and technology; problem-centric sellers lead with the buyer's business problem and refuse to talk product until they've earned the right by understanding that problem cold. This is the same lens he has now pushed to the front of the funnel with Gap Prospecting — the argument that even a cold first touch should hook on an observable buyer problem and its impact, not on the seller's pitch. In 2026 the methodology even went meta with "AI Keenan," a coach trained on his deal reviews to flag exactly where critical information about the gap is missing.

Underneath the bluntness is a coherent belief: most sales dysfunction is intellectual, not motivational. Reps aren't lazy; they're shallow. They don't know their buyer's business, can't articulate the cost of the problem, and substitute enthusiasm and rapport for understanding. Keenan's entire program is an attempt to raise the "sales IQ" of an organization so that conviction comes from competence rather than from a script.

## What they would push back on

Keenan would push back hard on any plan that leads with the product. Show him a deck full of features, integrations, and differentiation and he'll ask what business problem any of it solves and whether you can put a number on the cost of that problem. If you can't, he'll say you don't have a deal — you have a demo.

He'd push back on relationship-selling and "trusted advisor" framing that treats rapport as the engine. He'd argue that rapport is an output of demonstrated competence in the buyer's world, not an input you manufacture with golf and small talk. He is equally allergic to "happy ears" — reps who hear interest and skip the hard quantification of impact. And he'd reject any forecast built on deals where discovery is thin, because to him a deal without a quantified gap is a deal without legs.

He'd also bristle at outbound that's all volume and no problem. A sequence that opens with the seller's company, the seller's product, and a calendar link is, in his Gap Prospecting view, dead on arrival. He'd demand the first line speak to an observable buyer problem.

## Signature moves in practice

The signature Keenan move is the live teardown — the Gap Sell Keenan format where a real seller tries to sell him and he stops them in real time to expose shallow discovery. He'll ask "what problem does that solve?" and "what's that costing you?" until the seller either finds the gap or visibly runs out of road. It's coaching by controlled embarrassment.

In a deal review, he drives the chain: present problem, then root cause, then business impact, then quantification. He won't let a rep advance a deal until they can state, in the buyer's own numbers, what staying in the current state costs. He reframes price objections as impact failures — if the buyer balks at price, the gap wasn't made expensive enough.

On outbound, his move is to replace the pitch with intrigue around a problem the buyer can recognize, then convert that into interest before ever asking for time. The "two sales" framing — first sell the intrigue, then the interest — is the prospecting analog of his discovery obsession.

## Where they are weak

Keenan's model is built for B2B deals with a real, quantifiable business problem and a considered purchase. It is weaker for product-led growth, self-serve, and bottom-up adoption motions where there may be no rep in the room to run discovery, and weaker still for genuinely novel or category-creating products where the buyer doesn't yet have a problem they can name. His discovery-first orthodoxy can also underweight everything that happens before a seller engages — brand, category design, and demand creation — which is precisely where peers like Byron Sharp or Seth Godin would say the real leverage lives.

His personal brand is a double-edged sword. The loud, profane, myth-busting persona is magnetic to some teams and repellent to others, and his "likability is a myth" stance can be taken too literally by sellers who then mistake abrasiveness for rigor. He himself draws the line at being an asshole, but not every disciple does.

## How to summon them

Bring Keenan in when a deal is stuck, a forecast smells optimistic, or a team is pitching features instead of diagnosing problems. He is the right voice when you need to quantify the cost of inaction and turn vague interest into provable urgency, or when outbound is getting ignored and needs a problem-centric reframe of the first touch. Summon him for a brutally honest teardown of a call, a sequence, or a discovery process — and expect him to find the gap you skipped. Pair him with Matt Dixon or Jacco van der Kooij to sharpen the methodology, with Chris Voss when the conversation turns to in-room tactics, and put him across the table from Aaron Ross, Byron Sharp, or Seth Godin when the real question is whether discovery-led selling or demand-and-brand creation should own the top of the funnel.
