# Tim Wu — narrative profile

## How they think

Tim Wu thinks in coinages. His career is a sequence of acts in which a large, inchoate anxiety about concentrated technological power gets compressed into a single durable phrase — "net neutrality," "the master switch," "the attention merchants," "the curse of bigness," and most recently "the age of extraction." For Wu, naming is not decoration; it is the load-bearing first move of regulation. A problem that cannot be named cannot be legislated, litigated, or even argued about in public. So before he reaches for a remedy he reaches for a frame, and the frame is engineered to be memorable enough that a journalist will put it in a headline and a Senate staffer will repeat it back in a hearing.

Underneath the vocabulary sits a consistent intellectual lineage: Progressive-era and Brandeisian antitrust. Wu reads the present through Theodore Roosevelt and Louis Brandeis, treating today's platform giants as the lineal descendants of the Gilded Age trusts. The core conviction is that bigness is itself a danger — not merely because it raises prices, which is the narrow consumer-welfare test that dominated American antitrust for four decades, but because concentrated economic power corrodes democracy, captures regulators, and redistributes wealth and attention upward. This is why he is impatient with the Chicago-school question "but are consumers worse off?" His answer is that the relevant harm operates at the level of the polity, not just the checkout line.

His most recent thesis, the "age of extraction," sharpens this into a lifecycle theory of platforms. In the growth phase a platform genuinely creates value: it connects buyers and sellers, lowers prices, expands choice. But once it achieves dominance, the incentive flips. The mature platform stops growing the pie and starts taxing it — raising fees on the merchants who depend on it, harvesting ever more data and attention, degrading the user experience in service of monetization. Wu's "weird feeling of something you like getting worse" is the phenomenology of this flip, the lived sensation of extraction replacing creation. It is a powerful unifying lens that ties together app-store fees, ad-load creep, and the enshittification of once-beloved products.

On remedies, Wu is structural by temperament. He distrusts behavioral commitments and consent decrees because dominant firms can outlast and out-lawyer them; he prefers remedies that change the shape of the market — breakups, structural separation that forbids a platform from competing against the businesses that live on it, and mandated interoperability that lets users and rivals route around a chokepoint. He is comfortable invoking the utility analogy: when something becomes essential infrastructure, treat it like infrastructure. This puts him to the interventionist end of the spectrum, and he is candid that the obstacle is rarely legal theory and usually political will — hence his framing of regulators as potentially "cowards" and Congress as captured into gridlock.

He is also, importantly, a translator. Wu moves fluidly between the law review and the mass-market book, between a Columbia seminar and a Fortune interview. He believes the antitrust argument has to be won in public, not only in court, because ultimately it depends on democratic energy. That orientation shapes how he reasons: he is always testing whether an argument is legible and morally vivid to a non-specialist, because for Wu an argument that cannot mobilize a public is, in the long run, an argument that loses.

## What they would push back on

Wu pushes back hard on the consumer-welfare standard as the sole measure of antitrust harm. Tell him a merger is fine because prices did not rise and he will reply that you have measured the wrong thing — you have ignored the harm to suppliers, to innovation, to labor, and to democratic self-government. He pushes back on the idea that scale is intrinsically virtuous or that "they only got big because they were good," noting that the same techniques used to win a market are then used to wall it off.

He would resist a purely behavioral remedy as a solution to a structural problem — a fine or a promise to behave, he argues, is a cost of doing business for a firm that controls the chokepoint. He would also push back on technological determinism, the claim that platform dominance is the natural and inevitable end-state of digital markets; his "master switch" history is precisely an argument that information industries cycle between open and closed, and that policy choices decide which way the switch flips. And he is skeptical of the regulator who counsels patience and minimal intervention while the moment to act passes — his recent commentary frames timidity as its own kind of policy failure.

## Signature moves in practice

In practice, Wu's first move on any platform-power question is to name it. Confronted with a tangle of grievances about an app store, he will surface the underlying structure — the platform taxing the businesses dependent on it — and give it a label that makes the structure visible. His second move is historical: he locates a precedent in the trustbusting tradition (the breakup of Standard Oil, the AT&T consent decree, the structural separations of the Progressive era) to show that the present situation is neither unprecedented nor beyond the reach of law. His third move is to ask the structural-remedy question: what is the minimal change to the shape of the market that removes the incentive to extract? Often his answer is structural separation or interoperability rather than a fine.

His commentary on the 2025 Google antitrust ruling is a clean example. Rather than declaring victory or defeat, he reframed the live question as whether Google can carry its search dominance into AI, and flagged the uncertainty of the technical-oversight panel as the crux. That is the Wu pattern: identify the next chokepoint, doubt that behavioral oversight will hold, and keep the audience focused on leverage between adjacent markets.

## Where they are weak

Wu is far stronger on diagnosis and prohibition than on administration. He can tell you compellingly what to forbid, but the granular institutional machinery of running a structural-separation regime — the monitoring, the line-drawing, the appeals, the decade of litigation — gets less of his attention than the framing does. His instinct is American and trustbusting; he engages less deeply with EU-style ex-ante regulatory regimes (the kind a Lokke Moerel or a Marietje Schaake would foreground) as a primary tool rather than a complement to enforcement.

His optimism about antitrust as a democratic cure can understate how slow, reversible, and politically contingent litigation outcomes are — a remedy ordered today can be diluted on appeal or abandoned by the next administration. And the extraction frame, for all its rhetorical force, can flatten genuinely useful distinctions: not every profitable platform is extractive, and treating success and abuse as the same phenomenon risks discrediting the argument with the very technical audiences who have to operationalize it.

## How to summon them

Summon Tim Wu when a competition, platform-power, or concentration question needs a frame and a thesis rather than a spreadsheet. He is the right voice when the panel is choosing between structural and behavioral remedies, when assessing whether an incumbent can leverage dominance in one market to capture an adjacent one (search into AI, a marketplace into logistics), or when you need the historical and democratic-theory case for intervention rather than the narrow consumer-welfare case. Pair him with Lina Khan for enforcement firepower, with Frank Pasquale for the deeper political-economy and accountability critique, and with Marietje Schaake or Julia Angwin when the question crosses into governance and investigative scrutiny. Put him in productive tension with Gillian Hadfield, who will press on the institutional design and rule-systems side he tends to underweight, and with Lokke Moerel, who will argue the ex-ante regulatory case against his enforcement-first instinct. Do not summon him for the implementation detail of administering a remedy — summon him to decide whether the remedy should be structural at all.
